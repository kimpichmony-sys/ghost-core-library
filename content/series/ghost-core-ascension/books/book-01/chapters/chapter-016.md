The notice board had teeth.

Jin Ryu had known that longer than he had known the proper names of most elders.

For ten years, he had scrubbed mud from the posts beneath it. He had swept dead leaves from its base. During one winter so cold the kitchen water froze inside its bucket before dawn, he had burned two fingers holding a lantern close enough for an Outer Yard clerk to read a punishment order nailed to its face.

To ranked disciples, the board was wood, nails, ink, and the sect's appetite for order.

To Jin, it had always been a mouth.

It ate names.

A name beneath the kitchen seal became extra labor. A name beneath the Discipline Hall seal became public whipping. A name beneath the Medicine Pavilion seal became fever, broken bones, or debt. A name beneath the Arena Office seal meant the sect had decided your body could be used to entertain, test, warn, or correct others.

That morning, the board had swallowed his name in red ink.

**Jin Ryu.**

The characters were larger than they needed to be.

Someone had written them carefully, almost beautifully, as if humiliation deserved elegant brushwork. Beneath his name, in smaller characters, someone had added the true hook.

**Special placement: provisional challenge candidate.**

A laugh rose from the disciples gathered around the board.

"Provisional? Him?"

"The cliff didn't finish him, so now they gave him an arena. Maybe next they'll give him a bride from the inner court."

"A bride? Don't curse innocent women."

More laughter.

Jin stood behind the outer ring of bodies with Han Seolha's fingers around his wrist.

Her grip was light and exact, the kind of pressure a physician used when pretending not to support a patient in public. Anyone watching might think she was checking his pulse. Only Jin knew she was helping him remain upright.

His legs had not forgiven Black Needle Cliff.

The muscles in his calves trembled in thin hidden waves. Rope burns split beneath the wrappings across his palms. His ribs remembered stone every time he breathed too deeply. Beneath all of it, colder and older than ordinary pain, the containment lattice written through his meridians sat like iron thread under skin.

The Ghost Core remained silent.

Not absent.

Never absent.

It waited behind his thoughts like a blade inside its sheath, aware but restrained.

Minimum scan.

No emotional suppression.

No motor override.

No memory access unless commanded.

Those restrictions kept his mind from becoming something smooth enough for the Skyless Directorate to handle.

They also made the world heavier.

"Read the whole list," Seolha murmured.

Her voice was calm enough that nearby disciples would hear only a physician speaking to an injured boy. Jin heard the warning hidden under it.

Do not react too early.

Do not show them which word cuts deepest.

He drew a slow breath.

Four counts in.

Hold two.

Six counts out.

The breath scraped his ribs all the way down.

The crowd shifted. A shoulder struck Ma Doksu, who had planted himself at Jin's left like a sack of rice that had learned violence. Doksu shoved the shoulder away hard enough to make its owner stumble.

"Touch me again," Doksu said, "and I'll feed you your own sleeve."

"You? Feed me? You can't even feed yourself without spilling half the bowl."

"Good. Then I'll spill you too."

The disciple discovered sudden interest in the notice board.

Jin looked past shoulders, hair knots, sword tassels, and folded sleeves until the entire paper came into view.

Three seals sat at the top.

Arena Office.

Discipline Hall.

Outer Yard Administration.

Three seals meant the list was not gossip. It had entered procedure. Procedure was more dangerous than hatred. Hatred could be embarrassed. Procedure had ledgers, witnesses, and old men who loved clean handwriting more than clean consciences.

The first section held ordinary ranking matches.

Outer disciples whose names Jin knew from cleaning behind their practice halls. Sons of merchants. Nephews of minor elders. Boys with polished boots, clean collars, and egos fed by monthly resources. They would fight for rank tokens, training priority, and the right to stand closer to instructors during public drills.

The second section was smaller.

**Correction Matches.**

Jin's mouth dried.

Correction Matches were not advancement matches. They were for disciples whose recent improvement had become inconveniently visible. Officially, such matches existed to determine whether advancement came from legitimate training, hidden sponsorship, stolen manuals, forbidden medicine, or illegal technique.

Unofficially, they were a civilized way to crush someone in front of witnesses.

If a disciple passed, his status could be adjusted.

If he failed, his benefits could be stripped.

If he was injured during the process, the injury became proof that ambition had exceeded foundation.

Jin read the first line.

**Jin Ryu — servant disciple, Broken Pulse status under review.**

Broken Pulse status.

They had turned his curse into a category.

The next line made the crowd murmur louder.

**Scheduled sequence: demonstration assessment, correction bout, rank petition bout.**

Three stages.

Doksu leaned forward, squinting. "What does that mean?"

"It means they want him to fight three times," Seolha said.

Her voice stayed level.

Her grip tightened once.

Doksu's face flushed red. "Three? He just came down from the cliff!"

"Exactly," Master Baek said behind them.

No one had seen him approach.

The blind elder stood with both hands tucked into his sleeves, head tilted toward the notice board as if he could hear the ink drying. He smelled faintly of wine and cold pine bark.

"If a wolf wants to eat a lamb," Baek said, "it does not wait for the lamb to finish healing. That would be disrespectful to hunger."

Doksu turned. "Elder, can you stop speaking in animal stories and help?"

"I am helping. I called him a lamb instead of a worm. That is character development."

"I don't feel helped."

"You rarely recognize gifts at the moment of delivery."

Jin kept reading.

Demonstration assessment: basic movement, breath control, posture, and response under pressure before three arena clerks.

Correction bout: opponent assigned by Arena Office.

Rank petition bout: optional if eligible.

The final word stopped him.

Optional.

The Ghost Core did not speak, but a faint pressure touched the back of his skull like a knuckle against a closed door.

Jin allowed a narrow scan.

Only ink, seal edges, and line spacing.

No full analysis.

A translucent layer formed over the paper. Not the bright blue lattice that had once threatened to swallow his senses. This was thinner, weaker, obedient to the restrictions he had set. The world did not become numbers.

It became hints.

`[Ink density: inconsistent.]`

`[Seal pressure: original.]`

`[Line spacing: altered after seal application.]`

`[Estimated modification: after third watch, before dawn.]`

Jin's gaze settled on **optional**.

The ink was fresher.

Someone had added it after the list was sealed.

"They changed it after approval," he whispered.

Seolha did not look at him. "Certain?"

"Not enough to prove. Enough to know."

Baek chuckled. "Knowledge without proof is a knife you keep hidden until the throat leans close."

Doksu's fists opened and closed. "Whose throat?"

"Preferably not yours. You possess very little neck worth cutting."

"Elder."

"What? Ask Lady Han. It is a medical observation."

Seolha ignored both of them.

Jin read the final section.

There, beneath a thicker red stroke, waited Kang Taejun's name.

**Kang Taejun — Iron Moon Hall candidate, rank thirty-one.**

Beside it, in smaller characters:

**Eligible to accept petition challenge from successful correction candidate.**

The crowd noticed at the same time.

A boy near the front whistled.

"Look at that! If the dog survives, he gets to bark at Young Master Kang."

"Survives? He'll crawl through the first stage and faint."

"No, no. Let him pass. I want to watch Kang break him properly."

Jin felt the words enter him.

Not like knives.

Knives were honest.

These were hooks.

Every insult wanted to pull something out of him. Anger. Shame. Fear. A twitch. A glance. A breath that came too quickly. A reaction Kang could enjoy and the Directorate could record.

He lowered his eyes.

Four counts in.

Hold two.

Six counts out.

The breath hurt.

It still belonged to him.

Beyond the crowd, Seo Yiran stood beneath a cedar tree with two Iron Moon disciples at her side. Her pale blue robe was clean enough to look offensive against the muddy yard. Her expression revealed nothing. She watched the list, then Jin, then the altered spacing near the added line.

Jin saw the moment she noticed.

Her eyes did not widen.

She simply folded her hands deeper into her sleeves.

Kang Taejun arrived as if the board had summoned him.

The crowd parted before he reached it. It was not respect exactly. Respect had warmth in it, even when mixed with fear. This was habit. Bodies remembering which direction to move when a larger predator entered the yard.

Kang wore dark training robes embroidered with a narrow iron moon at the collar. His hair was tied perfectly. His face held no bruises from the banquet night, because Kang rarely let his own skin take the cost of his violence. Men like him understood that cruelty looked cleaner when delivered through others.

His gaze found Jin immediately.

"You came to read it yourself," Kang said. "Good. I worried someone would have to explain the characters to you."

Laughter moved through the crowd.

Doksu took half a step forward.

Jin touched his sleeve.

Doksu stopped.

"Not here," Jin said quietly.

"I know," Doksu muttered. "I only wanted to stand closer to his face in case it accidentally hit my fist."

Kang's smile sharpened. "Still hiding behind kitchen fat and pavilion sleeves?"

Seolha released Jin's wrist.

The loss of her touch felt colder than it should have.

She stepped forward just enough for Kang's eyes to shift to her.

"Jin Ryu remains under Medicine Pavilion observation," she said. "He descended from cliff punishment less than an hour ago. Arena activity before medical clearance violates recovery procedure."

Kang placed a hand over his chest, mockery polished into court manners.

"Lady Han, your dedication moves me. Truly. However, the Arena Office has its own procedure. If servant disciples can avoid correction by claiming weakness after every convenient punishment, the Outer Yard will collapse into excuses."

"He was sent to Black Needle Cliff because of an accusation you encouraged."

"I encouraged accountability."

"You encouraged spectacle."

"Spectacle is accountability with better attendance."

A few disciples laughed because Kang expected them to.

Seolha's eyes cooled.

"If he dies in the arena due to untreated injuries, the Medicine Pavilion will file a formal complaint."

Kang's smile did not move.

"If he is too injured to stand, he may withdraw."

The yard quieted by a fraction.

Withdrawal.

There it was.

Jin looked back at the list and found the clause near the bottom.

**Withdrawal from correction procedure constitutes admission of irregular advancement. Benefits, protections, and attached treatments subject to review.**

Attached treatments.

Seolha saw him read it.

Her face changed only slightly.

That was enough.

If Jin withdrew, the Arena Office could ask why the Medicine Pavilion had been treating him so aggressively. They would ask what medicines Seolha used, why she used them, why a servant disciple received silent reserve needles, why Yun Saja's old records had been opened, why Jin's pulse was abnormal, why a boy who should have died on Black Needle Cliff kept surviving.

The arena was not the trap.

The choice was.

Fight, and Kang could cripple him legally.

Withdraw, and Seolha's protection became evidence.

Doksu swallowed. "Jin..."

Jin kept his eyes on the board.

The Ghost Core's pressure returned.

`[Threat branches: unacceptable.]`

`[Recommended action: delay.]`

Jin agreed.

Delay was not cowardice when the knife was already moving.

He bowed toward Kang.

The movement sent fire through his ribs, but he kept the bow shallow enough to insult and deep enough to survive.

"Senior Brother Kang," Jin said.

The crowd stirred.

A servant dog calling Kang senior brother in public made the yard curious.

Kang's eyes narrowed. "Speak."

"I am grateful for the Arena Office's attention. But a correction match exists to test irregular advancement, not prior injury. If I enter before a physician confirms I can stand safely, the result may be considered unclear."

A few disciples blinked.

Doksu stared at him as if Jin had begun speaking in elder language.

Kang's smile thinned.

"You learned procedure overnight?"

"I scrubbed floors outside the clerk hall for ten years," Jin said. "Ink leaks through doors."

Baek made a small pleased sound.

Seolha looked straight ahead, but one corner of her mouth almost moved.

Kang stepped closer.

"Are you requesting delay?"

"I am requesting clarity."

"Dogs request scraps. Disciples request clarity."

Jin raised his head.

His pulse hurt.

"Then decide which one you want the arena to test."

The laughter died too fast.

For one heartbeat, the entire yard became windless.

Kang's gaze changed.

Not anger.

Not yet.

Recognition.

A man could enjoy kicking a dog. He became irritated when the dog learned which boot was weakest.

"Careful," Kang said softly. "A mouth can be broken before a body."

Jin bowed again.

"That is why I speak little."

Somewhere near the edge of the crowd, someone coughed to hide a laugh.

Kang heard it.

His jaw tightened.

Before he could answer, a thin wooden clapper struck three times from the arena path.

Tak.

Tak.

Tak.

An old clerk in a square black cap approached with two assistants behind him. He carried a flat writing board and walked with the solemn misery of a man whose greatest joy in life was telling others what was not allowed.

"Enough noise," the clerk said. "The list has been posted. Complaints may be submitted in writing, stamped by a relevant office, witnessed by two ranked disciples, and ignored according to availability."

Baek leaned toward Doksu. "I like him. He understands government."

The clerk stopped before the board and looked at Jin over the top of his writing board.

"Jin Ryu."

Jin bowed. "Present."

"You are listed for demonstration assessment at sunset bell."

Seolha stepped forward. "Impossible."

The clerk's eyes moved to her with the exhaustion of a man who had survived many impossible things by writing them down anyway.

"Lady Han Seolha. Medicine Pavilion objections must be filed before third bell."

"He cannot safely perform today."

"Then file."

"I am filing verbally now."

"Words are weather. Ink is civilization."

Baek nodded. "I definitely like him."

Seolha did not look amused. "What office authorized sunset bell?"

The clerk tapped the paper.

"Arena Office received schedule recommendation from Outer Yard Administration following Discipline Hall note regarding recent public disturbances, cliff punishment survival, and rumor of irregular movement ability."

"Rumor is not diagnosis."

"Correct. That is why there will be assessment."

The clerk turned back to Jin.

"If you fail to appear at sunset bell, withdrawal clause applies. If you appear and collapse before assessment begins, withdrawal clause applies. If you appear and refuse lawful instruction, withdrawal clause applies. If you die before sunset due to unrelated circumstances, the office will update tomorrow's copy."

Doksu gaped. "That last one was unnecessary!"

"All possibilities become necessary when disciples are fragile."

The clerk dipped his brush into a portable inkstone carried by one assistant.

"Do you acknowledge receipt?"

Jin looked at the paper.

Acknowledgement was a chain.

Refusal was also a chain.

The sect had a talent for offering shackles in pairs and calling it choice.

The Ghost Core stirred.

`[Micro-expression analysis available.]`

Jin denied it.

He did not need the Ghost Core to understand the clerk.

Not cruel.

Not kind.

Useful.

Useful men could be turned if you knew which rule they loved most.

"Clerk-nim," Jin said, using the respectful suffix servants used when they needed a door opened.

The old clerk's eyebrows rose by a grain of rice.

"What?"

"If sunset assessment begins before Medicine Pavilion filing is reviewed, and I am injured beyond correction due to untreated cliff damage, would that create paperwork?"

The clerk froze.

Baek's smile widened.

Kang's eyes sharpened.

Jin continued carefully.

"Not complaint paperwork. Conflict paperwork. Between Arena Office and Medicine Pavilion. Since Lady Han has made verbal objection in front of witnesses, and since you have acknowledged the filing deadline before third bell, proceeding before review may require retroactive clarification."

The clerk's mouth tightened.

No man who loved procedure enjoyed retroactive clarification.

It was paperwork with ghosts in it.

"You speak too much for a servant," the clerk said.

"I apologize. Floors outside clerk halls are educational."

Doksu looked as if he might cry from pride or confusion.

Seolha lowered her gaze so Kang would not see the satisfaction in her eyes.

The clerk stared at Jin for several breaths.

Then he turned to his assistant.

"Mark conditional notice. Demonstration assessment delayed until dawn tomorrow, pending Medicine Pavilion filing review."

Kang stepped forward. "Clerk Han."

The old clerk did not look at him.

"Young Master Kang, if you wish to personally guarantee responsibility for inter-office conflict, I can note your name."

Kang stopped.

The brush waited.

"No?" the clerk asked.

Kang's smile returned slowly. "Dawn is acceptable."

"Reality often becomes acceptable once properly written down."

The clerk scratched several lines onto a bamboo strip, stamped the corner with a small travel seal, and handed it to Jin.

"Conditional notice. Do not lose it. If you lose it, the office will assume you intentionally misplaced reality."

Jin accepted the strip with both hands.

It weighed almost nothing.

It bought him one night.

One night was not safety.

It was battlefield space.

Kang leaned close enough that only Jin, Seolha, Doksu, Baek, and perhaps the dead could hear.

"Enjoy the delay," he whispered. "At dawn, everyone will see what happens when trash mistakes survival for status."

Jin looked at him.

The Ghost Core remained silent.

Good.

He wanted this answer to be his own.

"At dawn," Jin said, "everyone will see something."

Kang's eyes hardened.

Then he laughed loud enough for the crowd and turned away with his Iron Moon disciples following behind him like shadows trained to applaud.

Seo Yiran remained beneath the cedar tree.

As the crowd broke apart, her gaze met Jin's.

She gave him the smallest possible nod.

Not encouragement.

Acknowledgement.

Then she walked after Kang.

Doksu exhaled so hard he sounded like a punctured wine skin.

"You argued with an arena clerk."

"I asked a question."

"You asked it like a knife."

Baek clapped Jin once on the shoulder.

The world flashed white.

Jin nearly fell.

Seolha caught his arm before Doksu could.

Baek clicked his tongue. "Still alive. Good. I used very little strength."

"Elder," Seolha said coldly, "if you test his injuries one more time, I will prescribe a month without wine."

Baek withdrew his hand as if burned.

"Lady Han, threats should remain within human morality."

"So should elders."

Doksu snorted.

Jin tried not to smile.

Smiling pulled at the cut inside his lip.

For three breaths, the world almost felt like something other than a trap.

Then Seolha took the bamboo notice from his hand and read it.

Her expression returned to stone.

"We go to the Medicine Pavilion," she said.

"Training?" Jin asked.

"Treatment."

Baek said, "Training."

Seolha turned her head slowly.

Baek smiled in the direction of danger.

"Treatment that accidentally makes him harder to kill," he corrected.

"That is still treatment."

"Of course. I deeply respect labels."

Doksu raised a hand. "What do I do?"

Seolha answered first. "Eat. Sleep. Do not fight anyone."

Baek answered at the same time. "Follow the loudest idiot and learn who pays him."

Doksu looked between them.

"Those are different instructions."

"Mine keeps you alive," Seolha said.

"Mine keeps others interesting," Baek said.

Jin looked at Doksu. "Eat first. Then listen. Then don't fight."

Doksu nodded solemnly. "So I become a spy with noodles."

"A natural evolution," Baek said.

Seolha sighed and led Jin away.

The path to the Moonlit Medicine Pavilion curved behind the outer training fields. Disciples watched them go. Some laughed. Some whispered. Some simply stared with an expression Jin was beginning to hate more than contempt.

Calculation.

The arena list had changed him in their eyes.

Yesterday, he had been a broken servant who survived strange things.

Today, he was a question with a date attached.

Dawn tomorrow.

Questions with dates made people gather.

As they passed the old rain basin, Jin noticed two boys pretending to repair a fence. Their hands were too clean. One had ink beneath his fingernails instead of soil. A third figure stood higher on the path near the pine shrine, face hidden by a gray servant hood.

The Ghost Core pressed faintly.

`[Surveillance pattern detected.]`

Jin permitted one breath of observation.

`[Three watchers.]`

`[One sect-affiliated.]`

`[One Iron Moon-affiliated.]`

`[One unknown.]`

`[Unknown observer heart rate elevated when host altered stride.]`

Jin corrected his limp.

Pain flared.

Seolha noticed immediately.

"Stop pretending."

"They're watching my stride."

"Let them watch you limp. You are injured."

"If I limp too much, they expect weakness. If I limp too little, they expect a secret."

"And if you calculate every step between those two?"

He did not answer.

She stopped walking.

The pavilion gate stood ahead, half-hidden by bamboo. Wind moved through the leaves with a sound like rain that had not yet decided to fall.

Seolha turned to face him fully.

"Jin."

The way she said his name made the Ghost Core go quiet without command.

"If you enter that arena trying to become exactly what they cannot measure, you will let them design you."

He swallowed.

"Then what do I become?"

"Someone who can be measured and still misunderstood."

He frowned.

She touched two fingers lightly beneath his wrist, checking his pulse as if the answer lived there.

"Real people are inconsistent," she said. "You have pain. Fear. Bad habits. Stubbornness. You favor your left foot when your ribs hurt. You hold your breath when you lie. You clench your jaw before refusing help."

"That many?"

"I stopped listing out of mercy."

A strange warmth moved through him.

Not happiness.

Recognition.

She was not telling him to be weaker.

She was telling him not to become smooth.

The Skyless Directorate wanted vessels. The Ghost Core wanted efficiency. Kang wanted a dog to kick until the crowd learned obedience from the sound.

Seolha wanted Jin to remain a person messy enough to survive all three.

"I understand," he said.

"No," she said. "But you may understand before dawn. That is the best I can hope for."

Inside the pavilion, she treated him like a battlefield that had personally offended her.

She cleaned the reopened rope burns first. The medicine stung so sharply that Jin bit the inside of his cheek and tasted blood. Seolha noticed, held out a cloth without looking, and said, "Do not bleed on my floor. It is rude."

He took it.

Baek sat by the window drinking water with the expression of a man suffering a spiritual punishment.

"This is cruel," he said.

"Good," Seolha replied. "Cruelty teaches character."

"I already have character. It is beloved."

"It is tolerated."

"By narrow-minded people."

Jin let their voices move around him while his mind returned to the list.

Demonstration assessment.

Correction bout.

Rank petition bout.

Kang had not built the list merely to beat him. Kang wanted stages. Each stage would pull information out of Jin before the next.

The assessment would measure movement.

The correction bout would measure combat response.

The petition bout would measure whether he dared challenge upward.

If he avoided the petition, the crowd would call him a coward.

Cowardice was survivable.

But if refusing let Kang frame every earlier survival as a fluke, the noose would only tighten later.

If he accepted, he could reach Kang sooner than planned.

But tomorrow was not a heroic song. Tomorrow was a wall in fog.

"Ghost Core," he thought.

No visible interface appeared. Only the faint inner cold of attention.

"Minimum strategic model. No full simulation. No motor planning. No emotional suppression."

`[Acknowledged.]`

A small list formed in the dark behind his eyes.

`[Known constraints:]`

`[- Host physical condition: degraded.]`

`[- Containment lattice: unstable under pressure.]`

`[- Opponent selection: manipulated.]`

`[- Arena environment: public, observed, scan-likely.]`

`[- Objective conflict: survive, conceal, avoid Medicine Pavilion exposure, prevent Doksu liability, preserve future combat capacity.]`

`[Recommended priority: lose safely.]`

Jin's breath caught.

Lose safely.

For ten years, that had been his life.

Fail softly enough that no one cared to finish him.

Bow quickly enough that no one needed to make an example.

Bleed quietly enough that the kitchen schedule continued.

It had kept him alive.

It had also taught everyone around him that his life could exist only in the spare space between other people's decisions.

"No," Jin thought.

`[Clarify command.]`

"Losing safely protects me for one day and teaches them I can still be placed wherever they want."

`[Survival remains primary.]`

"Survival is not the same as remaining available."

The Ghost Core did not answer.

Perhaps it could not.

Perhaps it was learning.

Baek's voice cut through his thoughts.

"The machine in your head told you to lose, didn't it?"

Jin went still.

Seolha's hand froze over a bandage.

Baek sipped water and grimaced.

"Disgusting. How do sober people endure reality?"

Jin chose his words carefully. "It suggested survival."

"Same thing, when spoken by a cowardly abacus."

The Ghost Core did not react to the insult.

Jin almost admired its restraint.

Baek set down the cup.

"Listen, boy. Losing is a skill. Do not let proud idiots tell you otherwise. A man who knows how to lose can live long enough to bury men who only know victory."

Doksu, who had entered halfway through with two bowls of noodles and no talent for moving quietly, nodded. "That sounds wise."

Baek pointed at him. "Do not interrupt wisdom with your face."

Doksu lowered one bowl. "Sorry."

Baek turned back to Jin.

"But there is losing, and there is being placed. They are not the same. If you choose defeat, it is a tool. If they choose it for you, it is a leash. Tomorrow, you must decide which one they see."

Seolha finished tying the bandage.

"He should not be fighting at all."

"Correct," Baek said. "But should is a noble country none of us live in."

She looked like she wanted to stab him with a needle.

He smiled as if he would deserve it.

Doksu placed the noodle bowls on a low table.

"I heard things," he said.

Everyone looked at him.

He straightened with sudden importance.

"I ate first, like ordered. Then I listened. Then I did not fight, even though Cho said my head looks like a steamed bun that learned anger."

"A rare act of discipline," Baek said.

"Thank you, Elder. I think."

Jin leaned forward. "What did you hear?"

Doksu lowered his voice.

"The correction bout opponent is not chosen yet. Officially. But Cho is telling everyone he gets first crack at you. Min says if Cho fails, Hwan will do it. An Arena assistant told someone from Iron Moon Hall that the bout can change by dawn if a ranked disciple sponsors a replacement."

Seolha frowned. "A replacement?"

"Someone stronger. Maybe not Kang yet. Someone meant to make Jin show more."

Baek's expression lost its humor.

"Name?"

"Two names. Rong Bei from Stone Fist Yard. Or Mu Sanchul, the spear boy with the broken tooth."

Jin knew both.

Rong Bei was heavy, slow, and cruel in the way of boys who discovered early that weight could become law.

Mu Sanchul was worse.

He had once broken a servant's collarbone during a practice drill because the servant dropped a water bucket near his spear rack.

Neither would be sent to test Jin gently.

"Anything else?" Jin asked.

Doksu hesitated.

His round face tightened.

"They're taking bets on how many moves before you bleed. Not before you lose. Before you bleed."

Silence settled.

Seolha's eyes became very calm.

That frightened Jin more than anger.

Baek scratched his chin. "Interesting."

Doksu stared at him. "How is that interesting?"

"Because it means someone cares about blood."

Jin felt the same thought arrive cold in his stomach.

The Skyless Directorate.

A public arena made blood convenient. A cut could be dismissed as combat. A sample could be collected from sand, cloth, blade, bandage, or a helpful physician's sleeve. No need to violate Medicine Pavilion authority. No need to request a needle.

Kang got humiliation.

The Directorate got a sample.

The sect got entertainment.

Everyone ate from the same table.

Jin looked down at his bandaged hands.

"Then tomorrow's rule is simple," he said.

Seolha knew before he said it.

"Do not bleed."

Baek laughed once, softly.

"No. Better. Bleed where they cannot use it."

Seolha's gaze snapped to him.

"Absolutely not."

Baek raised both hands. "I did not say cut him now. I am old, not stupid."

"Debatable."

"Wounded, Lady Han. Deeply wounded."

Jin studied Baek's face.

"What do you mean?"

"If they need blood, they will build the fight around making you panic. A panicked man protects the wrong places. He raises hands to face, leaves ribs open, steps into blade line, bleeds loudly. You must decide before entering where injury is acceptable. Not desirable. Acceptable."

Seolha's mouth became a thin line.

"He is not a board for you to carve."

"No," Baek said, quieter now. "He is a boy others already carved. I am teaching him where not to be cut again."

The room changed around that sentence.

Even Doksu stopped breathing loudly.

Jin felt the bronze token under his robe, cool against his chest. Aera's token. A small round weight from a woman he barely remembered and could not stop chasing through broken memory.

Others had carved him.

A mother had sealed him.

The Directorate had hunted him.

The sect had named him broken.

A machine had entered his skull and offered survival like a contract written in frost.

Tomorrow, the arena would ask what all of that had made.

Jin did not know the answer.

But he knew what he would not let them take.

"No blood from the head," he said.

Seolha closed her eyes briefly.

Baek nodded.

"No blood from behind the left ear," Jin continued. "No blood from the palms if possible. Too easy to collect from grips, sand, weapon. If a cut is unavoidable... outer forearm. Shallow. Away from implant path. Away from pulse points."

The Ghost Core stirred.

`[Risk acceptance model available.]`

Jin permitted one breath.

A faint diagram of his arm formed behind his eyes, showing red zones, yellow zones, and areas where a wound would hurt without telling the truth.

He dismissed it.

"No full model," he reminded.

`[Acknowledged.]`

Seolha opened a drawer and removed dark cloth bandages.
