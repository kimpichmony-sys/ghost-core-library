Jin Ryu woke to the smell of bitter herbs, old bandages, and rain-soaked wood.

For several breaths, he did not open his eyes.

He had survived too many mornings by pretending to sleep through them. A sleeping dog was easier to ignore than a standing one. A silent boy invited fewer kicks than a boy who looked awake enough to resent them. So his first instinct, even with fire crawling under his ribs and a needle of cold pain buried behind his left ear, was not to move.

It was to become small.

It was to become harmless.

Rain tapped against the shutters. Somewhere nearby, water dripped into a clay basin one patient drop at a time. Herbs dried from ceiling beams above him, giving the room a bitter, green smell that clung to the tongue. Beneath it was the iron scent of old blood and the sourness of bodies that had been opened, stitched, and ordered to continue living.

The Outer Yard infirmary.

Jin knew that smell.

Servant disciples came here only when a wound became too ugly to hide, or when a senior disciple needed a physician's mark proving that punishment had not crossed into murder. No one wasted medicine on broken tools unless the break might embarrass someone with a name.

For a fragile moment, Jin tried to believe the rest had been fever.

There had been no black door beneath the mountain.

No chamber wider than any hall the Obsidian Meridian Sect admitted to owning.

No starless furnace breathing blue light over a pit that had no bottom.

No impossible voice speaking inside his skull.

No needle of ancient metal crawling into the flesh behind his left ear as if his body had been waiting for it since birth.

A dream, he told himself.

Pain, fear, hunger, and blood loss could make a boy see anything.

He held that lie with both hands.

Then pale blue text opened behind his closed eyelids.

`[Consciousness restored.]`

Jin's heart slammed against his ribs.

Pain answered immediately.

It rose from everywhere at once. From his left forearm, where splintered heat pulsed under tight wrappings. From his ribs, where each breath felt like a thin blade being negotiated past bone. From his shoulders and back, where old bruises lay under new ones like stacked ink. From the place behind his left ear, where something cold and precise beat out of rhythm with his heart.

Not a wound.

Not exactly.

A second pulse.

`[Emergency stabilization incomplete.]`

`[Warning: abrupt movement may worsen thoracic fracture.]`

Jin opened his eyes.

The ceiling above him was low and smoke-darkened. Wooden beams crossed it like tired ribs. Bundles of silverleaf, bitterroot, and black-vein mint hung upside down by red thread, swaying in the weak gray light that leaked through the shutters. A medicine lamp burned in the corner, its flame small and blue at the base.

He lay on a narrow cot with one shortened leg. A thin blanket covered him to the waist. His ribs had been bound so tightly that breathing felt borrowed from someone who might demand it back. His left arm rested stiffly across his stomach inside a splint. The fingers were swollen purple around the knuckles.

Someone had washed the blood from his face.

Someone had not been gentle.

Jin swallowed. His throat scraped like dry sand.

`[Damage report available.]`

The words did not enter through his ears. They arrived cleanly, directly, without breath or vibration. That made them worse. A human voice could be doubted. A human voice could crack, hesitate, lie badly.

This one had no body to blame.

Jin's fingers dug into the blanket.

`[Primary injuries: two cracked ribs. Left ulna hairline fracture. Soft tissue trauma across shoulder and back. Neural inflammation surrounding implant site. Severe qi stagnation. Meridian obstruction across all primary channels.]`

Meridian obstruction.

The phrase struck harder than any staff.

He had heard healers speak around it for years. Poor vessel response. Weak channels. Closed gates. Broken pulse. All the soft ways adults named a life they had already decided could not climb.

But the voice had not said weak.

It had not said natural.

It had said obstruction.

Jin tried to sit up.

The world punished him with perfect efficiency.

Pain stabbed beneath his ribs. His vision flashed white. His breath vanished halfway into his chest and refused to move. His right hand clawed at the cot frame while his left arm throbbed inside the splint.

A wooden cup clicked against a table.

“Do that again,” a woman's voice said from beside the medicine shelves, “and I will let you break yourself properly. It may teach you faster than I can.”

Jin froze.

Slowly, carefully, he turned his head.

Han Seolha stood beneath a rack of drying silverleaf, grinding medicine in a stone bowl. Her robes were white with pale blue lining, clean in a room that made cleanliness feel like arrogance. Her black hair had been pinned with a single silver needle. She was eighteen, only two years older than Jin, but even outer disciples twice her size lowered their voices when she passed.

Some called her Senior Sister Han.

Others called her the Ice Needle Witch when they thought she could not hear.

Jin suspected she heard every time. He also suspected she allowed the name to live because fear was useful medicine.

Her face was calm. Not gentle. Not cruel. Calm in the way a blade was calm before it decided where to cut.

Jin forced his head down in the shallowest bow his ribs allowed.

“Senior Sister Han.”

His voice sounded like broken bark.

Seolha watched him for a breath, then went back to grinding the dark green paste.

“You remember your manners. That is disappointing. I had begun to wonder if your head injury had improved your personality.”

Jin did not know how to answer.

So he said nothing.

The pestle moved in slow circles.

“Name.”

Jin blinked. “My name?”

“No. The name of the Emperor's favorite horse.”

“Jin Ryu.”

“Age.”

“Sixteen.”

“Outer Yard status.”

“Servant disciple.”

“Last clear memory before losing consciousness.”

The black door opened behind his thoughts.

Blue light.

Stone teeth.

Left Limp screaming.

His mother's voice saying a name he had not allowed himself to remember.

Ryu.

Then the Ghost Core, cold and exact, threading itself into the wound behind his ear.

Jin lowered his gaze to the blanket.

“I was running.”

The pestle stopped.

“From?”

“Kang Taejun's men.”

“That part is not difficult to believe.”

He said nothing.

“After that?”

“I fell.”

Seolha lifted her eyes.

The room seemed to become quieter around that single movement.

“You fell.”

“Yes.”

“Down a cliff?”

“No.”

“Into a tiger's mouth?”

“No.”

“Through a roof? Into a grinding mill? Beneath a cart full of iron cauldrons?”

Jin pressed his lips together.

Seolha set the pestle down.

“You have two cracked ribs, a fractured arm, qi backlash without enough qi to justify the backlash, cold-metal residue around a puncture behind your ear, and dust in your clothing that does not match any tunnel recorded beneath the Outer Yard. You were found unconscious near a forbidden storage passage that Discipline Hall insists has been sealed for ten years.”

Jin looked up before he could stop himself.

“Who found me?”

“Master Baek.”

The name moved through the room like the smell of old wine.

Blind Baek.

The retired elder who slept in abandoned courtyards, drank from gourds that never seemed empty, and laughed at things nobody else understood. Half the sect called him a drunk. The other half called him a ghost who had forgotten to die. Jin had seen him only at a distance, usually leaning against a pillar with clouded eyes and a mouth curved as if the world were telling him a private joke.

“Master Baek brought me here?”

“Dragged would be more accurate.”

“Why?”

“That is a question for him.” Seolha wiped medicine from the pestle with a strip of cloth. “When I asked, he said, 'Dead dogs attract flies. Breathing dogs attract trouble. Trouble is more amusing.' Then he fell asleep in my doorway.”

For one dangerous moment, Jin almost laughed.

The sound never escaped.

`[Social variable: Baek Chun identified as anomalous protector.]`

Jin's eyes sharpened.

Seolha noticed.

Of course she noticed.

Her gaze moved from his face to the place behind his left ear. Nothing in her expression changed, but something in the air did.

“Does it hurt?” she asked.

Jin's hand twitched toward the wound before he forced it still.

“What?”

“The puncture behind your ear.”

“A little.”

“A little.”

“Yes.”

Seolha picked up a silver needle from the table. It was longer than his smallest finger and thinner than rain.

“Lies are easier to swallow when they are not insulting.”

Jin said nothing.

She crossed the room and stood beside the cot. Up close, he could smell wintermint on her sleeves and sleeplessness beneath it. Her eyes were dark, steady, and far too awake.

“Turn your head.”

Jin obeyed.

The movement pulled at his ribs and made his teeth lock together.

Cool fingers brushed the hair away from his left ear.

The Ghost Core went silent.

Too silent.

Seolha inhaled once, very softly.

Jin could not see her face, but he felt the pause.

“What is it?” he asked.

“Do not move.”

The silver needle touched the skin behind his ear.

Not pierced. Only touched.

The Ghost Core reacted like a snake waking under a stone.

`[Foreign diagnostic contact detected.]`

`[Threat level: uncertain.]`

`[Concealment recommended.]`

Jin's breath shortened.

No.

Not now.

Not in front of her.

Seolha's needle slid a hairbreadth lower.

Pain flashed behind his eye, bright and clean.

At the same instant, her fingers tightened in his hair.

“There,” she whispered.

Jin's throat dried.

“What?”

She removed the needle and stared at its tip.

A speck of black dust clung to the silver.

Not blood.

Not scab.

Black dust, fine as ground ink, with a faint metallic sheen.

Seolha folded the needle into a square of white cloth without looking away from him.

“Your pulse has changed.”

Jin's heart gave a stupid, obvious jump.

“My pulse has always been bad.”

“No.”

One word. Cold enough to cut.

“Bad pulses are irregular. Weak pulses are thin. Blocked pulses stutter at the gates. Yours does all of those things.” She touched two fingers to his wrist, then two to the side of his throat. “And now there is something else underneath it.”

Jin felt every drop of rain against the shutter.

“A second pulse?” he asked before he could stop himself.

Seolha's gaze snapped to him.

The room held its breath.

`[Conversation risk elevated.]`

Jin wished he could tear the voice out with his nails.

Seolha released his wrist slowly.

“I did not say that.”

“No.”

“But you understood it.”

Jin said nothing.

Seolha stepped back and poured water from a clay jug into a cup. Her hands were steady. That frightened him more than shaking would have.

“Drink.”

He took the cup with his right hand. The water smelled faintly of licorice root. When it touched his tongue, bitterness spread down his throat and settled behind his ribs.

`[Unknown compound detected.]`

`[Toxicity: low.]`

`[Analgesic and anti-inflammatory properties probable.]`

`[Recommendation: consume.]`

Jin drank.

Seolha watched his throat move.

“Who taught you that phrase?” she asked.

“What phrase?”

“Second pulse.”

Jin set the cup down carefully.

“No one.”

“Better.”

He looked at her.

“That one was less insulting?”

“It was shorter.”

Something almost like amusement touched the corner of her mouth and vanished before it became human.

Then the infirmary door opened.

Jin turned too quickly.

Pain lanced his ribs. The Ghost Core filled his vision with warnings he did not have time to read.

A thick-shouldered man in dark brown Discipline Hall robes stepped inside, followed by a thin clerk carrying a writing board. The man's hair was tied in a tight knot, his beard trimmed to a hard line beneath his jaw. He looked at Jin the way officials looked at damaged property whose owner had not yet been confirmed.

“Han Seolha,” he said. “Discipline Hall requires the servant disciple's statement.”

Seolha did not bow.

The clerk noticed. The thick-shouldered man pretended not to.

“Elder Jang,” she said. “The patient is not fit for questioning.”

“He is fit to breathe.”

“Barely.”

“That is enough.”

Seolha moved between the cot and the door with the casual precision of a door sliding shut.

“The boy has cracked ribs, a fractured arm, neural inflammation, and signs of qi shock. If you force a formal statement now, I will record Discipline Hall as the cause of medical deterioration.”

Elder Jang's eyes narrowed.

“Careful.”

“I am being careful. That is why he is still alive.”

The clerk looked down very hard at his board.

Jang's gaze shifted past Seolha to Jin.

“Servant disciple Jin Ryu.”

Jin lowered his head.

“Yes, Elder.”

“You entered a forbidden passage.”

“I was chased.”

“By whom?”

Jin could have said Kang Taejun.

He could have said Cho, Min, and the others. He could have said they hunted him through the storage yard, laughed when he bled, and drove him into darkness like a rat.

The old Jin would have stayed silent because silence hurt less than naming people who could hurt him again.

The new thing behind his ear offered a colder reason.

`[Direct accusation against Kang Taejun may trigger retaliation before recovery.]`

`[Recommended response: partial truth.]`

Jin hated that the recommendation made sense.

“I did not see clearly,” he said. “It was dark.”

Elder Jang's mouth thinned.

“You expect Discipline Hall to believe that you ran into a sealed passage by accident.”

“No, Elder.” Jin forced the words through his dry throat. “I expect Discipline Hall to believe I was trying not to die.”

The clerk's brush paused.

Seolha did not turn around, but Jin saw one sleeve shift as if her hand had tightened inside it.

Jang stared at him.

Then he smiled without warmth.

“A servant disciple grows bold after one night in the infirmary.”

Jin lowered his eyes.

“Pain makes manners expensive.”

A dangerous silence followed.

The Ghost Core flashed three warnings at once.

`[Provocation detected.]`

`[Authority displeasure rising.]`

`[Recommend apology.]`

Jin swallowed the apology.

Seolha spoke first.

“If Discipline Hall wants a written statement, send the request through the Medicine Pavilion. I will answer when the patient can sit without risking collapse.”

“The passage is sealed again,” Jang said. “No door. No chamber. No evidence beyond broken stone and several injured disciples claiming the Broken Pulse Dog led them into danger.”

Jin's fingers went cold.

Several injured disciples.

So some of Kang's men had survived.

And they had already begun shaping the story.

Seolha's voice remained flat.

“Injured disciples often become creative when asked why they were injured.”

Jang looked at her for a long breath.

Then his gaze dropped to the cloth folded around the silver needle on her table.

Seolha reached for it before his eyes settled too long.

“Medicine waste,” she said.

“I did not ask.”

“No. You looked.”

The clerk stopped breathing.

Jang's smile vanished.

“This is not over.”

“No,” Seolha said. “It has barely begun.”

For a moment, Jin saw something move behind the elder's eyes. Not fear. Recognition. As if Seolha had used words from an old conversation he had hoped dead.

Then Jang turned and left.

The clerk followed so quickly he nearly struck the doorframe with his writing board.

Only after their steps faded did Jin let out the breath he had been holding.

Seolha turned back to him.

“That,” she said, “was stupid.”

“I know.”

“Then why do it?”

Jin stared at the blanket.

Because he was tired.

Because something ancient had opened behind his skull and told him truths with less kindness than his enemies.

Because he had nearly died saving Left Limp, and the first official words he heard afterward were still another attempt to make his suffering convenient for someone else's record.

Because for one moment, silence had tasted worse than punishment.

“I wanted to know if my voice still belonged to me,” he said.

Seolha did not answer immediately.

When she did, her voice was softer.

“That is an expensive thing to test.”

“Yes.”

She returned to the table, opened a small wooden drawer, and removed a folded strip of yellow paper. She wrote something on it in quick, sharp strokes.

“What is that?” Jin asked.

“An excuse.”

“For what?”

“For keeping you here until sunset.”

“I have duties.”

“You have cracked ribs.”

“The kitchens do not care.”

“I do.”

The words landed too plainly.

Jin looked at her.

Seolha's expression did not change, but she glanced away first.

“Do not misunderstand,” she said. “A dead patient creates paperwork.”

“Of course.”

“And I dislike paperwork.”

“Senior Sister's mercy is terrifying.”

“It should be.”

The almost-smile returned, then vanished again.

By late afternoon, fever had begun to chew at the edges of Jin's thoughts.

Seolha changed his bandages twice. Each time she inspected the wound behind his ear with the care of someone pretending not to be horrified. She did not ask again about the second pulse. Jin did not offer anything. Between them, the silence became less empty and more dangerous, like a covered blade laid on a table.

The Ghost Core remained mostly quiet.

When it did speak, it spoke in fragments.

`[Neural inflammation decreasing slowly.]`

`[Motor control unstable.]`

`[Concealment protocol partially active.]`

At that last line, Jin's stomach tightened.

Concealment from whom?

Before he could ask, Seolha pressed two fingers to his wrist.

The Ghost Core's text flickered.

`[External pulse evaluation detected.]`

`[Recommend stillness.]`

Jin became stone.

Seolha's fingers did not move.

“Again,” she murmured.

“What?”

“Nothing.”

Her lie was better than his.

Near sunset, she finally allowed him to stand.

Allowed was a generous word. She watched him drag himself upright with the expression of a physician observing a poorly designed experiment. His legs shook. His ribs protested. His left arm felt like it belonged to someone who hated him. The room tilted twice before agreeing to remain mostly where it was.

Seolha handed him a bamboo staff.

“Use this.”

“I can walk.”

“Yes. Badly.”

He took the staff.

The Ghost Core immediately overlaid faint lines across the floor.

`[Walking support acquired.]`

`[Recommended gait adjustment: reduce left-side load. Shorten stride by eight percent. Avoid sudden torso rotation.]`

Jin stared at the lines.

They looked like moonlight drawn over dirt.

“Stop,” he whispered.

Seolha looked up.

“What?”

“Nothing.”

Her eyes narrowed.

“Jin Ryu.”

He flinched at the full name.

No one in the Outer Yard used both pieces unless reading a punishment list.

“You will not show anything strange in front of Kang Taejun,” she said.

His hand tightened around the staff.

“I don't know what counts as strange anymore.”

“That is honest. Keep that sentence close.”

She stepped nearer and lowered her voice.

“You are weak. Injured. Frightened. Those things may save you. Let people believe them.”

“They are true.”

“Good lies often are.”

Jin met her eyes.

“Why are you helping me?”

For the first time all day, Seolha looked younger than eighteen.

Only for a heartbeat.

Then the Ice Needle Witch returned.

“Because your pulse should not exist.”

“That is not help.”

“It is curiosity.”

“That is worse.”

“Yes.”

She reached into her sleeve and pulled out a small packet wrapped in oiled paper.

“Three doses. One after sunset, one before sleep, one at dawn. They will reduce fever and make you less stupid with pain. Do not take more.”

“What happens if I take more?”

“You become very calm.”

“That sounds useful.”

“Permanently.”

Jin put the packet inside his robe with great care.

Seolha then handed him the yellow excuse slip.

“If anyone asks why you missed half your duties, show this.”

“Will they respect it?”

“No.”

“Then why give it?”

“So when they ignore it, I can become formally offended.”

Jin stared at her.

“That is a weapon?”

“Most paper is, if sharpened properly.”

For reasons he could not explain, that made him feel safer than the staff.

He bowed as deeply as his ribs allowed.

“Thank you, Senior Sister Han.”

Seolha's face remained unreadable.

“Survive until tomorrow before thanking me. Gratitude from corpses is difficult to verify.”

Jin left the infirmary through the side door as dusk pooled blue along the courtyard stones.

Every step hurt.

The staff clicked softly beside him. The Ghost Core offered corrections and then withdrew them when Jin clenched his teeth hard enough. He did not know whether the thing had obeyed him or merely found his resistance inefficient.

The Medicine Pavilion courtyard lay quiet behind him. Paper lanterns burned under curved eaves. Rainwater gathered in shallow grooves between stones. Beyond the gate, the Outer Yard waited with its smoke, hunger, and hierarchy.

Jin had crossed that yard thousands of times.

He had never felt it watching him before.

At the water trough, two kitchen boys stopped talking when they saw him. Near the firewood shed, an old servant woman spat once to the side—not at him, exactly, but not away from him either. On the training path, three outer disciples leaned together and whispered.

Broken Pulse Dog returned.

The forbidden tunnel did not eat him.

Kang's men came back bleeding.

He limps like a ghost.

Jin kept his head down.

`[Social attention elevated.]`

“I noticed,” he breathed.

`[Recommendation: reduce verbal self-response in public.]`

For a heartbeat, irritation burned hotter than pain.

Then a familiar voice rasped from beside the grain shed.

“Talking to yourself already? Good. Means you are still poor. Rich madmen have servants to answer back.”

Jin stopped.

Choi Deok sat on an overturned basket with one leg stretched stiffly in front of him. Everyone called him Left Limp, sometimes with pity, usually without. He was older than Jin by four years, broad through the shoulders, with a face that looked as if it had been assembled from stubbornness and old swelling. A strip of cloth wrapped his forehead. One eye had darkened purple.

He was alive.

Something in Jin's chest loosened so violently it hurt.

“Deok-hyung.”

Left Limp's mouth twisted.

“Do not use respectful words when you look like boiled trash. It makes the world feel unbalanced.”

Jin crossed the few steps between them, too slowly. Deok watched the staff, the splinted arm, the tight bandages under Jin's robe, and the way he avoided turning his torso.

“You look worse than me,” Deok said.

“You always say that.”

“Usually I lie to encourage you. Today I am being honest.”

Jin sat on the basket beside him because standing had become expensive.

For a moment neither spoke.

Then Deok lowered his voice.

“They said you led us into a sealed passage.”

“I didn't.”

“I know.”

Jin looked at him.

Deok scratched the side of his nose.

“You are many things. Stupid enough to run toward trouble, yes. Important enough to know where ancient sealed passages are hidden, no.”

Despite everything, Jin laughed once.

Pain punished him. He accepted it.

Deok's expression softened for half a breath before hardening again.

“Kang's dogs are angry.”

“They are always angry.”

“No. This is a different smell. Cho broke two fingers. Min lost a tooth. Hwan keeps saying the tunnel moved.”

Jin's blood chilled.

Deok leaned closer.

“They are saying you did something.”

“I was unconscious.”

“People do many suspicious things while unconscious. Snore. Drool. Survive when senior disciples expected them not to.”

Jin looked toward the training yard.

At the far edge, near the shadow of the weapon rack, Cho stood with one hand wrapped in thick cloth. Min was beside him, his lip swollen. Hwan hovered behind them with the pale look of a man who had seen stone open like teeth and wanted someone weaker to blame.

All three were watching Jin.

The Ghost Core became cold in his skull.

`[Hostile attention confirmed.]`

`[Threat actors: three.]`

`[Current host combat capacity: insufficient.]`

Jin almost smiled.

At least the voice was honest.

Cho pushed away from the rack first.

Deok muttered, “Of course. Heaven forbid dogs wait until after supper.”

Jin tightened his grip on the bamboo staff.

“Stay behind me,” Deok said.

“You can barely stand.”

“That makes two of us. But I am wider.”

Cho stopped a few paces away, Min and Hwan flanking him like badly trained shadows. His wrapped hand hung stiffly at his side. His eyes were red with sleeplessness and humiliation.

“You,” Cho said.

Jin lowered his head.

“Senior Brother Cho.”

“Do not senior brother me.”

“As Senior Brother wishes.”

Min spat near Jin's foot.

“Something happened in that tunnel.”

“Yes,” Jin said. “You chased me into it.”

Deok made a small choking sound that might have been admiration or terror.

Cho's face darkened.

“You think the Medicine Pavilion protects you?”

“No.” Jin lifted the yellow excuse slip with his good hand. “It mostly threatens paperwork.”

Hwan took half a step back before realizing he had done it.

Cho looked at the slip. He could not read all of it upside down, but he recognized the blue Medicine Pavilion mark.

His injured hand twitched.

“You got bold.”

“No,” Jin said. “Just tired.”

Cho's uninjured hand rose.

The Ghost Core threw lines across Jin's vision.

`[Incoming strike probable: right palm. Target: face. Evasion success probability: 18 percent. Guarding success probability: 22 percent. Pain escalation unavoidable.]`

Jin's body tensed.

Then a voice cut across the yard.

“Touch my patient and I will record the swelling pattern of your fingers in your throat.”

Everyone turned.

Han Seolha stood beneath the Medicine Pavilion gate, pale robes untouched by the mud, one hand hidden inside her sleeve.

She had followed him.

No—Jin corrected himself.

She had predicted this.

Cho's face shifted through anger, calculation, and resentment.

“Senior Sister Han,” he said tightly. “We were only speaking.”

“You speak with raised hands?”

“My fingers ache.”

“I can remove them. Then they will stop.”

Deok looked at the ground very hard. His shoulders shook once.

Cho lowered his hand.

Seolha's gaze moved across the three disciples and stopped on Hwan.

“You. If you vomit again from shock, do it outside the dining hall this time. Last night's mess was unnecessary.”

Hwan went white.

Min grabbed Cho's sleeve.

Cho stared at Jin one last time.

“This is not finished.”

“No,” Seolha said from the gate. “But it is paused.”

The three left.

Only when they were gone did Deok exhale.

“I take back everything bad I have ever said about Ice Needle Witch.”

Seolha's eyes moved to him.

Deok immediately added, “Respectfully. In my heart. Where no one can prove it.”

Jin did not know whether he wanted to laugh or collapse.

Seolha crossed the yard and stopped in front of him.

“You made it forty-seven steps before attracting trouble.”

“I think trouble was already waiting.”

“Then you walked into it efficiently.”

She checked his face, then his wrist, then the way he held the staff.

“Go to your quarters. Sleep. Speak to no one else.”

“Is that medical advice?” Jin asked.

“It is survival advice wearing medical robes.”

Deok pushed himself up with a grunt.

“I'll take him.”

Seolha studied him.

“You are injured.”

“Everyone here is injured. Some of us are simply louder about it.”

A brief silence passed between them. Something like approval, but not gentle, touched Seolha's face.

“Do not let him fall.”

Deok hooked one thick hand under Jin's good elbow.

“If he falls, I will fall under him. That is what wide men are for.”

Jin wanted to protest.

He did not have the strength.

The servant quarters smelled of damp straw, ash, and tired bodies. The room he shared with six others had walls thin enough to hear coughing from the next shed. His sleeping mat lay in the corner, rolled and tied with twine. No one had stolen it. That kindness, or oversight, almost undid him.

Deok helped him sit.

“Your face is doing that thing,” Deok said.

“What thing?”

“The thing where you pretend you are thinking deeply but actually you are trying not to faint.”

“I am thinking deeply about not fainting.”

“Good. Become a scholar.”

Jin's mouth twitched.

Deok crouched, reached under Jin's mat, and pulled out a small cloth bundle.

“I hid this before Kang's dogs searched.”

Jin stared.

“My things?”

“Your many treasures.” Deok opened the bundle. “One cracked comb. Two copper bits. A needle with no eye. A thread charm ugly enough to scare ghosts. Truly, the wealth of kings.”

Jin reached for the thread charm before he meant to.

It was faded red and black, knotted around a tiny bead of dark wood. His mother had tied it around his wrist when he was five. By the time he entered the sect, the thread had worn thin, so he hid it with his few belongings and touched it only on nights when hunger made him remember being held.

Aera.

He had not thought her name clearly in years.

Not because he had forgotten.

Because names hurt less when buried.

The moment his fingers touched the charm, the Ghost Core went still.

Then the world narrowed.

`[Pattern residue detected.]`

Jin's breath caught.

`[Nonstandard thread architecture.]`

`[Dormant authorization trace present.]`

`[Cross-reference: Skyless Mandate.]`

The words struck the inside of his skull like bells underwater.

A flash of blue-white light.

His mother's hand, thinner than he remembered, tying the charm around his wrist.

Her voice low and urgent.

“Never let them name you by what they want to use.”

Then another voice, deeper, not hers.

**Vessel candidate unstable. Seal him or lose him.**

Jin jerked back.

Pain flared. His ribs spasmed. The room tilted.

Deok grabbed him before he hit the wall.

“Jin!”

The Ghost Core flickered.

`[Memory resonance aborted.]`

`[Neural load increasing.]`

`[Recommendation: rest.]`

Jin laughed once, breathless and afraid.

“Now it recommends rest.”

Deok's grip tightened.

“What?”

Jin looked at him.

Deok's face was close, bruised, worried, and painfully ordinary. Not simple. Not stupid. Ordinary in the way a hand pulling you from mud was ordinary. Necessary. Human.

Jin closed his fingers around the charm.

“Nothing,” he said.

Deok stared at him for a long moment.

Then he sat back.

“You are lying badly.”

“Yes.”

“Are you going to tell me?”

Jin wanted to.

The wanting surprised him.

But he could still feel the black dust on Seolha's needle, Elder Jang's eyes, Cho's wrapped fingers, and the Ghost Core's cold warnings. Telling Deok everything would not make him safer. It would only give the world another place to cut.

“Not yet,” Jin said.

Deok looked at the floor.

The silence hurt more than accusation.

Finally, Deok nodded.

“Then tell me when lying starts killing you faster than truth.”

Jin swallowed.

“I will.”

“You better. I am too poor to mourn dramatically.”

Deok stood with a groan, limped to the doorway, and paused.

“One more thing.”

Jin looked up.

“Kang's people think something from the tunnel stuck to you. Not a ghost. Not a curse. Something useful.”

Jin's hand tightened around the charm.

“Who told you that?”

“Kitchen hears things. Laundry hears worse things. Servants are walls with ears and stomachs.” Deok's face hardened. “Be careful. People who cannot beat you may try to sell the reason you survived.”

He shifted his bad leg and failed to hide the wince.

“Senior Cook Jang is sending me to the southern mule sheds before dawn,” he added. “Officially because I am too useless for kitchen work until this leg stops pretending to be wood. Unofficially because Cho keeps looking at me like I owe him a cleaner story.”

Jin looked up.

Deok shrugged with only one shoulder. “If I disappear from the yard for a while, do not mistake absence for courage or betrayal. It is just geography.”

“You do not have to explain yourself to me.”

“Good,” Deok said. “I was doing it badly.”

Then he left.

Jin lay down slowly, because every other motion seemed impossible.

The mat smelled of straw and old smoke. Through the thin wall, he heard servants whispering, bowls clattering, someone coughing until they cursed their own lungs. Ordinary sounds. Living sounds.

Behind his left ear, the Ghost Core pulsed once.

`[Inquiry pending.]`

Jin stared at the ceiling.

“What are you?”

A pause.

`[Ghost Core: emergency combat cognition and survival optimization unit.]`

“Who made you?”

`[Access restricted.]`

“Why were you inside that chamber?”

`[Access restricted.]`

“Why do you know my mother?”

The room went cold.

`[Insufficient verified data.]`

That was not the same as no.

Jin's throat tightened.

“Were my meridians sealed?”

`[Confirmed.]`

The word had no pity in it.

Jin closed his eyes.

“By illness?”

`[Negative.]`

“By injury?”

`[Negative.]`

He already knew the next question.

He asked anyway because some doors had to be opened with your own hand.

“By someone?”

`[Confirmed.]`

For sixteen years, the Outer Yard had called him broken. For sixteen years, he had believed his body was a bad cup that could not hold the water everyone else drank. He had hated it. Pleaded with it. Bargained with it in the dark.

Now an impossible voice told him the cup had been sealed.

Not failed.

Sealed.

Jin did not cry.

The feeling was too large for tears.

Instead, he held his mother's thread charm until the old knot bit into his palm.

“Can you open it?”

The Ghost Core answered after a delay that felt almost human.

`[Current host condition inadequate.]`

“That is not an answer.”

`[Forced opening may result in systemic meridian collapse, neural failure, cardiac arrest, or hostile detection.]`

“So no.”

`[Not now.]`

Not now.

Two small words.

They should not have felt like a weapon.

Jin breathed carefully through the pain until the ceiling stopped swimming. Outside, night settled over the Outer Yard. The old hierarchy remained. Kang still stood above him. Discipline Hall still carried brushes sharper than knives. The forbidden tunnel had sealed itself as if it had never existed.

But inside Jin, one truth had changed shape.

He was not a dog born with broken legs.

Someone had tied the rope.

And ropes could be studied.

Ropes could be cut.

Across the Outer Yard, in the private back room of the Moonlit Medicine Pavilion, Han Seolha lit a second lamp.

She did not like second lamps.

They wasted oil. They lengthened shadows. They made secrets look more dramatic than they deserved.

Tonight, the first lamp was not enough.

On the table before her lay the folded cloth that held the black dust from Jin's wound. Beside it sat the silver needle she had used behind his ear. Its tip had darkened in a way silver should not darken unless touched by certain poisons, certain corpses, or certain metals that old physicians pretended did not exist.

Seolha opened a hidden drawer beneath the medicine table.

The mechanism clicked only after she pressed three points in the correct order. Her master had designed it. Yun Saja had designed many things that looked harmless until they saved your life.

Inside lay a thin notebook wrapped in oilcloth.

The cover bore no title.

Only a line burned into the leather with a heated needle:

**For patterns that should not return.**

Seolha's fingers paused over it.

“Master,” she whispered, “what did you find?”

The dead did not answer.

They rarely did. That was why they were safer than the living.

She opened the notebook.

The first pages were familiar. Failed treatments. Pulse anomalies. Notes on children brought to the sect with sealed records and bodies too damaged in ways too precise to be natural. Most had died. Some had disappeared. One had been marked only as Host-02 before the ink trail ended in three smeared drops of blood.

Seolha turned faster.

There.

A diagram filled the center of the page.

Two pulses crossing.

One human.

One impossibly regular.

Around the crossing point, Yun Saja had drawn a sealed star.

Beneath it were six words written in a hand Seolha knew as well as her own breath.

**Artificial blockage around primary meridian gates.**

Seolha's throat tightened.

She turned the page.

A second diagram showed a point behind the left ear. Not a wound point. Not an ordinary acupuncture gate. A place no standard meridian chart admitted mattered.

Yun Saja had circled it three times.

Beside the circle, he had written:

**If the second pulse appears, do not extract. Extraction kills the host and wakes the watcher.**

Seolha's hand went cold.

She looked toward the infirmary door, though Jin had already left.

Then she turned one more page.

The ink there was darker, pressed so hard it had scarred the paper.

**If this pattern appears in the boy, hide him before the Skyless Directorate comes.**

Below it, almost scratched through the page:

**Do not trust the sect's records of his birth.**

The lamp flame bent as wind slipped through the shutters.

Seolha sat very still.

For most of his life, the Outer Yard had called Jin Ryu the Broken Pulse Dog.

Her master had called him something else.

Not a boy.

**The boy.**

As if Jin Ryu had been waiting inside Yun Saja's notes long before the sect learned to mock him.

On the white cloth, the black dust glimmered once.

Blue.

Then dark.

Seolha closed the notebook with a careful hand.

For the first time that day, the Ice Needle Witch looked afraid.
