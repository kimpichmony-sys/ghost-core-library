Rain made the sect look clean.

That was the lie Jin Ryu hated most.

On dry days, the Obsidian Meridian Sect showed its true color. Black stone scuffed pale by thousands of boots. Training posts darkened by old blood. Outer Yard steps worn into shallow bowls by generations of disciples climbing toward power and servants carrying water behind them. The sect was a place built to remember pressure. Every wall, tile, and threshold held the mark of someone stronger pressing someone weaker into shape.

But when rain fell, all of that blurred.

The roofs shone. The courtyards reflected lanternlight. Blood became water. Mud softened footprints. Even the servants' hall looked less like a storage shed for unwanted lives and more like a building where people might sleep without listening for kicks in the dark.

Jin stood beneath the rear awning with two empty buckets beside his feet and one hand pressed against his ribs.

His breathing had improved.

That was what the Ghost Core claimed.

`[Respiratory rhythm: unstable but functional.]`

`[Rib cage status: bruising; micro-fracture risk reduced by 3.7 percent since last observation.]`

`[Recommendation: avoid twisting, sudden impact, and unnecessary confrontation.]`

Jin stared at the rain.

"Unnecessary confrontation," he muttered, "is what strong people call being found by them."

`[Statement classification: cynical but not inaccurate.]`

He did not know whether to be insulted.

Three days ago, pain had been a room with no door. He endured it because endurance was the only thing he owned. Now every ache came with a label. Every weakness came with a warning. His body, which had always felt like a sealed prison, had become a cold diagram written across the inside of his skull.

It should have been comforting.

Instead, it made him feel as if someone had lit a lantern inside his cage and asked him to be grateful for seeing the bars.

Across the courtyard, the Outer Yard disciples' quarters glowed warmly behind paper windows. Laughter leaked through the rain. Someone sang badly. Someone else shouted over a dice game. The smell of roasted chestnuts drifted from the kitchen, rich and sweet enough to make Jin's empty stomach fold in on itself.

The disciples ate roasted chestnuts.

The servants scrubbed the pans afterward.

"Jin."

Ma Doksu's voice came from the doorway behind him, low and already worried.

Jin turned.

Doksu stood with a folded oilcloth over one shoulder and a clay inventory tablet in his hand. Rainlight made his round face look paler than usual. He had the expression of a man delivering bad news while pretending it was only an errand.

"You're still breathing like an old bellows," Doksu said.

"I am breathing."

"A corpse can also release air if you step on it. That does not make it healthy."

Jin looked at him.

Doksu looked back.

For one breath, neither spoke.

Then Jin's mouth twitched despite his ribs. "That was disgusting."

"Good. You reacted. That means you are not dead yet." Doksu stepped under the awning and lowered his voice. "Old Hong wants the wine shed near the abandoned sword hall checked. Two jars are missing from the ledger. If they are actually missing, he says the servants will pay for them."

Jin looked toward the western hill.

The abandoned sword hall sat beyond the main courtyards, half-eaten by pine shadows and neglect. Once, elders had tested sword intent there. At least, that was what the kitchen boys claimed when they wanted a story to make spoiled millet taste less miserable. Now the roof sagged, the practice stones were cracked, and disciples used the place for drinking, gambling, and beating anyone too unimportant to complain.

"The wine shed," Jin said.

Doksu nodded.

"In this rain."

"Thieves like rain. Rain makes honest men lazy."

"Why me?"

Doksu glanced away.

That was enough answer.

Jin felt the name before Doksu said it.

Kang Taejun.

The memory returned with the clean cruelty of a blade: Cho's fist flying toward his face, the Ghost Core's cold warning, Jin's body moving half a finger away from death, and stone cracking beneath Cho's knuckles. Then Kang watching from across the yard, not angry enough to be careless.

Interested.

Interest from someone like Kang was worse than hatred. Hatred struck immediately. Interest waited, measured, and found better tools.

Doksu pushed the oilcloth into Jin's hands. "Old Hong first wanted you sent to clean Kang's side courtyard. I said the missing wine was more urgent."

"You argued with him?"

"I whined until he grew tired. It is one of my few martial arts."

"That is dangerous."

"So is letting you enter Kang's courtyard alone while he is wondering why his dog suddenly learned to dodge." Doksu's voice sharpened. "Go there, check the marks, write what you see, and return. Do not be clever. Do not test anything. Do not follow any strange sounds. Do not open any door that looks older than the sect."

Jin's fingers tightened around the oilcloth.

Doksu noticed, of course. Doksu noticed too much while pretending to notice nothing.

"Whatever happened in that tunnel," Doksu said quietly, "hide it better."

Jin looked at the rain.

"Nothing happened in the tunnel."

"Good. Say it exactly like that. Too flat for guilt, too boring for curiosity." Doksu thrust the clay tablet at him. "And if someone follows you, don't use the cliff path. The stones are loose."

Jin took the tablet.

He wanted to say thank you.

He wanted to say stay away from me.

He wanted to say there is something in my skull that names me Host instead of Jin, and if the people searching for it find me, they might cut open anyone who stood too close.

But every true sentence had sharp edges tonight.

So he only nodded.

Rain swallowed him as soon as he stepped off the awning.

Cold water seeped under his collar and ran down the ridge of his spine. Mud sucked at his straw sandals. Each step tugged at bruises hidden beneath his robe. The Ghost Core measured everything: distance to shelter, temperature drop, gait deviation, probability of slipping on moss-covered stone.

Jin ignored half of it.

Not out of pride.

Out of fear that if he obeyed every suggestion, he would forget where the voice ended and his will began.

The path to the abandoned sword hall climbed through crooked pines. The higher he went, the quieter the sect became. The laughter faded first. Then the kitchen smoke. Then even the training yard lamps disappeared behind veils of rain. By the time Jin reached the western hill, the world had narrowed to wet stone, black branches, and the slow pain of breathing.

The old sword hall emerged from the mist like a beast that had died sitting upright.

Its front doors hung crooked. The carved plaque above them had split down the middle, leaving only three readable characters: Sword, Still, Void. Moss filled the cuts in the wood. Rain dripped from the roof in uneven strings. Beyond the hall, a smaller shed leaned against a stone retaining wall, its roof patched with warped boards and old tiles stolen from better buildings.

The wine shed.

Jin stopped before the door.

`[Environmental scan: low light. Wood rot. Alcohol vapor concentration: high.]`

`[Auditory anomaly: interior movement detected.]`

Jin's hand froze above the latch.

Something moved inside.

Not rats.

Rats scratched. Rats hurried.

This sound was patient.

A cup touched wood.

Jin stood in the rain and considered leaving.

That was the intelligent choice. Doksu would approve. Old Hong would shout. Kang might still find another excuse tomorrow, but tomorrow was at least not tonight.

Then the Ghost Core spoke.

`[Recommendation: retreat. Threat variables unknown.]`

Jin's jaw tightened.

The voice was probably right.

That was why he hated it.

He opened the door.

Warm wine smell rolled out, thick enough to taste. The shed was larger inside than it looked, packed with stacked jars, wooden racks, cracked shelves, and dusty porcelain cups abandoned by disciples who thought theft became less shameful if they drank elegantly. A single oil lamp burned on a crate near the back.

Beside it sat an old man with clouded eyes, bare feet, a patched outer robe, and a wine gourd hanging from one loose hand.

He looked like someone the sect had forgotten to bury.

He also held a porcelain cup as if it were a weapon pretending to be fragile.

"Close the door," the old man said. "Rain ruins wine, and I am already busy ruining it myself."

Jin did not move.

The old man turned his cloudy gaze toward him.

Not at him.

Toward him.

"Boy," he said, "either enter, leave, or become furniture. I dislike indecisive furniture."

Jin stepped inside and closed the door.

The rain became a dull drum on the roof.

"This servant was sent to inspect the missing jars," Jin said.

"Terrible news." The old man lifted his cup. "You found them. They died bravely."

Jin looked at the jars near the old man's feet. Two were open. One had been resealed badly. The ledger marks on their necks matched Old Hong's missing numbers.

"Those belong to the servant hall inventory."

"Nothing belongs to a hall. Things belong to whoever has the strength to keep them or the shamelessness to deny taking them. Tonight I possess both."

Jin looked around the shed.

No other exits except a small back shutter, too narrow for a grown man but large enough for a message tube. No visible weapon. Several cups. Several jars. One old man who should have smelled harmless and somehow did not.

`[Subject assessment: elderly male. Visual impairment probable.]`

`[Musculoskeletal status: inconsistent with age presentation.]`

`[Threat probability: elevated.]`

The old man grinned.

"Your breathing changed."

Jin's spine tightened.

"My ribs hurt."

"Yes. That is the excuse. I was commenting on the reason." The old man drank. "Come closer. I want to hear how ugly your footsteps are."

Jin did not come closer.

The old man's grin widened.

"Good. Not entirely stupid. Only mostly."

Jin held the clay tablet against his side. "Should I write that an elder stole wine?"

"If you know I am an elder, you are already too informed for a servant."

"The servants talk."

"Servants talk better than elders. Elders lie to protect dignity. Servants lie to survive. Survival gives language flavor." The old man tilted his head. "Name."

Jin hesitated.

Names were handles. Handles let people carry you wherever they pleased.

"Jin Ryu."

"Ah." The old man clicked his tongue. "Broken Pulse Dog."

The words landed where they always did.

In the throat.

Behind the eyes.

Beneath the ribs, where anger had learned to fold itself small.

Jin lowered his gaze by habit, then forced it up again. The effort hurt more than the insult.

"Yes," he said.

The old man stopped smiling for half a breath.

Then he laughed.

It was a terrible sound. Not cruel, exactly. Too delighted for that. Too old. Too full of dust and knives.

"Good answer," he said. "Most boys deny the leash while still choking on it. You at least know what they call you."

"Knowing a name does not make it true."

"Better." The old man pointed his cup at him. "There may be a spine under all that servant mud. Stand nearer."

`[Recommendation: maintain distance.]`

Jin took one step forward.

The Ghost Core went silent in a way that felt offended.

The old man's clouded eyes remained unfocused, but his head turned precisely with Jin's movement.

"You walk differently than yesterday," he said.

Jin's mouth went dry.

"You saw me yesterday?"

"No." The old man lifted the cup to his nose. "I heard the yard talking about a dog that learned not to bite but to make a fist bite stone. Yards are shameless gossips. Stones worse. They keep everything."

Jin said nothing.

"Take another step."

"Why?"

"Because if I wanted to kill you, asking politely first would be wasteful."

That was not comforting.

Jin stepped again.

Something flicked through the air.

A porcelain cup.

Not thrown hard. Not thrown to kill. It spun from the old man's fingers as if released by accident, white against lamplight, harmless until the Ghost Core exploded behind Jin's eyes.

`[Incoming object. Trajectory: left cheek. Avoidance route available.]`

Jin moved.

Too fast in thought.

Too slow in flesh.

He tried to follow the glowing line that appeared in his vision, but his left foot slipped on spilled wine. His ribs seized. The cup clipped his cheek instead of smashing into it.

Porcelain shattered against the rack behind him.

Pain opened warm across his face.

The old man sipped wine from a second cup.

"Ugly," he said.

Jin pressed two fingers to his cheek. Blood came away bright.

"You threw that at me."

"An impressive investigation. Perhaps Old Hong should promote you to noticing captain."

`[Minor laceration detected. Blood loss negligible.]`

`[Motor response failure: foot traction miscalculated; rib pain interference underestimated.]`

Jin almost snapped back at the voice.

The old man threw the second cup.

This one came lower, toward Jin's shin.

`[Incoming object. Jump avoidance recommended.]`

Jin nearly obeyed.

Then remembered the ribs.

He did not jump. He dragged his foot back half a step, ugly and late. The cup struck his shin anyway, but only glanced off before breaking near the door.

Pain flashed up his leg.

The old man leaned forward.

"That one was yours."

Jin breathed through his teeth.

"What?"

"First dodge was borrowed. Too neat in the head, too late in the bones. Second dodge was frightened, clumsy, and therefore closer to honest." The old man picked up a third cup. "Again."

Jin's hand clenched around the clay tablet.

"I came for inventory."

"And found education. The heavens are generous when drunk."

The third cup flew.

This time the Ghost Core drew three lines. One clean. One safer. One miserable.

The clean line required a twist his ribs could not bear.

The safer line put him too close to the shelves.

The miserable line barely deserved to be called movement: bend the knee, drop the shoulder, let the cup pass close enough to steal warmth from the skin.

Jin chose the miserable line.

The cup hissed past his ear and broke against the doorframe.

For a heartbeat, only rain spoke.

The old man smiled.

Not the mocking grin from before.

A small, sharp thing.

"There," he said. "The dog has teeth in his feet."

Jin's cheek burned. His shin throbbed. The Ghost Core flickered with irritation across his sight.

`[Avoidance success. Efficiency: poor.]`

"Poor is alive," Jin whispered.

The old man heard him.

His laugh filled the shed again.

"Now that," he said, "is almost wisdom."

Jin stared at him through the lamplight. The old man's eyes were clouded white, but his face did not carry the softness of blindness. It carried attention stripped of needing eyes.

The rumors returned, no longer sounding like servant entertainment.

Baek Guryong.

The Blind Wine Ghost.

A sword instructor before Jin was born. A traitor, according to one version. A war hero, according to another. An elder with no hall, no disciples, no formal authority, and enough old fear around his name that even drunk outer disciples lowered their voices when joking about him.

The old man who had once killed seven assassins with a chopstick.

Probably false.

Probably.

"Master Baek," Jin said.

Baek's expression soured. "Do not say it like that."

"Like what?"

"Like you are placing your neck on a block and asking me to admire the angle." Baek pointed his cup at Jin's posture. "Straighten."

Jin straightened.

It hurt.

Baek nodded. "Better. Fear is allowed. Folding is not."

"Servants fold."

"Then be a poor servant. You already have practice at being poor."

The insult should have stung.

It did.

But beneath it was something Jin had rarely heard from anyone above him.

Instruction.

Baek poured more wine with the solemnity of a priest performing funeral rites for his own liver. "Now tell me, Broken Pulse Dog. What corrected your first step?"

Jin's blood chilled.

"Pain."

"Pain corrects idiots by making them dead. Try again."

"Luck."

"Luck has no rhythm. Yours did."

"Fear."

"Fear made your pupils shrink before the cup left my hand. Fear is useful. It did not draw that line down your left side." Baek tapped his temple with one finger. "Something counted."

The Ghost Core's silence became enormous.

Jin could hear rain sliding from the roof. Wine dripping from a cracked jar. His own pulse striking the wound behind his left ear.

"I don't know what you mean," he said.

Baek sighed. "You lie like a boy who has never been believed enough to practice. Very tragic. Very inefficient."

"Are you going to report me?"

"To whom? The Discipline Hall that only sees crimes committed by people without backing? The elders who think the sect's foundations are older than their own greed? Old Hong, whose ledger I have heroically lightened?" Baek drank. "No. I hate reports. Paperwork is how cowards stab from far away."

That was not mercy.

Jin knew better than to relax around a man who used jokes as curtains.

"Then what do you want?"

"Tonight? Wine. Usually? Fewer fools. From you?" Baek's cloudy gaze settled near Jin's face. "I want to know why a boy with dead meridians moves like someone listening to a star fall."

The words struck something inside the Ghost Core.

A cold pulse passed through Jin's skull.

`[Unknown phrase detected: star fall.]`

`[Archive correlation: restricted.]`

`[Do not disclose internal systems.]`

Jin's fingers tightened.

Baek's eyebrows rose. "Ah. There. It heard me."

Jin took one step back.

The old man did not move.

"Do not look so hunted," Baek said. "If I wanted you dead, I would have used the jar. Cups are for flirting."

"That was flirting?"

"My technique has declined with age."

Jin almost smiled.

Almost.

Baek caught the almost and looked pleased. "Still a boy under the suspicion. Good. Machines make poor disciples. Revenge makes worse ones."

The word machine hit too close.

Jin's voice sharpened. "I never said machine."

"No. But your face did when I said counted." Baek set down the cup. "Listen carefully. Whatever has crawled into your life, do not let it train the fear out of you."

"Fear is weakness."

"Spoken like someone taught by idiots with full stomachs." Baek snorted. "Fear is information. Remove it, and you become a corpse with elegant posture. The trick is not to kill fear. The trick is to make it report honestly."

The Ghost Core did not comment.

Jin hated that he wanted it to.

Baek leaned back against the rack. "Something is helping you see paths. It cannot walk them for you without stealing the shape of your legs. Remember that."

Jin thought of the tunnel. Of the black door opening. Of cold metal biting behind his ear. Of the voice naming him Host, not Jin.

Host.

A place for something else to live.

"What if I have no choice?" he asked.

Baek's face changed.

The drunkenness did not vanish. It stepped aside.

"Then make one," he said.

Simple words.

Impossible words.

Jin had spent years believing choices belonged to other people. Elders chose. Disciples chose. Kang chose who bled and when. Servants survived the shape of those choices and called the result life.

Then the black door opened.

Then a voice in his skull began offering survival like a bargain written in a language he could not yet read.

Choice felt like a story told to boys who had parents, backing, and unsealed meridians.

Baek reached into the sleeve of his robe and tossed something underhand.

Jin flinched.

Then caught it.

A cork.

Small. Dark. Stained with wine.

Jin stared.

"Cork?"

"A profound treasure. Very rare. I stole it from the floor."

"What am I supposed to do with it?"

"Stand three steps from a wall. Throw it. Before it returns, choose where your foot goes. Not where the counting voice says first. Not where pride says second. Where you choose after listening to both. Then compare." Baek lifted one finger. "If you always obey the first answer, you become predictable. If you always reject it, you become dead. Learn the difference."

Jin closed his fingers around the cork.

It was trash.

It was the first training tool anyone had ever given him.

His throat tightened so suddenly he had to look down.

Baek made a disgusted sound. "Do not weep on it. Cork swells."

"I was not going to weep."

"Good. Tears improve very little except funerals and bad poetry."

Jin tucked the cork into his sleeve with more care than it deserved.

"Thank you," he said.

He bowed before habit could deepen it into a servant's fold.

Not low.

Not broken.

A small bow.

A human one.

Baek looked away. "Too early to thank me. I might still sell you for better wine."

"You said paperwork is cowardly."

"For cheap wine, yes. For excellent wine, a man discovers courage."

This time Jin did smile.

A small one.

Brief.

Still real enough that it frightened him.

Then the floorboard outside the shed creaked.

Not rain.

Not wood settling.

A careful foot choosing how not to sound like a foot.

The Ghost Core flared.

`[Presence detected. Distance: four paces.]`

`[Breath suppression: trained.]`

`[Threat level: high.]`

Baek's drunken slouch returned so quickly it seemed it had never left.

His hand found the wine gourd. His head tipped. His mouth loosened.

"Inventory boy," he called loudly, "you saw nothing. I drank nothing. The missing jars eloped."

The door opened without a knock.

A gray-robed clerk stood in the rain.

At first glance, he belonged to Steward Hall: plain robe, waxed tablet case, narrow cap, expression pinched by the suffering of minor authority. But Jin had scrubbed enough Steward Hall floors to know their clerks. Real clerks cultivated irritation like a garden. They smelled of ink, lamp oil, and resentment.

This man smelled faintly of bitter medicine and cold metal.

His sandals were too clean for the path.

His eyes moved first to Jin's cheek, then to the wound hidden behind Jin's left ear.

Too fast.

Too precise.

Jin lowered his head before anger could show.

`[Facial observation: subject has identified implant vicinity.]`

`[Recommendation: concealment posture.]`

Jin shifted as if embarrassed, letting wet hair fall against the side of his face.

The gray clerk smiled.

"Steward Hall received a report of unauthorized presence near old stores," he said. "Inventory records must be inspected."

Baek raised his wine gourd. "Excellent. Inspect this. It has suffered greatly."

The clerk's smile did not move. "Elder Baek."

"Am I? People keep telling me that. Very suspicious."

"This servant is assigned here?"

Jin held out the clay tablet with both hands. "Old Hong sent me to verify missing jars."

The clerk did not take it immediately.

His gaze dipped to Jin's sleeve.

Where the cork rested.

Impossible that he could know.

Unless he had watched.

Unless he had been watching before the door.

Jin kept his breathing shallow and dull.

Weak boy. Injured boy. Servant boy. Nothing worth opening.

The clerk took the tablet.

For a moment, his fingers brushed Jin's.

Cold.

Not cold from rain.

The Ghost Core flashed red behind Jin's eyes.

`[Contact scan attempt detected.]`

`[External diagnostic pulse: low intensity.]`

`[Concealment protocol recommended.]`

Pain sparked behind Jin's left ear.

He swallowed it.

Baek hiccupped.

The sound was ridiculous.

The wine gourd flew from his hand.

It struck the clerk's wrist just hard enough to make the clay tablet slip.

The tablet fell.

Baek lurched forward to catch it, missed magnificently, kicked a jar, and sent wine splashing across the floor in a fragrant wave.

The clerk stepped back to avoid the spill.

His hand withdrew from Jin.

The scan broke.

Baek blinked clouded eyes. "Tragic. The wine attacked first."

The clerk stared at him.

For one instant, the polite mask thinned.

Something cold looked through.

Then the mask returned.

"Elder Baek should rest," the clerk said.

"I have tried. Rest is boring. Wine has better manners."

The clerk looked at Jin again. "You are injured."

"Yes, honored clerk."

"How?"

Jin touched his cheek. "I slipped."

Baek gasped. "On my reputation, no doubt. It is very wet."

The clerk's eyes remained on Jin. "And behind the ear?"

Jin let confusion enter his face slowly, as if fear had to travel through mud to reach him. "Behind my ear?"

"There is swelling."

"Cho hit me near the water basin two days ago," Jin said.

Not a perfect lie.

A useful one.

The clerk's expression did not change.

"Outer Yard violence has become troublesome."

"Has it?" Baek asked brightly. "I thought it was the curriculum."

The clerk closed the tablet case. He had not written anything.

"I will report that the missing jars were found damaged by roof leakage. The servant hall should not be charged."

Jin bowed.

"Thank you, honored clerk."

The clerk stepped back into the rain.

At the threshold, he paused.

"Jin Ryu," he said.

Jin's pulse tightened.

He had not given the man his name.

The clerk smiled faintly. "Take care not to slip again. Some wounds reopen when examined."

Then he was gone.

Neither Jin nor Baek moved until the Ghost Core spoke.

`[Presence departing.]`

`[Threat unresolved.]`

`[External diagnostic origin: unknown. Pattern similarity: restricted.]`

Baek's drunken mask fell away.

"Not Steward Hall," he said.

Jin wiped rainwater and blood from his cheek. "No."

"You knew from his feet?"

"From his eyes. He looked at the wrong wound first."

Baek's mouth curled. "Good. Paranoia is only ugly when it is wrong."

Jin looked at the door.

His hand still tingled where the clerk had touched him.

"Who was he?"

"A man pretending to be small."

"That is not an answer."

"It is the kind of answer that keeps boys with strange heads breathing."

Jin turned back to Baek. Rain drummed overhead. The lamp flame shook.

"You know more than you are saying."

"Obviously. I am old. If I did not know more than I said, age would be a terrible investment."

"About the thing in me."

Baek's humor thinned.

For a moment, he seemed to listen not to Jin but to something far beneath the shed, beneath the old sword hall, beneath the black bones of the mountain.

"I know enough to be afraid of men who ask about silent stars," Baek said. "And enough to be afraid of boys who answer them."

The Ghost Core pulsed once.

`[Phrase correlation: silent stars.]`

`[Archive access denied.]`

Jin tasted metal.

"Silent stars," he repeated.

Baek cursed softly. "Do not repeat old words just because they shine. That is how traps train prey."

"My mother knew those words?"

Baek stilled.

The rain seemed to press harder against the roof.

Jin had not meant to ask it so directly. The word mother escaped because the old man's caution had shaped a space exactly that size.

Baek took a long drink.

Then another.

"Dangerous question," he said.

"You said names have handles. You also said I should make choices."

"Rude child. Quoting a man against himself."

"Did you know her?"

Baek did not answer.

That was answer enough to make Jin's heart climb into his throat.

"What was her name?" Jin asked.

"No."

"Please."

"Worse."

Jin stepped forward. His ribs protested. He ignored them. "Everyone here calls me dog. The Ghost Core calls me Host. You call me boy. I have one person in my memories who called me anything else, and even her voice is broken. If you know her name, tell me."

Baek's face hardened.

Not in anger.

In pain.

It made him look suddenly less like a drunk legend and more like an old man who had survived too many doors closing behind people he could not save.

"If I give you that name," he said, "it may wake things you cannot put back to sleep."

"Things are already awake."

Baek's clouded eyes turned toward the wound behind Jin's ear.

"Yes," he said quietly. "They are."

Silence held them.

Then Baek said one word.

"Aera."

The shed vanished.

Not completely.

The rain remained, but it moved farther away. The lamp became a star seen through water. The wine smell thinned into smoke and medicinal bitterness.

A woman's hand touched Jin's cheek.

Warm.

Callused at the base of the fingers.

A red thread brushed his skin.

A voice whispered, "If they ask what you are, be ordinary. If they ask what you saw, be blind. If they ask what I gave you—"

Static tore through the memory.

Silver pain.

A door without stars.

A boy crying without sound.

Jin gasped back into the shed with one hand gripping the rack and blood running from his nose.

`[Memory fragment forcibly triggered.]`

`[Neurological strain detected.]`

`[Recommendation: cease emotional trigger exposure immediately.]`

Baek was standing.

Jin had not seen him rise.

The old man no longer looked drunk at all.

"Aera," Jin whispered.

The name hurt.

It also fit.

Like a missing bone returning to the body.

Baek's jaw worked once. "I should not have said it."

"You should have said it years ago."

"I did not know you were here."

"Everyone knew I was here."

"No." Baek's voice sharpened. "Everyone knew Broken Pulse Dog was here. That is not the same thing."

Jin had no answer for that.

He wiped his nose with his sleeve. Blood smeared dark across the cloth. The Ghost Core urged stillness. His heart refused.

"Was she a disciple?"

"More than that. Less than the stories would claim."

"Did the sect kill her?"

Baek looked toward the rain-dark door.

"The sect kills many things without knowing which knife did the cutting."

"That is another safe answer."

"Yes. You should collect them. They are useful armor until you can survive unsafe ones."

Jin wanted to hate him for that.

He could not.

Not completely.

Baek moved to the back shelf and reached behind a row of old jars. His fingers found something hidden in shadow. He drew it out briefly: a strip of black thread so fine it bent like cloth and caught the lamplight like metal. Along one edge marked into the weave was a broken circle with three short lines.

Jin's breath stopped.

The black door beneath the mountain had worn a shape almost like that.

The Ghost Core reacted with painful cold.

`[Relic material detected.]`

`[Origin: classified.]`

`[Archive access denied.]`

Baek closed his fist around the thread before Jin could reach for it.

"Your mother left something like this after a fight," he said. "At the time, I thought it came from an assassin's sleeve. Later, I learned wiser men had died for knowing less."

"Give it to me."

"No."

"It was hers."

"And if you carry it, you will not reach morning." Baek returned the thread to its hiding place. "Tonight, you carry a cork. A cork means nothing. A relic thread means knives in your sleep."

Jin's hand tightened around the sleeve where the cork rested.

"Why show me?"

"Because ignorance is a cage. Knowledge is a knife. A boy should know which sharp things exist before deciding which ones to touch."

"You sound like you are teaching."

"Disgusting accusation. Take it back."

Jin did not smile this time.

His cheek hurt. His nose bled. His memory had been cut open by a single name. Outside, a false clerk walked away with a report that might already be reaching whoever hunted the word Host.

And yet, inside his sleeve, a piece of trash had become the weight of a beginning.

Baek sat again, slower now.

Age returned to him like a robe.

"Go," he said. "Before Old Hong decides the wine has eloped with you too."

"What do I write?"

"Write that rain damaged the ledger, the jars were accounted for, and no elder of dignity was found committing theft."

"That is a lie."

"It is three lies. Be precise."

Jin picked up the cracked clay tablet. The gray clerk's touch had left no visible mark, but his fingers still felt cold.

At the door, he paused.

"Master Baek."

"I told you not to say it like that."

"Why help me?"

The old man looked down into his cup.

For once, he did not answer quickly.

"Because Aera once asked me to open my eyes," he said. "And I was too proud to admit they were already useless."

Jin did not understand.

He also understood too much.

He bowed again.

Small.

Human.

Then he stepped into the rain.

The path down from the western hill had become a black ribbon of mud and reflected lanternlight. Wind pushed water through the pines. Somewhere below, the Outer Yard still laughed, unaware that a false clerk had come and gone, that an old drunk had named a dead woman, that a boy with sealed meridians carried a cork as if it were a sword manual.

Jin walked slowly.

Not because the Ghost Core told him to.

Because he chose each step.

Halfway down, the voice spoke.

`[Training object acquired: cork.]`

`[Suggested protocol: reactive movement calibration.]`

Jin kept walking.

"Not yet," he said.

`[Clarify.]`

"First I choose where to stand. Then you may tell me how badly I chose."

The Ghost Core was silent for three breaths.

`[Host preference recorded.]`

Host.

Jin touched the wound behind his ear through wet hair.

"My name is Jin Ryu."

The voice did not respond.

When he reached the servant hall, Doksu was waiting beneath the awning exactly where Jin had left him, pretending not to be waiting at all. He held a broom upside down like a weapon and looked offended by the weather.

His eyes went immediately to Jin's cheek.

"You checked wine," Doksu said flatly, "and the wine attacked you?"

"Something like that."

"I hate when I am right."

Jin handed him the clay tablet.

Doksu examined the cracked edge, the smeared marks, and the half-written lie. "Rain damaged the ledger?"

"Master Baek suggested it."

Doksu went very still.

"You met the Blind Wine Ghost."

"Yes."

"And survived."

"Mostly."

Doksu looked at the cut on Jin's cheek again. "Did he teach you anything?"

Jin thought of cups spinning through lamplight. Of fear as information. Of Aera's name. Of a black metal thread hidden behind wine jars. Of the false clerk's cold fingers.

He slid a hand into his sleeve and touched the cork.

"A little."

Doksu stared.

"That answer makes me want to leave the sect and become a turnip farmer."

"You would eat the inventory."

"True. A tragic flaw."

For a moment, they stood beneath the awning listening to the rain.

Then Doksu took the tablet and said, more quietly, "You are shaking."

Jin looked down at his own hands.

They were.

Not from cold.

Not only from pain.

Aera.

The name moved inside him like a buried ember disturbed by breath.

Doksu did not ask.

For that, Jin was grateful enough that it hurt.

"Get inside," Doksu said. "Before Old Hong sees your face and charges you for damaging it."

Jin stepped into the servants' hall.

Behind him, high on the western hill, the wine shed lamp winked out.

In the dark, Baek Guryong remained seated beside the stolen jars long after the boy left.

The old man did not drink.

He listened to the rain, to the mountain, to the deep places beneath the sect where old machines dreamed in stone.

After a long while, he reached behind the jar and touched the strip of black metal thread.

His clouded eyes lifted toward nothing.

"Aera," he murmured, and this time no laughter followed. "What did you hide in your son?"
