The stone beneath Black Needle Cliff had spoken once.

It did not speak again.

Jin Ryu returned before dawn with mud dried around his ankles, old scratches reopened across his palms, and Master Baek's cork clenched between his teeth hard enough to make his jaw ache. The cliff wind crawled through the pines above him. Below, the Obsidian Meridian Sect slept beneath layers of fog, its roof tiles hidden, its lanterns dying one by one like tired stars.

At the base of the cliff, half-buried beneath thorn grass and broken shale, the strange fragment lay exactly where the Ghost Core had marked it the previous evening.

It was no bigger than two finger joints.

Black. Smooth. Wrong.

Not stone. Not metal. Not jade.

Something old enough to belong under a mountain.

Something clean enough to look newly placed.

Jin crouched above it without touching.

`[Dormant relay fragment detected.]`

The cold voice in his skull had repeated those words three times since yesterday, as if saying them again might make Jin less foolish.

It had not.

“What does a relay fragment do?” Jin whispered.

The cliff answered with wind.

The Ghost Core answered with silence.

Jin frowned. “Do not pretend you did not hear me.”

`[Known function: signal reception, signal transmission, short-range synchronization.]`

“Known by who?”

Another pause.

Longer this time.

`[Data source corrupted.]`

Jin stared at the fragment.

The words were plain, but the feeling behind them was not. Ghost Core did not speak with emotion. It did not sigh, hesitate, complain, or fear. Yet sometimes its pauses carried weight. Not the weight of a person thinking, but of a locked door knowing there was someone alive behind it.

A relay.

Signal reception.

Signal transmission.

Synchronization.

The words meant little to most disciples. They belonged to a world of signal lamps, messenger birds, and secret orders passed through coded knots.

To Jin, they meant something simpler.

Someone could listen.

Or call.

He extended one finger.

`[Contact not recommended.]`

Jin stopped one hairbreadth from the fragment.

“Danger?”

`[Unknown.]`

“Will it explode?”

`[Unknown.]`

“Will it summon someone?”

`[Unknown.]`

“Helpful.”

`[Correct.]`

Jin almost laughed, then bit down on it.

Laughter carried in the morning stillness. So did footsteps. So did panic. He had learned that long before the Ghost Core lived in his head. Dogs survived by keeping their joy quiet and their pain quieter.

He drew his sleeve over his fingers, pinched the fragment through the cloth, and lifted.

Nothing happened.

No lightning. No scream. No hidden door opening beneath his feet.

Only a faint tremor behind his left ear, where the wound from the implant still sometimes throbbed when the weather changed.

`[Weak handshake attempt detected.]`

Jin froze.

The blue lines across his vision flickered once. Not the normal scan lines Ghost Core used to measure distance, angle, heat, and pulse. These were thinner. Older. They curved in impossible patterns before collapsing into noise.

`[External device seeking host verification.]`

“Reject it.”

`[Rejected.]`

The tremor stopped.

Jin’s breath came shallow.

For three heartbeats, the world under the cliff felt larger than the mountain above it. The Obsidian Meridian Sect, with its proud halls and cruel training yards, suddenly seemed like a clay lid over a sealed well. Everyone walked, fought, lied, and bowed on top of something they did not understand.

Jin wrapped the fragment in old cloth and slid it into the hidden seam inside his servant robe.

If it could call something, he needed it hidden.

If it could listen, he needed it away from other people.

If it could answer questions, he needed Seolha.

A twig snapped above the cliff path.

Jin flattened himself behind a thorn bush before the thought finished forming.

Two disciples came down the narrow trail carrying buckets. Outer Yard kitchen boys. Both wore gray aprons and moved with the heavy-footed misery of people awake before their bodies agreed to the arrangement. One had a thin face and a permanent scowl. The other yawned so wide Jin could see the pale coating on his tongue.

“Why here?” the yawning one complained. “Water yard is closer.”

“Because Elder Han says cliff spring water tastes cleaner.”

“Elder Han also says pine smoke improves digestion.”

“Argue with him, then.”

“I am stupid, not suicidal.”

They passed without seeing Jin.

He waited until their footsteps faded before he moved.

His ribs protested as he stood. The poison had not come yet. The attack had not come yet. But his body felt as though it expected both. Every improvement carried a bill. Broken Star Step had saved him from stones yesterday, but it had also stirred the sealed lattice in his meridians until his insides felt scraped raw.

`[Left knee inflammation: minor.]`

“I know.”

`[Right ankle instability: moderate.]`

“I know.”

`[Sleep debt: severe.]`

Jin began climbing the path back toward the servant quarters. “Then sing me a lullaby.”

`[No relevant protocol found.]`

“That was a joke.”

`[Joke classification: low efficiency.]`

This time Jin did laugh, but only once.

The laugh died when the wind shifted.

Smoke. Rice steam. Morning gruel.

His stomach clenched with hunger so sharp it felt like being stabbed by something familiar. He had trained before breakfast again. Worse, he had carried water, hidden a forbidden fragment, and spent half the night failing to sleep because every creak in the servant hall sounded like a Discipline Hall clerk coming to count his heartbeat.

He needed food.

That simple need was almost embarrassing.

Ancient relics could hide beneath cliffs. Dead mothers could leave secrets inside sealed meridians. A strange machine-voice could calculate the angle of a thrown stone. Hidden directorates could move through the sect wearing false faces.

But if Jin did not eat, his hands shook.

That was the cruel honesty of flesh. No matter how many impossible things woke inside his skull, his body remained a starving boy's body first.

By the time he reached the rear kitchen, the sun had begun leaking pale gold over the Outer Yard roofs. Disciples crowded near the main serving tables in loose lines sorted by rank, strength, and arrogance. Inner candidates got polished bowls and side dishes with actual meat. Ordinary Outer Yard disciples got rice, salted greens, and soup thin enough to reflect regret. Servants ate last behind the kitchen wall from chipped bowls that had outlived three generations of hands.

Jin belonged behind the wall.

Doksu was already there, sitting on an overturned crate with his bowl balanced on one knee and his round face twisted in concentration.

He held two pairs of chopsticks.

One pair in his right hand.

One pair in his left.

Jin stopped. “What are you doing?”

Doksu looked up with the solemn expression of a general preparing for war. “Training.”

“With rice?”

“Ambidextrous eating.”

“Why?”

“In case my right hand gets cut off.”

Jin stared at him.

Doksu shrugged. “You keep getting chased into forbidden tunnels, stabbed by air, and visited by suspicious physicians. I am preparing for the friendship's future.”

“That is your preparation?”

Doksu raised the left chopsticks. A clump of rice fell immediately back into the bowl.

“I am at the beginning of the path.”

For a moment, the morning became almost normal.

Almost.

Jin sat beside him. His body wanted to collapse, but he kept his back straight because the Ghost Core had trained him to notice how weakness invited attention. He set the cork beside his bowl. Master Baek had ordered him to keep it between his teeth during morning breathing, then remove it only to eat. If he bit through it, Baek had said, he would be forced to train with a river pebble next.

Doksu eyed the cork. “You know most people train with swords.”

“Most people have swords.”

“You have teeth?”

“Apparently.”

Doksu leaned closer. “Does the cork make you stronger?”

“It makes me annoyed.”

“Elder Baek’s training is deep.”

Jin picked up his bowl.

Steam rose from the rice in soft white curls. Nothing looked wrong. Nothing smelled wrong at first. The grains were clumped from poor washing, a few edges browned from the bottom of the pot. There were salted greens on the side and two strips of dried radish. Servant food. Cheap food. Safe because no one important cared enough to poison it.

That was what Jin thought.

Then he lifted the bowl closer.

A metallic bitterness touched the back of his tongue before the rice touched his lips.

He stopped.

`[Warning.]`

The world tightened.

Doksu shoved a mouthful into his cheek. “What?”

Jin did not answer.

The Ghost Core’s blue scan lines spread across the bowl. They broke the steam into layers, separated moisture from heat, starch from salt, harmless kitchen ash from something else.

Something fine.

Something deliberate.

`[Toxic compound detected.]`

Jin’s fingers tightened around the bowl until his knuckles whitened.

“How bad?” he whispered.

Doksu blinked. “The rice? Always bad.”

`[Compound profile: meridian relaxant, blood-thinning agent, qi-diffusion catalyst.]`

A second line appeared.

`[Additive traces consistent with Iron Meridian Softening Powder.]`

The words were unfamiliar, but the Ghost Core marked them red.

Jin’s heartbeat slowed in the wrong way.

He had not swallowed.

Not yet.

Carefully, he lowered the bowl.

Doksu’s smile faded. “Jin?”

“Do not eat mine.”

“I was not planning to. You bite people now.”

“Doksu.”

The tone made his friend put his bowl down.

Jin leaned closer to the rice, breathing through his mouth. The metallic smell was almost impossible to notice beneath steam and salt. If he had been one breath hungrier, if he had eaten without scanning, if the Ghost Core had been silent one heartbeat longer—

His stomach twisted.

Not from poison.

From the thought of how close ordinary hunger had come to killing him.

`[Recommended action: preserve sample. Identify delivery vector. Avoid ingestion.]`

Jin looked toward the kitchen door.

Kitchen boys moved in and out carrying empty pots. An old cook shouted about lazy hands. A servant girl scrubbed a cutting board with sand. Steam fogged the rear wall. Too many people. Too many paths. Too many hands.

Then his vision flickered.

A ghostly image appeared.

Not from now.

From earlier.

The Ghost Core dragged a memory forward: Jin entering the rear kitchen; Doksu waving with two pairs of chopsticks; a gray-sleeved kitchen hand setting a bowl near the crate before moving away. The memory slowed. Enlarged. Clarified.

The hand wore no ring.

But the wrist bore a thin mark, like skin rubbed raw beneath a bracelet.

`[Recent compression mark detected. Possible removed identification band.]`

Jin’s mouth went dry.

“Can you track him?”

`[Insufficient visual data.]`

“Of course.”

Doksu whispered, “Track who?”

Jin covered the bowl with his sleeve. “Someone put medicine in this.”

Doksu’s face changed so fast it hurt to see. Confusion first. Then anger. Then fear.

“Medicine good or medicine bad?”

“The kind you give a rat before you see how long it takes to stop twitching.”

Doksu went pale.

His first instinct was not to step away.

He grabbed Jin’s wrist instead.

“Did you eat?”

“No.”

“You swear?”

“I smelled it.”

Doksu stared at him. “You smelled poison in rice?”

Jin hesitated.

Too late.

Suspicion did not always arrive wearing a black robe. Sometimes it wore a friend’s worried eyes.

“I have been sick so long I notice bad things,” Jin said.

It was not a complete lie. It was only missing the part where a dead machine in his skull had become better at noticing danger than his own hunger.

Doksu looked unconvinced, but fear overruled curiosity. “We go to Lady Han.”

“Not yet.”

“What do you mean not yet? Someone poisoned you.”

“And if we run straight to the Medicine Pavilion, the person watching learns three things.”

Doksu swallowed. “What things?”

“That I detected it. That I have someone helping me. That poison does not make me panic.”

“You are panicking very calmly.”

“I am improving.”

Doksu did not laugh.

That made the morning colder.

Jin took an old scrap of cloth from his sleeve, scooped a small portion of rice into it, and tied the cloth tight. Then he lifted the bowl and rose.

Doksu stood with him. “Where are you going?”

“To return my meal.”

“That sounds stupid.”

“It is stupid.”

“Then why?”

“Because if a dog refuses food, the master checks the bowl.”

Doksu’s face twisted. “I hate when you say things like that.”

“So do I.”

They moved toward the waste pit behind the kitchen.

Jin kept his gait uneven, ordinary, tired. Not too fast. Not too careful. He let his shoulders droop, let his head dip, let the world see the same broken servant disciple it had always seen. Ghost Core marked possible watchers in his vision: kitchen door, grain shed roof, laundry wall, practice yard corner. Most were harmless. A cook. A servant. A disciple scratching his nose.

One was not.

A thin man stood near the grain shed with a ledger tucked under one arm.

He wore kitchen gray, but his shoes were too clean.

His posture held no servant’s exhaustion.

His eyes did not stay on Jin.

That was why Jin noticed him.

People who hated Jin stared openly. People who pitied him looked away. People who were curious glanced and forgot.

The thin man watched the space around Jin instead.

Escape paths.

Witnesses.

Distance to the gate.

`[Threat probability: elevated.]`

Jin emptied the poisoned rice into the waste pit.

He made sure some grains stuck to the side of the bowl.

Then he coughed.

Once.

Hard.

He bent forward, gripping his stomach.

Doksu caught on half a breath later and widened his eyes with theatrical horror so excessive that Jin nearly regretted trusting him.

“Jin!” Doksu shouted. “Your stomach again?”

Too loud.

Too dramatic.

But useful.

Several heads turned.

The thin man near the grain shed did not move.

His eyes sharpened.

Jin coughed again, making it wetter this time. He let saliva drip from his mouth and wiped it with his sleeve.

`[Performance quality: moderate.]`

“Not now,” Jin muttered.

Doksu leaned close, pretending to support him. “Am I doing good?”

“You sound like a dying ox.”

“That is my best work.”

The thin man finally turned away.

Not toward the kitchen.

Toward the side path leading behind the laundry wall.

Jin’s vision marked him blue.

`[Subject retreating.]`

“Doksu,” Jin whispered.

“Yes?”

“Go to Seolha. Take the sample. Use the lower herb path. Do not run until you are out of sight.”

“What about you?”

“I am going to follow him.”

“No.”

“Quiet.”

“No, that is the answer. No. You are weak, injured, cursed, and apparently delicious to poisoners.”

Jin looked at him.

Doksu’s hand tightened around his sleeve.

For all his foolishness, Ma Doksu was not stupid. Cowardly sometimes. Hungry often. Easily frightened. But not stupid.

He knew exactly what following the thin man meant.

It meant Jin was choosing danger on purpose.

“I cannot keep being the last person to understand who wants me dead,” Jin said softly.

Doksu’s mouth opened, then closed.

Jin placed the cloth-wrapped rice into his hand.

“If I do not come to the Medicine Pavilion before sunset, tell Seolha about the man with clean shoes.”

“That is a terrible last message.”

“It is not last.”

“It better not be.”

Doksu shoved the sample into his robe and lumbered toward the lower herb path, trying very hard not to look like someone carrying evidence of attempted murder.

He looked exactly like someone carrying evidence of attempted murder.

Jin hoped no one noticed.

Then he followed the thin man.

Not closely.

He could not afford closely. The man moved with professional patience, turning corners without looking back, pausing only when natural pauses made sense: beside stacked laundry baskets, near a servant arguing with a cook, beside a wall where ivy had died from winter frost. Each pause gave him a reflection, a sound, a chance to check pursuit.

Jin stayed three turns behind.

Ghost Core helped.

Not by making him invisible. Nothing so generous. It measured rhythm: how long the man took between corners, which paths avoided crowds, which patches of gravel would crunch, which roof eaves dripped water loud enough to cover a step.

`[Recommended route: left drainage channel.]`

Jin slipped behind a line of drying robes and stepped into the shallow ditch. Cold mud swallowed his sandals. He ignored it.

`[Breathing rate rising.]`

“I know.”

`[Fear response detected.]`

“I know.”

`[Fear may impair observation.]`

“Then observe for me.”

`[Acknowledged.]`

The thin man left the Outer Yard kitchens and entered the narrow service lanes between low storage buildings. Few disciples came here unless punished. The smell changed from rice steam to old straw, lamp oil, damp wood, and mouse droppings.

Jin’s hand drifted toward the cork in his sleeve.

Not a weapon.

But Baek had taught him to hold it between his teeth until his jaw stopped fighting the rest of his body. A stupid tool. A childish tool.

A reminder.

Do not clench everything.

Keep the breath moving.

His foot slipped on moss.

Ghost Core flashed a correction too late.

Jin caught himself against a wall, nails scraping wood.

Ahead, the thin man stopped.

Jin froze.

The service lane held its breath.

A drop of water fell from a roof edge into a broken jar.

Plink.

The thin man slowly turned his head.

Jin pressed himself into the shadow between two stacked crates and let his body sag like discarded cloth.

Not hiding like a spy.

Hiding like trash.

That came naturally.

The man’s gaze moved over the crates, the jar, the damp wall, the strip of shadow. It passed across Jin’s robe and did not stop.

Then he turned away.

Jin did not breathe until the Ghost Core reminded him.

`[Oxygen deprivation counterproductive.]`

He exhaled silently.

The thin man entered an abandoned grain store.

Jin waited twelve breaths before crossing the lane.

The door had not closed fully. Through the crack, he saw darkness, sacks piled against one wall, and a pale square of light on the floor from a hole in the roof.

Voices.

The thin man spoke first.

“The subject detected the compound.”

Another voice answered from deeper inside.

Calm. Male. Educated.

“Before ingestion or after?”

“Before.”

A pause.

“Interesting.”

Jin’s blood felt colder than morning cliff stone.

The second voice continued. “Did he use a talisman?”

“No visible talisman.”

“Physician intervention?”

“Not before detection. The large servant is carrying a sample toward the lower paths.”

Jin’s fingers tightened.

Doksu.

`[Heart rate spike.]`

Jin forced his hands open.

The calm voice did not sound worried. “Allow it. The physician girl will confirm enough to frighten herself and not enough to understand.”

“The sect elder?”

“Baek is a complication.”

Jin heard paper unfold.

“We expected Host-03 degradation after contact with the Furnace relic. Instead, he walks, adapts, and detects a Skyless-grade softening agent before ingestion.”

Host-03.

The words struck harder than any fist Kang had thrown.

Not boy.

Not disciple.

Not Jin Ryu.

Host.

Number three.

Jin tasted iron though he had not eaten the rice.

The thin man lowered his voice. “Should we retrieve him?”

“Not yet. Premature retrieval risks exposing the old chamber. The sect still believes its mountain is merely haunted by tradition. Let the subject mature inside pressure.”

Pressure.

As if suffering were a furnace temperature.

As if humiliation, hunger, broken bones, sealed meridians, and poison were not cruelty but cultivation conditions.

The calm man continued. “Notify Director Yeom that Host-03 is responsive. Increase observation. Avoid lethal quantities unless adaptation plateaus.”

Jin’s vision narrowed.

Director Yeom.

A name.

Not a shadow. Not a guess. A name.

`[Memory anchor created: Director Yeom.]`

The thin man asked, “What about Kang Taejun?”

“He remains useful.”

“For pressure?”

“For stupidity.”

Jin almost smiled despite himself.

Then the thin man said, “Kang’s people may act beyond instruction. The Cho boy injured his hand. His pride is unstable.”

“Good. Instability produces revealing conditions.”

Good.

A person had tried to poison Jin’s breakfast, watched his friend carry evidence, discussed using bullies as tools, and called all of it good.

Something inside Jin shifted.

Not rage.

Rage was hot. Rage made fists shake and vision blur. Rage was what Kang wanted from people weaker than him because rage stepped forward without thinking.

This was colder.

This was a shape forming around rage.

A vessel.

He needed to know more.

Jin leaned closer to the door crack.

The floorboard under his left foot sighed.

Not cracked.

Not loud.

But enough.

Inside, the voices stopped.

`[Detection likely.]`

Jin moved before the warning finished.

The grain store door burst outward.

The thin man came through first with a short knife held reverse-grip, low and close to the wrist. Not a kitchen knife. Not a thug’s blade. A professional little thing made for quiet work.

Ghost Core painted three lines across Jin’s vision.

Throat.

Belly.

Inner thigh.

All killing lines.

Jin stepped back.

Too slow.

The knife kissed his outer robe and opened cloth above his ribs.

`[Evasion incomplete.]`

“I noticed.”

The thin man’s eyes widened a fraction.

“You heard.”

Jin did not answer.

The man advanced.

No anger. No shout. No wasted breath.

That frightened Jin more than Kang ever had.

Kang fought like a hammer thrown by pride. This man fought like a needle held by someone who had forgotten hands could tremble.

A second figure moved inside the grain store, but did not emerge.

The calm voice spoke from the darkness. “Do not damage the cranial site.”

Cranial site.

Behind the ear.

Implant wound.

The thin man adjusted his angle.

`[Attack pattern recalculating.]`

Blue lines bloomed.

Too many.

Jin’s body could not follow them. His ankle still hurt. His ribs burned. His right knee did not trust the ground. The perfect dodge Ghost Core suggested would tear the sealed lattice in his hip.

Chapter 7 returned to him like Baek’s laugh in the wind.

Do not calculate what a normal disciple should do.

Calculate what this broken body can survive.

The knife came toward his belly.

Jin did not retreat straight.

He folded badly.

Ugly.

Crooked.

Broken Star Step.

His left foot slid into a shallow puddle. His weight dropped early. The knife passed over his ribs instead of into his gut, slicing cloth and skin in a hot line. Jin bit down on a grunt and slammed his shoulder into the stacked crates beside him.

The crates toppled.

Not at the thin man.

At the broken jar.

The jar shattered.

Water, clay, and noise exploded across the lane.

A servant shouted nearby.

The thin man cursed for the first time.

Jin ran.

Not well.

Not heroically.

He ran like a person who knew exactly how close his blood was to spilling.

`[Route: laundry wall. Crowd density increasing.]`

Jin obeyed.

The thin man pursued for seven breaths, then stopped.

Professionals hated witnesses more than missed chances.

Jin rounded the laundry wall and stumbled into a group of servant women carrying wet robes. One cursed him. Another slapped the back of his head. A third saw blood on his side and went silent.

“Fell,” Jin gasped.

“Into a knife?” she asked.

“Sharp wall.”

“You are a terrible liar.”

“Training.”

He kept moving before she could decide whether compassion was worth trouble.

His ribs screamed.

The cut was shallow, but fear made it feel deeper. Ghost Core monitored blood loss with insulting calm.

`[Laceration: superficial. Infection risk: moderate.]`

“Good. I was worried my morning lacked variety.”

`[Sarcasm detected.]`

“High efficiency?”

`[Low.]`

Jin almost smiled again.

Then he remembered Doksu.

The lower herb path.

He changed direction.

`[Recommended destination: Medicine Pavilion.]`

“I am going.”

`[Current path inefficient.]`

“Doksu is on it.”

`[Ma Doksu has low combat capability.]`

“I know.”

`[High loyalty.]`

The words landed strangely.

Jin slowed for half a heartbeat.

High loyalty.

Ghost Core measured muscles, poison, balance, threat angles. It spoke of people like variables. But somehow, in its cold inventory of the world, Ma Doksu’s foolish stubborn kindness had become data.

High loyalty.

Jin ran faster.

He found Doksu near the old herb terraces, cornered by two Outer Yard disciples wearing Kang’s colors tied around their wrists.

Not Cho. Not Min.

New dogs from the same kennel.

One held Doksu by the collar. The other slapped his pockets.

“What are you hiding, pig?”

“Snacks,” Doksu said breathlessly.

The disciple slapped him.

Doksu’s head snapped sideways.

Jin stopped at the edge of the path.

For a heartbeat, all calculation vanished.

The disciple raised his hand again.

Jin picked up a stone.

Ghost Core flashed warnings.

`[Direct confrontation not recommended.]`

Jin threw.

The stone struck the slapper’s wrist with a crack.

The disciple yelped and spun. “Who—”

Jin was already moving.

Not toward them.

Toward the side, where the old herb terrace wall had collapsed into a slope of loose soil.

“Broken dog,” the collar-holder spat. “You want to die too?”

Jin breathed through the pain in his side.

Two disciples. Stronger than him. Angry. Careless. No knives visible. One bruised wrist. One holding Doksu too close.

`[Victory probability in direct combat: 12%.]`

“Not fighting.”

`[Clarify.]`

Jin kicked the collapsed wall.

Loose soil slid.

Not enough.

He kicked again, harder, pain sparking through his ankle.

The slope gave way.

Mud, stones, and old herb roots poured across the narrow path.

The disciple holding Doksu lost footing first because he was leaning backward. Doksu, to his credit or panic, dropped his entire weight.

Both of them crashed down.

The second disciple lunged at Jin.

Ghost Core painted a perfect counter.

Jin ignored it.

He threw the cork.

It struck the disciple in the eye.

For one magnificent heartbeat, the world paused in respect for stupidity.

Then the disciple screamed.

Doksu scrambled free, clutching his robe.

“Run!” Jin shouted.

Doksu ran.

Jin followed.

Behind them, the two disciples cursed, slipped, and crashed into each other on the mud-slick path.

Master Baek would probably call it disgraceful.

Jin considered it one of his cleaner victories.

They reached the Moonlit Medicine Pavilion with mud up their legs, blood on Jin’s side, a poisoned rice sample in Doksu’s robe, and no cork.

Han Seolha opened the door before they knocked.

Her expression moved from annoyance to alarm in the time it took to see Jin’s face.

“What happened?”

Doksu bent over, gasping. “Breakfast.”

Seolha stared.

“Breakfast stabbed you?”

“Poison,” Jin said.

Her eyes sharpened.

Doksu fumbled the cloth-wrapped sample from his robe and held it out like an offering to a very dangerous goddess.

Seolha took it without touching the rice directly. She smelled it once, and her face hardened.

“Inside.”

The same word as before.

The same cold urgency.

This time, Jin had not swallowed the poison.

This time, that made Seolha more afraid, not less.

She laid the rice on a black lacquer tray, sprinkled a gray powder over it, and watched the grains turn faintly blue around the edges.

Doksu whispered, “Is blue good?”

“No.”

“Blue is often pretty.”

“This blue is murder.”

Doksu nodded rapidly. “Then I hate blue.”

Seolha added one drop of clear liquid.

The grains smoked.

A sharp iron smell filled the room.

Jin’s stomach tightened.

Seolha stepped back.

“Meridian Softening Compound,” she said.

Ghost Core answered in his skull at the same time.

`[Confirmed.]`

Seolha looked at Jin. “You did not eat?”

“No.”

“How did you know?”

The question hung between them.

Doksu looked from Seolha to Jin. Fear had made him quieter. But not blind.

Jin could lie to enemies easily because enemies expected lies. Lying to people who had carried him, hidden him, healed him, and gotten slapped because of him felt like swallowing a stone.

“I smelled metal,” he said.

Seolha’s gaze did not soften.

“That compound is nearly odorless under steam.”

“Nearly is not fully.”

“You expect me to believe your nose is better than my reagent kit?”

“No.”

The room went still.

Doksu stopped breathing loudly.

Seolha’s hand drifted toward the needle case at her waist. Not as a threat. As habit. As comfort.

Jin touched the side of his head behind his left ear.

The wound had mostly closed, but under the skin something cold seemed to listen.

“I cannot explain all of it,” he said.

Seolha’s voice lowered. “Can you explain any of it?”

Jin reached into his robe and removed the cloth-wrapped fragment from beneath Black Needle Cliff.

The room temperature seemed to drop.

Seolha did not touch it.

“What is that?”

“A piece of the same nightmare.”

Doksu looked at the fragment, then at Jin. “Should I stand farther away?”

“Yes,” Seolha said.

“No,” Jin said.

Doksu stood exactly where he was, suffering between instructions.

Seolha fetched a jade tray marked with containment symbols from a locked cabinet. She made Jin place the fragment on it himself. The moment it touched the tray, the Ghost Core pulsed behind his ear.

`[Relay contact established.]`

Jin stiffened.

The fragment emitted no light.

The tray’s carved symbols darkened.

Seolha whispered, “Impossible.”

“What?”

“These containment marks are for corrupted qi artifacts. It is reacting as if the object is alive enough to refuse being named.”

Doksu took half a step back.

Jin heard something.

Not with his ears.

A broken sound, like a voice speaking through water and stone.

`[…Host…zero…three…status…]`

His vision flickered.

For one heartbeat, the Medicine Pavilion vanished.

He saw a white room with no windows.

A woman screaming without sound.

A child floating in black liquid.

Silver needles entering skin.

A man in a red official robe observing through glass.

Then the image broke.

Jin stumbled.

Doksu caught his arm. “Jin!”

Seolha seized his wrist and checked his pulse.

The second rhythm hammered beneath the first.

“What did you see?”

Jin swallowed.

The room was back. Medicine shelves. Lantern light. Seolha’s hand on his wrist. Doksu’s terrified face. The poisoned rice smoking faintly on the tray.

“A room,” Jin said. “White. A woman. A child. Needles.”

Seolha’s fingers tightened.

“Your mother?”

“I do not know.”

Lie.

He did know.

Not with certainty. With a wound deeper than certainty.

Some part of him had recognized the shape of the woman’s fear before the Ghost Core could give it a name.

Aera.

His mother.

The name Master Baek had spoken like a regret.

The fragment pulsed again.

`[Unauthorized relay ping blocked.]`

Jin gripped the table.

“Something is trying to contact it.”

Seolha went very still.

“You are sure?”

“Yes.”

“How?”

Jin closed his eyes.

There was the question again.

How?

Because a voice lived in his skull. Because an impossible relic had buried itself behind his ear. Because his body had been written into a prison before he was old enough to know the shape of a lock. Because strangers called him Host-03 and discussed whether pressure would make him mature.

Because his life had never belonged only to him, and now the thing inside him wanted data.

He opened his eyes.

“I heard them today.”

Seolha’s expression changed.

Not surprise.

Dread.

“Who?”

“A man with clean shoes. Another man in an abandoned grain store. They called me Host-03.”

Doksu whispered, “I hate that name.”

Jin continued. “They said Director Yeom should be notified. They said Kang is useful. They said not to retrieve me yet.”

Seolha sat down slowly.

It was the first time Jin had seen her look her age.

Not the untouchable cold physician of Moonlit Medicine Pavilion. Not the sharp-tongued woman who could insult death into waiting outside. Just a young woman holding too many pieces of a dead master’s warning and realizing the warning had already found her door.

“Director Yeom,” she said.

“You know him?”

“No.”

But her eyes had moved toward the locked drawer where Healer Yun’s notebook rested.

Jin noticed.

So did Ghost Core.

`[Deception probability: 71%.]`

Jin did not call her out.

Not yet.

Seolha stood and opened the drawer. She removed the notebook, flipped past coded diagrams, burial prescriptions, and meridian sketches until she found a page sealed with dried red wax.

She broke it.

Her face paled as she read.

Doksu leaned toward Jin. “Is this the part where someone explains things?”

“No,” Jin said. “This is usually the part where things become worse.”

Seolha read aloud.

“‘If the name Yeom appears near the sealed children, abandon ordinary medicine. Skyless Directorate protocol uses layered tests: poison, pressure, isolation, affection, betrayal. They do not simply kill subjects. They measure what suffering unlocks.’”

The room became very quiet.

Affection.

Betrayal.

Doksu looked sick.

Jin felt the words enter him like needles placed with perfect skill.

Poison, pressure, isolation.

He knew those already.

Affection and betrayal were more frightening.

Because they required people.

Seolha closed the notebook.

“The poison was not an assassination.”

Jin nodded. “It was a test.”

Doksu’s voice shook. “Testing what?”

Jin looked at the poisoned rice, then at the relay fragment, then at the faint reflection of his own face in Seolha’s medicine tray.

A thin, scarred boy.

Broken Pulse Dog.

Host-03.

Whatever else they had named him before he could speak.

“Whether I would die,” he said. “Whether I would notice. Whether I would ask for help. Whether help would come.”

No one answered.

Outside, the sect bells rang the start of morning training.

Ordinary disciples would be gathering in the yards, complaining about sore muscles, boasting about forms, sneering at those below them. Kang Taejun would stand among them with clean robes and arrogant eyes, useful in his stupidity. Somewhere, a thin man with clean shoes would be reporting failure. Somewhere beyond the sect, Director Yeom might be reading the name Host-03 from a strip of paper and smiling.

Jin reached for the poisoned rice sample.

Seolha caught his wrist. “Do not touch it.”

“I need it.”

“For what?”

“To become difficult to test.”

Her eyes sharpened. “That is exactly the kind of thought that gets people killed.”

“I am already on that road.”

“Then stop walking faster.”

Jin looked at her hand around his wrist.

It was firm. Warm. Alive.

Affection, the notebook had said.

Betrayal.

The Skyless Directorate measured both.

His first instinct was to pull away from everyone before they could be used against him. That instinct felt safe, which meant it was probably one of the traps.

Jin forced himself not to move.

“I will not swallow it,” he said. “But if they use poison, I need to know poison. If they use Kang, I need to know Kang. If they use fear, hunger, pity, friendship—”

Doksu flinched.

Jin stopped.

The words had cut where he had not meant them to.

He turned to his friend.

“I am sorry.”

Doksu looked down at his hands.

For a moment, the foolishness drained from him completely.

“If I am a weakness,” Doksu said quietly, “then get stronger faster.”

Jin had no answer.

Seolha released his wrist.

“I can make a neutralizer,” she said. “Not a cure after ingestion. A detection wash. You touch suspected food with it. If the compound is present, the food darkens. It will not catch every poison.”

“Better than nothing.”

“It requires ghostvine ash.”

“Where is that?”

“Restricted storage.”

“Of course.”

“And I cannot request it without a reason.”

Doksu lifted one hand weakly. “Can we say we are poisoning rats?”

Seolha looked at him.

Doksu lowered his hand. “Bad plan.”

Jin thought of the waste armory, the broken tools, the old storerooms no one cared to guard properly. He thought of kitchen ash, cliff moss, Baek’s wine cups, old medicine cupboards, and the way servants knew a sect’s hidden paths better than its proud disciples.

“Tell me what ghostvine ash looks like,” he said.

Seolha frowned. “No.”

“I can find things.”

“You can get killed finding things.”

“I almost got poisoned eating things.”

“That is not an argument.”

“It is a pattern.”

Doksu sighed. “I preferred when our biggest problem was Kang being ugly inside.”

Seolha pinched the bridge of her nose.

Then she took a small brush, wrote three characters on scrap paper, and pushed it toward Jin.

“Ghostvine ash is gray with a green shine under lamplight. It smells like wet copper. If the jar has a red seal, do not open it. If the jar has a black seal, do not breathe near it. If the jar whispers, leave the building.”

Doksu stared. “Jars whisper?”

“In this sect? Sometimes.”

Jin folded the paper.

Seolha pointed at him. “You are not stealing from restricted storage today.”

“I know.”

“You are going to rest.”

“I know.”

“You are lying.”

“I know.”

For the first time that morning, Seolha looked genuinely tempted to stab him with a needle for educational purposes.

Instead, she cleaned the cut on his side, wrapped it tight, and gave Doksu a packet of powder.

“For shock,” she said.

Doksu accepted it. “For Jin?”

“For you.”

He blinked. “Oh.”

“Drink it with water before you faint on my floor.”

“Thank you, Lady Han.”

“Do not thank me. You are bleeding from the lip because someone slapped you.”

Doksu touched his mouth as if surprised his body had followed the plot.

Seolha treated that too.

Jin watched in silence.

The Ghost Core gave no comment. No warning. No calculation.

For once, the quiet inside his skull felt almost respectful.

When they left the Medicine Pavilion, the morning had fully arrived. Sunlight touched the sect roofs. Training shouts rose from the yards. The world looked unchanged, which made everything worse.

Jin and Doksu walked side by side along the lower path.

Doksu kept glancing at him.

“What?” Jin asked.

“Host-03 sounds stupid.”

Jin looked ahead. “Yes.”

“You need a better secret name.”

“I do not need a secret name.”

“You already have too many. Broken Pulse Dog. Jin Ryu. Host-stupid-three.”

“Host-03.”

“I refuse accuracy.”

Despite himself, Jin smiled.

Doksu saw it and looked relieved, which made Jin’s chest hurt more than the knife cut.

At the fork near the practice yard, Doksu stopped.

“Jin.”

“Hm?”

“If someone tries to use me against you…”

Jin’s smile vanished.

Doksu scratched his cheek and looked away. “I am not saying abandon me. That would be rude. But maybe do not tell me everything. I am bad at secrets. My face leaks.”

Jin studied him.

Doksu was afraid.

Not of Jin.

For Jin.

With Jin.

Because of Jin.

The Skyless Directorate measured affection.

Jin wondered if they understood how heavy it was.

“I will tell you enough to stay alive,” Jin said.

Doksu nodded. “That sounds like a terrible friendship, but better than a dead one.”

“It is temporary.”

“Good. Because when you become terrifying, I expect better food.”

“You think terrifying people eat well?”

“They should. Otherwise what is the point?”

Jin looked toward the training yard.

Across the packed earth, Kang Taejun stood with a circle of disciples around him. His hand rested on the hilt of his practice sword. His robes were clean. His hair was tied perfectly. He laughed at something one of his followers said.

Then his gaze moved.

It found Jin.

The laugh faded.

For the first time, Kang did not look at him like a dog.

He looked at him like a stain that had not washed out.

Useful, the Skyless Directorate had called him.

For stupidity.

Jin felt no satisfaction from the insult. Kang’s stupidity could still break bones. Stupidity, when backed by rank, money, and permission, was a kind of weapon.

Ghost Core marked Kang’s posture.

`[Hostile attention detected.]`

“I know.”

Kang smiled.

Not with humor.

With promise.

A disciple beside him whispered something. Kang’s eyes dropped briefly to Jin’s bandaged side, then to Doksu’s split lip.

He knew something had happened.

Maybe not enough.

Enough to be dangerous.

A bell rang again.

Training began.

Jin stepped onto the practice yard with dried mud still on his sandals, a knife cut under his robe, poison knowledge in his sleeve, a relay fragment hidden under Seolha’s containment tray, and a new name burning behind his ribs.

Host-03.

He did not know what Host-01 and Host-02 had been.

He did not know whether they had survived.

He did not know whether his mother had saved him, hidden him, doomed him, or all three.

But he knew this:

The people watching him did not want a dead boy.

They wanted a result.

Jin lowered his stance as the instructor shouted the first form.

Pain flared through his sealed meridians.

Ghost Core measured it.

Jin breathed through it.

Across the yard, Kang watched.

Above the yard, somewhere behind walls and false names, the Skyless Directorate watched.

Jin let them.

A dog that knew it was being watched could learn where the leash began.

And one day, where to bite.
