date: "2026-06-22"

The cliff did not care who had written a prison into Jin Ryu's body.

That was why he went there.

Black Needle Cliff stood behind the northern storehouses, past the ash garden, past the old punishment yard, past a row of leaning stone lanterns no one lit anymore. The sect did not forbid disciples from approaching it. That would have made the cliff important. Instead, the Obsidian Meridian Sect treated the place the way it treated useless things: it left it alone until someone bled on it.

The cliff rose from the mountain like a broken blade.

Black stone teeth jutted from its face, narrow and cruel. Mist coiled beneath them in slow gray sheets, hiding the drop. Wind climbed from below and screamed through every crack as if the mountain were breathing through broken ribs. There were old stories about the place. A drunk outer disciple who had vanished without a scream. A thief tied to a stone needle overnight, leaving only rope by morning. Sword marks cut into the rock by an ancestor who had trained there until his feet split open.

Jin believed only the wind.

Wind could not flatter him. Wind could not call cruelty discipline. Wind could not pretend a locked body was a moral failure.

It pushed.

He either remained standing or he did not.

That honesty was kinder than the sect.

The first dawn after Han Seolha told him someone had written a prison into his meridians, Jin stood before the cliff with old bruises under his robe, a wound behind his left ear sealed into a dark line, and three needle points along his body throbbing like tiny accusations. His ribs still protested when he breathed too deeply. His left shoulder pulled whenever he raised it too high. The back of his skull burned whenever the thing inside his head measured the world without asking permission.

He took Master Baek's cork from his sleeve.

It looked ridiculous in his palm. Brown. Chewed. Sour with old wine no amount of washing could remove. A scrap fit for a drunk elder's floor, not a training tool.

Baek's lazy voice returned to him.

*Put it between your teeth when your fear gets louder than your feet.*

At the time, Jin had thought the old man was mocking him.

Now he was less certain.

He placed the cork between his teeth.

The wind struck.

His whole body flinched. His right foot scraped half an inch across loose gravel. One pebble clicked over the edge, bounced once, then vanished into the mist.

Jin froze.

`[Environmental instability detected.]`

The Ghost Core's voice was clean and cold behind his thoughts.

`[Surface: uneven black volcanic stone. Wind pressure: variable. Fall risk: severe. Current host condition: unsuitable for cliff-edge training.]`

Jin stared at the ridge ahead. In some places it was no wider than two palms. Old cracks cut across it like black veins. Rain from the night before had left beads of water in the grooves, waiting to punish careless feet.

"Good," he muttered around the cork.

`[Clarify response.]`

He removed the cork just enough to speak. "If I can stand here, I can stand in the yard."

`[Conclusion unsupported. Combat terrain differs from cliff terrain.]`

"People pushing me differ from wind pushing me?"

`[Yes.]`

Jin almost laughed. His ribs stopped him.

He put the cork fully back between his teeth.

The cliff asked its only question.

*Can you remain standing?*

Jin inhaled through his nose.

In.

Hold.

Out.

The cork kept his jaw from chattering. Its rough surface pressed against his tongue, crude and ordinary, forcing him to notice his own breath instead of the open air beside his foot. The old Jin had tried to meditate the way proper disciples did, spine straight, palms settled, breath smooth as silk. His body had always turned those attempts into coughing, pain, and shame.

The cork did not ask for elegance.

It only asked him not to bite through it.

`[Breath interval inconsistent.]`

Jin ignored the voice.

`[Right knee tremor detected.]`

He lowered his weight by the smallest amount.

`[Correction incomplete.]`

The wind pushed from the left.

His foot wanted to slide. Instead, Jin moved his hip first. Not beautifully. Not properly. His shoulder dipped too far, his knee bent too late, and any instructor in the Outer Yard would have clicked his tongue at the disgrace of it.

But his sole stayed on stone.

Ten breaths.

Twenty.

Thirty.

Sweat gathered at his temples despite the cold. His calves shook. The old weakness spread through his limbs like wet cloth. It was always like this. The first moment of effort offered hope. The next reminded him hope collected payment.

A normal disciple's body warmed as qi moved.

Jin's body jammed.

`[Containment lattice pressure increasing at lower dantian gate.]`

His eyes narrowed.

"Show me," he said.

`[Visual overlay may worsen headache.]`

"Show me."

The world gained another skin.

Thin blue traces appeared across his vision, running from chest to abdomen, hips to thighs, thighs to feet. Most lines were broken. Some ended against red-black knots. Around his lower belly, where a martial artist's dantian should have gathered and circulated qi, a dark geometric lattice pulsed like a cage seen beneath water.

Seolha had drawn it with ink.

The Ghost Core showed it inside him.

Jin forgot to breathe.

His left foot slipped.

Stone disappeared beneath his heel.

`[Fall risk: critical.]`

He bit down hard enough to hurt his jaw. Pain snapped through him. He threw his weight forward, slammed one palm against the cliff wall, and dragged his foot back onto the ridge while stone tore skin from his hand.

A handful of gravel went over the side.

One breath.

Two.

Then, far below, he heard the faintest scatter of stones striking rock.

There was a bottom, then.

That should have comforted him.

It did not.

Jin dropped to one knee and spat blood from where his teeth had cut his lip.

`[Training method inefficient.]`

"Better than dying ignorant."

`[Ignorant hosts possess higher survival probability when avoiding lethal terrain.]`

"Then you chose the wrong host."

`[Correction: host selected by external activation sequence. Choice unknown.]`

Jin went still.

The wind moved around him.

"External activation sequence," he repeated.

`[Confirmed.]`

"My blood?"

`[Partial trigger.]`

"What was the other part?"

Silence.

Not absence. Jin had begun learning the difference. The Ghost Core's silence did not feel like a person refusing to answer. It felt like a locked door measuring whether opening would collapse the house.

`[Data sealed.]`

"By who?"

`[Data sealed.]`

Jin looked down at the red thread hidden beneath his sleeve.

"My mother?"

The cliff wind cried through the black needles.

`[Data sealed.]`

Not an answer.

Worse than one.

Aera had tied the thread around his wrist when he was too young to understand why her hands trembled. He remembered warmth. Rain. The smell of smoke in her hair. Her voice close to his ear.

*If the world calls you broken, do not argue with it. Survive long enough to prove it afraid.*

He had thought it was comfort.

Now it sounded like preparation.

His wounded palm left a red smear on the cliff wall.

Jin stood again.

Slowly.

The cliff waited.

The Ghost Core advised rest, water, stable-ground breathing, and avoidance of additional cranial stress.

Jin placed the cork between his teeth.

Then he stepped.

One pace.

Pause.

Breathe.

One pace.

Pause.

Breathe.

He did not try to imitate sect footwork. Beautiful movement belonged to people whose bodies obeyed them. He had watched outer disciples perform Crane Crossing Mist and Seven-Point Turning Leaf until envy became another organ in him. Their sleeves floated. Their heels kissed earth. Their qi ran through open channels like water through polished bamboo.

When Jin tried the same forms, the cage inside him rattled.

So he stopped asking his body to be beautiful.

He asked it to survive.

His right foot slid forward. His left hip angled inward. His shoulder lowered before the wind struck. The movement was cramped and ugly, but a half-finger shift meant the difference between stone and mist.

By the time the servant bell rang, Jin had crossed only twelve paces along the cliff and back.

His legs trembled when he reached level ground.

His palm bled.

His robe stuck to his back with sweat.

`[Result: negligible martial improvement.]`

Jin removed the cork.

"I am still standing."

For a moment, the Ghost Core said nothing.

`[Statement accurate.]`

Jin tucked the cork away and started back toward the waking sect.

Behind him, deep below Black Needle Cliff, a point of cold metal caught the first light.

Jin did not see it.

But the Ghost Core did.

`[Anomalous signal detected.]`

He stopped. "What signal?"

The answer came late.

`[Signal lost.]`

Jin stared into the mist until the servant bell rang a second time.

Nothing stared back.

The second dawn came colder.

His body punished him for returning to ordinary life as if extraordinary pain excused nothing. He hauled water until the buckets cut crescents into his fingers. He scrubbed storage jars until old mold lodged beneath his nails. He carried firewood to kitchens where disciples complained the steam was weak, the broth was thin, and servant boys were slower every year.

Cho watched him all morning with his wrapped knuckles held close to his chest.

Min whispered beside the water yard.

Kang Taejun did not appear.

That worried Jin more than if he had.

Dogs barked before biting.

Snakes did not.

At dinner, Ma Doksu dropped half a steamed bun into Jin's bowl without looking at him.

Jin stared at it.

"Why?"

"Because you look like a corpse that lost an argument with another corpse," Doksu said.

"That sounds generous."

"It is not. If you die, they will make us clean your mat."

Jin tore the bun in half. It was warm enough that steam touched his fingers.

Doksu kept his eyes on his own bowl. "Kang's people asked where you are assigned tomorrow."

Jin paused.

"Who?"

"Min. Cho. One of the tally clerks."

"Old Clerk Han?"

"New face. Thin. Clean hands. Smelled like ink and wintergrass."

Jin remembered the false clerk near the Medicine Pavilion. Soft steps. Soft voice. Questions with no dirt under them.

He swallowed the sudden dryness in his throat.

Doksu glanced at him. "You know something."

"No."

"Too quick."

"I know many things. Most are useless."

"Then know this useful thing." Doksu lowered his voice. "Kang is smiling again."

Jin's appetite faded.

Doksu pushed rice around his bowl. "When Kang smiles after losing face, someone else bleeds."

Across the hall, two boys turned away too quickly.

"Thank you," Jin said.

Doksu snorted. "I did not warn you. I complained about future work."

"Of course."

"And Jin."

The big servant boy finally looked at him. Doksu's face was simple in the way a locked gate was simple.

"Whatever strange thing you are doing, do it faster."

Jin slept badly.

Every time he closed his eyes, he saw the lattice inside his abdomen. Black lines. Red knots. Blue pathways blocked before they could become rivers.

Near midnight, he dreamed Aera sat across from him in the Starless Furnace. Her hands were covered in ink. Rain dripped from her sleeves onto black metal. She opened her mouth to speak.

The Ghost Core's voice came out.

`[Data sealed.]`

Jin woke before dawn with his nails dug into his palm.

The cut from the cliff had reopened.

He wrapped it, took the cork, and went out before anyone stirred.

Black Needle Cliff waited with frost on its teeth.

Today the wind was harder. It struck from below, vanished, then returned from the side as if changing its mind out of spite. Jin stepped onto the ridge and understood at once that yesterday had only introduced him.

Today the cliff was hungry.

`[Training discouraged.]`

"I know."

`[Wind pressure exceeds previous session by thirty-seven percent.]`

"I know."

`[Host slept insufficiently.]`

"I know."

`[Pain tolerance is not structural integrity.]`

That one made him stop.

The Ghost Core was right.

Pain was not proof of strength. Enduring stupidity was still stupidity. If he died here, Kang would not become kinder, Seolha would not learn the truth, and whatever Aera had hidden would remain buried beneath someone else's boot.

Jin removed the cork from between his teeth.

"Can you simulate a route?"

`[Define objective.]`

"Cross twelve paces and return without falling."

`[Additional combat conditions?]`

"No."

`[Generating movement pattern.]`

Blue lines spread over the ridge. Points marked stable stone. Arcs connected one foothold to another. A ghostly figure made of pale light stepped across the cliff ahead of him.

It was beautiful.

That was the first warning.

The simulated Jin moved like an inner disciple raised by patient masters. His spine stayed aligned. His shoulders softened at the exact moment wind struck. His weight flowed from one foot to the next with no hesitation, no locked channel, no old damage, no invisible hand tightening around his dantian.

It was the kind of movement Jin had spent years watching from behind water barrels.

The kind his body had never once allowed him to own.

`[Optimal route generated.]`

For a moment, hunger overcame caution.

Not hunger for food.

Worse.

Hunger for the body he should have had if no one had stolen it from him.

Jin placed the cork back between his teeth and followed the phantom.

First point.

Good.

Second.

Pain flashed, but he endured it.

Third.

His hip turned.

A red knot inside his lower abdomen pulsed.

Fourth.

The phantom asked his left meridian channel to open and receive weight.

His real body refused.

The containment lattice tightened.

It felt as if hooks had sunk into his belly and pulled inward.

Jin staggered.

`[Correction: shift weight to rear foot.]`

He tried.

His rear foot was already sliding.

`[Correction: engage lower spine.]`

He tried.

His lower spine locked.

`[Correction: rotate ankle seventeen degrees.]`

He tried.

Pain drove through his ankle like a nail hammered into bone.

The cliff tilted.

The perfect phantom continued ahead of him, beautiful and useless.

Jin's left foot left the stone.

For one breath, he hung between mountain and mist.

His right hand clawed out. Fingernails scraped black rock. His shoulder slammed into the cliff wall hard enough to empty his lungs. He caught a crack with three fingers.

Below him, mist opened like a mouth.

`[Fall risk: critical.]`

Jin could not answer.

His feet kicked empty air. His fingers slipped. One nail tore loose, and white pain exploded through his hand.

Then the phantom disappeared.

New lines appeared.

Shorter.

Crooked.

Ugly.

`[Emergency route generated.]`

A blue mark glowed to his right: a thumb-sized notch in the rock.

Too far.

`[Reach probability: twenty-two percent.]`

Jin laughed without breath.

Twenty-two percent was more than most of his life had offered.

`[Use pain response. Bite.]`

The command made no sense until it did.

He bit the inside of his cheek hard enough to taste blood.

Pain burst bright in his skull.

For one instant, panic broke.

He saw the notch.

Not as salvation.

As a place his broken body could reach if he stopped trying to move like the phantom.

Jin twisted wrong.

His shoulder collapsed inward. His hip jerked. His spine bent around the locked places instead of trying to force them open. Any instructor would have beaten him for it. Any proper disciple would have called it disgraceful.

His right foot slammed into the notch.

The impact nearly broke his toes.

But it held.

He pushed.

His fingers screamed.

His forearm dragged across stone, leaving skin behind.

He rolled onto the ridge and lay facedown on frost, gasping like a thing the mountain had spat back out.

The cliff did not praise him.

It simply failed to kill him.

For a while, that was enough.

When he could sit up, blood ran from his hand, cheek, and shin. His shoulder felt full of glass. The wound behind his ear burned in rhythm with his heartbeat.

`[Optimal route failure cause identified.]`

Jin laughed weakly. "Me."

`[Incorrect.]`

He opened one eye.

`[Failure caused by incompatibility between standard movement model and host containment lattice.]`

"That means me."

`[Incorrect. Host structure differs from assumed model.]`

"Still sounds like me."

`[Refinement: movement generated for unsealed martial body. Host is sealed martial body. Optimal model invalid.]`

Jin stared at the perfect path that had nearly killed him.

Then he looked at the ugly notch that had saved him.

"Stop calculating what a disciple should do," he said.

`[Clarify alternative.]`

He wiped blood from his mouth with the back of his hand.

"Calculate what I can do."

The Ghost Core went silent.

Jin wondered if it understood insult. Or humiliation. Or the cruelty of being shown perfection and nearly dying because part of him still wanted to borrow it.

Maybe it did not.

Maybe that was why he needed to teach it.

`[New parameter accepted.]`

Blue lines appeared again.

They were shorter now. Less elegant. Some ended suddenly and reappeared at crooked angles. Several movements looked like stumbles arranged with care. One route asked him to lower his shoulder before stepping. Another told him to let his right knee collapse slightly. Another marked a foot placement so ugly that the wind would strike his thigh instead of his chest.

It was not martial grace.

It was survival with structure.

Jin did not continue training.

Even he was not foolish enough to mistake near death for repeatable progress.

Instead, he sat on the frozen stone until dawn stained the mist pale and studied the ugly lines until they stopped looking like failure.

By the time he returned to the servant yard, his limp was impossible to hide.

Cho noticed first.

The other boy was sweeping one-handed, his injured knuckles wrapped in fresh cloth. His eyes brightened when he saw Jin's bloodied fingers.

"Look at that," Cho called. "The dog tried to climb something and lost."

Three servants turned.

Min leaned against a post with a bowl in his hand.

Jin kept walking.

Cho stepped into his path.

"Where were you?"

"Cleaning."

"With your face?"

Laughter scattered through the yard.

Jin felt the Ghost Core stir.

`[Threat: minor.]`

Minor.

A week ago, Cho had not felt minor.

That should have pleased him.

It did not. Pain made his temper thin.

Cho saw the change in his eyes and mistook it for fear. "Careful. If you keep crawling around like that, people might think you are trying to become human."

The insult was not clever.

It was old.

That made it worse.

Dog. Trash. Broken Pulse. Sect burden. Human only when work needed hands.

For years, Jin had swallowed those names because spitting them back changed nothing. Now each one sounded less like truth and more like someone else's attempt to keep a lock from rattling.

He looked at Cho's wrapped hand.

"How is the basin?"

The yard quieted.

Cho's smile twitched.

Min stopped eating.

Jin stepped around him.

Not fast. Not smooth.

A slight turn of the hip. A crooked foot. A path too ugly for Cho's shoulder to predict.

Cho turned too late.

For one heartbeat, his face held only confusion.

Not fear.

Not yet.

Just the expression of a man who had kicked the same door for years and discovered it had moved.

Jin walked toward the kitchen sheds.

Behind him, Cho spat. "Dogs can learn tricks."

Jin did not turn.

But he carried the words all day like extra weight.

The third dawn came with rain.

Not heavy rain. Worse. A thin needling drizzle that made stone slick and cloth cold. Fog pressed against Black Needle Cliff until the world became edges, wet stone, and hidden drops.

A sensible person would have stayed away.

A normal disciple would have trained beneath a roof.

Jin had never had much use for normal.

He stepped onto the ridge with his left hand wrapped, right ankle bound, shoulder bruised, lip split, and cork between his teeth.

`[Training in rain increases fatality risk.]`

"I know."

`[Host recovery incomplete.]`

"I know."

`[Recommend postponement.]`

The wind struck.

His foot slid.

His body remembered falling.

For one terrible breath, fear rose so violently that the mist below seemed to open again. His torn nail throbbed. His shoulder remembered empty air. His stomach tightened around the lattice.

The Ghost Core said nothing.

Maybe it had learned.

Maybe it was waiting.

Jin breathed through his nose.

In.

Hold.

Out.

The cork tasted of wine, rainwater, and blood.

He lowered his weight unevenly. Even balance was for bodies with honest pathways. He let his right side carry more. His left knee bent less. His shoulder dipped before the wind arrived. The shape was wrong.

Carefully wrong.

Step.

Pause.

Breathe.

Step.

Slide.

Correct.

The overlay appeared, but he had changed the question.

No perfect phantom walked ahead of him.

Only reachable marks appeared: red for routes that demanded too much from sealed meridians, gray for possible but costly, blue for ugly and survivable.

He followed blue when he could.

He used gray when he had to.

He avoided red.

Halfway across the ridge, voices rose from the upper path.

Jin froze.

Laughter cut through the wind.

Cho.

Min.

And Hwan, Kang Taejun's narrow-eyed shadow, the kind of boy who laughed half a breath late so important people knew he agreed with them.

Three figures appeared through the rain above him.

They should not have been there.

No one came to Black Needle Cliff by accident.

Cho saw him and grinned. "There he is."

Min whistled. "Our dog grew ambition."

Hwan crouched near the upper ledge and picked up a pebble.

Jin stood below them on a wet ridge, twelve paces from safety, mist beneath his left side and cliff wall to his right.

Bad position.

`[Threat assessment updated: moderate.]`

Only moderate.

Jin almost smiled around the cork.

Cho noticed it. "What is that? Did you steal food from a rat?"

Min laughed.

Hwan tossed the pebble. It struck the wall above Jin and bounced away.

Jin did not move.

Cho's amusement thinned.

Bullies did not only enjoy fear. Fear told them where they stood in the world. When a victim stopped giving the proper shape, they did not feel defeated. They felt robbed.

Cho picked up a larger stone.

"Dance," he said.

Jin removed the cork and tucked it into his sleeve.

Rain slid from his hair into his eyes. "What do you want?"

"To watch." Cho spread his hands. "We heard the Broken Pulse Dog was learning footwork. Show us."

Hwan lifted the stone.

The Ghost Core marked his wrist angle.

`[Projectile path prediction available.]`

Jin looked at the distance to level ground.

Seven paces.

In rain.

With three enemies above him.

With one ankle bound.

With a body built like a locked gate.

No good route.

Only usable ones.

He placed the cork back between his teeth.

Cho's face darkened.

Hwan threw.

The stone came fast, not at Jin's head but at the ridge before his leading foot.

Smart.

Or coached.

It struck wet stone and shattered. Shards skittered beneath Jin's sole.

The old Jin would have fallen backward.

The perfect phantom would have lifted weight and turned like an immortal in a painting.

Jin did neither.

He broke sideways.

His left shoulder dropped. His right foot dragged instead of stepping. His hip folded around the locked point in his lower body. The move was cramped and too low and disgraceful by every training-hall standard.

The shards passed under him.

His sandal caught the next blue mark.

He remained standing.

Cho stopped smiling.

Min blinked.

`[Threat pattern recorded. Survival route recalculating.]`

Hwan grabbed another stone.

"Again," Cho snapped.

The second throw came for Jin's knee.

Not to bruise.

To buckle.

Jin saw the line before the stone moved. The Ghost Core drew the arc, but he did not obey it blindly. He felt the wind first. Felt rain under his sole. Felt the cage in his abdomen tightening as if angry that he was still trying.

He stepped into wrongness.

Not away.

Into it.

The stone clipped his robe and struck the cliff behind him.

His ankle screamed.

White pain flashed through his vision.

But his body had folded beneath the path instead of rising above it.

Three paces left.

Min cursed. "He's cheating."

Cho's eyes turned mean.

"How does a dog cheat at falling?"

He picked up a flat piece of black stone with both hands.

Jin saw the intention before the throw.

Not one projectile.

Scatter.

If the stone struck the wall above him, fragments would spray across the whole ridge. No clean dodge. No single line. Nothing to calculate except pieces.

`[Warning: evasive probability below safe threshold.]`

Jin's heartbeat slowed.

Fear did not vanish.

It narrowed.

Through rain and mist, he saw not Cho but the prison inside his body. Lines written by someone who had expected him to live small. Bow small. Fail early. Die before asking why.

He heard Seolha.

*Someone wrote a prison into your body.*

He heard Baek.

*When your fear gets louder than your feet.*

He heard Aera.

*Survive long enough to prove it afraid.*

Cho threw.

The black stone hit the wall and shattered.

The Ghost Core flooded Jin's sight with lines.

Too many.

Red. Gray. Blue. White.

A migraine stabbed behind his left eye.

The system tried to calculate every shard, every angle, every possible correction.

Jin rejected it.

"Less," he hissed around the cork.

`[Clarify.]`

"Only what kills me."

The world simplified.

Most fragments vanished from the overlay.

Three remained bright: one toward his throat, one toward his ankle, and one narrow space where his body had to exist if he wanted to live.

Jin moved.

No.

He broke.

Right foot sliding instead of lifting. Left knee collapsing halfway. Shoulder turning inward. Spine bending like a reed that had never grown straight.

A fragment cut his cheek. Another tore his sleeve. The one aimed at his ankle struck where his foot had been half a breath ago. The one aimed at his throat passed through rain and empty air.

Jin landed on the final blue mark twisted so badly any instructor would have called it shameful.

His toes gripped stone.

His breath held.

Then he was past the narrowest part.

Two paces.

One.

Level ground.

Jin stepped off the ridge.

For a moment, only rain spoke.

Cho stared down at him from the upper ledge.

The expression on his face was not fear.

Not exactly.

It was the crack before fear entered.

Jin removed the cork from his mouth. Blood had soaked one end.

He looked up.

"I am still standing."

Cho's mouth twisted. "You think that means something?"

Jin's legs shook so badly he nearly answered by falling over.

He did not.

"Yes," he said.

Min grabbed Cho's sleeve. "We should go."

Cho shook him off, but he did not throw another stone.

That was its own answer.

Hwan kept staring at Jin's feet.

Not his face.

His feet.

Good.

Let him look. Let him report. Let Kang Taejun hear that the Broken Pulse Dog did not fall when stones rained on him.

Danger would come from that.

But danger had already been coming.

At least now it would have to come toward a moving target.

The three boys withdrew through the mist, their laughter dead before they reached the path.

Jin waited until their footsteps faded.

Then he dropped to one knee.

Pain returned all at once. His ankle throbbed. His cheek bled. His left eye pulsed with bright pressure. The wound behind his ear burned in time with his heart. The lattice in his abdomen tightened as if the prison wanted to punish him for discovering the shape of one crack.

He spat red into the rain.

`[Movement pattern successful under hostile projectile condition.]`

Jin laughed once. It came out broken.

"That was success?"

`[Host remained standing.]`

The words struck him harder than they should have.

Host remained standing.

A small thing.

A servant boy on a wet cliff had not fallen. No elder would record it. No hall would award contribution points. No disciple would chant his name. If the sect wrote a history of that morning, Jin Ryu would not appear in it.

But inside him, something shifted.

Not the prison.

Not yet.

Something beneath it.

A decision.

For years, he had believed strength meant becoming like the people who beat him. Smooth. Clean. Powerful. Untouchable.

Now, kneeling in rain with blood on his cheek and cork in his hand, he understood the first true lesson his broken body had ever taught him.

He did not need to move like them.

He needed a way to move that made their rules miss.

Jin forced himself upright.

"Record it."

`[Clarify command.]`

"The step."

`[Movement sample incomplete.]`

"Record it anyway."

`[Technique classification requires repeatable structure.]`

Jin looked back at the wet ridge, at the shards scattered across it, at the path his body had taken by refusing to be beautiful.

"Then call it provisional."

Three breaths passed.

Then blue text appeared across his vision, clean and merciless against the rain.

`[Provisional technique registered: Broken Star Step.]`

Jin stared at the words.

Broken.

Star.

Step.

The name was ridiculous.

It was ugly.

It was his.

Below the cliff, half-hidden behind mist and rain, the cold metallic glimmer appeared again.

This time, Jin saw it.

A point of light deep in the gray.

Too steady to be water.

Too sharp to be stone.

Then it vanished.

The Ghost Core spoke before he could ask.

`[Anomalous signal reacquired.]`

Jin wiped rain and blood from his cheek.

"What is it?"

`[Signal profile resembles dormant Ghost Core relay fragment.]`

The words meant almost nothing to him.

They meant everything to the part of him that had stopped believing in coincidence.

The bells rang for morning drill behind him.

Ahead, somewhere beneath Black Needle Cliff, something connected to the Ghost Core waited in the gray.

His ankle screamed.

His head throbbed.

His body shook.

Jin smiled anyway.

Not because he was happy.

Because the prison had walls.

And now one wall had a crack.
