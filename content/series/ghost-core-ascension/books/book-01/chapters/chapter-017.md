The message looked too thin to carry a life.

Black wax. Six words. A copied strip from a death report old enough for its paper to yellow at the edges.

That was all.

Yet it bent the air inside the Moonlit Medicine Pavilion until even breathing felt like pressing a thumb into an old bruise.

**Expose Host-03. Receive your master's truth.**

Han Seolha stood at the low treatment table with the paper beneath her fingers. The lantern above her burned steady, but its light seemed afraid to touch her face. Jin Ryu watched the shadow of her lashes lie motionless against her cheeks. He had seen her hands stay steady while silver needles entered swollen meridians. He had seen her cut poison from flesh without once flinching. He had seen her stare down Kang Taejun's people with a physician's smile sharp enough to split bone.

Now one thin slip of paper had made her still.

Ma Doksu did not speak. For once, even his stomach was silent.

Master Baek sat by the open window with his blind eyes turned toward the night. The old man's wine gourd rested unopened on his knee. That, more than anything, told Jin how dangerous the room had become.

Outside, the sect bells finished their evening count.

Three notes.

Then silence.

Seolha's thumb moved over the name half-hidden by her palm.

Yun Saja.

Her dead master.

The man whose notebook had warned her to hide the boy with sealed stars before the Skyless Directorate came.

Jin felt the Ghost Core stir behind his left ear, a cold pressure like a finger touching the inside of his skull.

**Text recognition available. Cipher reconstruction probability: seventy-four percent.**

No.

The pressure paused.

**Instruction unclear.**

Do not read what she is covering.

A thin line of pain passed through his temple.

**Information withheld by allied party may influence host survival probability.**

Jin lowered his eyes.

She has a right to choose what to show me.

For a moment, the Ghost Core said nothing.

Then the blue characters sank into the darkness behind his eyes.

**Restriction acknowledged. Allied privacy boundary registered. Temporary.**

Temporary.

Even the machine had to be taught that a wound was not a locked door waiting to be opened.

Seolha finally lifted her hand from the death report fragment. The paper had wrinkled beneath the pressure of her fingers.

"Everyone leave," she said.

Doksu looked at Jin.

Baek tilted his head.

Jin did not move.

Seolha looked up. Her voice was quiet enough to be mistaken for calm.

"I said leave."

Doksu opened his mouth, closed it, then bowed awkwardly. "Lady Han, if someone tries to stab you while we're gone, please scream before you die. I run faster when terrified."

No one laughed.

That seemed to make him feel worse. He shuffled out through the side door, broad shoulders hunched beneath his patched outer robe.

Baek rose more slowly. His cane clicked once against the floor.

"A hook works best when it has meat on it," he said.

Seolha did not answer.

"And grief," Baek added, "is meat the cruel never fail to smell."

"I know."

"Knowing does not stop hunger."

"Neither does drinking." Her voice sharpened, but only for one breath. "Go, Master Baek."

The old man smiled without humor. "Good. Anger is better than emptiness."

He went out after Doksu.

Jin remained.

Seolha's gaze found him like a needle finding skin.

"You too."

"No."

Her eyes narrowed.

His ribs still hurt when he breathed. His body remained wrapped in Seolha's resin-treated bandages, and his legs carried the deep tremor of a man who had climbed one thousand hostile steps on a body designed to fail. He had no strength to resist her if she decided to drag him out by the ear.

So he did the only thing he could.

He stayed seated and let weakness become weight.

"If this is about me," he said, "I should hear enough to understand the price."

"The price is mine."

"The bait is mine."

"Do not dress surrender as nobility." Her voice cut low. "You are not offering yourself because you are generous. You are offering yourself because part of you still believes your life is a debt other people are allowed to collect."

That struck closer than any fist Kang had ever thrown.

Jin's first answer died in his mouth.

Seolha saw it. Her expression changed, not softening, exactly, but bending around something painful.

"Do not do that," she said.

"Do what?"

"Stand there like a sacrifice and call it kindness."

Jin stared at the paper. Six words. An old name. A wound wide enough to swallow both of them.

"Then do not stand there like a physician and call loneliness professionalism."

The room went cold.

Seolha's hand closed once.

For a heartbeat, he thought she would slap him.

Instead she laughed once under her breath.

It was a terrible sound. Not amusement. Not even bitterness. More like a bowl cracking cleanly down the center.

"You have become rude since the mountain decided not to kill you."

"I learned from everyone around me."

"Baek?"

"Mostly Kang."

This time the corner of her mouth almost moved.

Almost.

Then she looked back at the death report.

"My master did not die of old injury," she said.

Jin did not breathe.

Seolha's fingers smoothed the paper again, carefully now, as if gentleness could change what had been written there.

"That was the official record. Internal meridian collapse after years of poison exposure. A tragic end for a physician who spent his life treating injuries no sane doctor would touch." Her eyes did not leave the page. "I believed it because I wanted to believe there was at least one cruel thing in this sect that was not deliberate."

The lantern hissed.

"The fragment says his collapse pattern was external."

Jin understood enough to feel his stomach sink.

"Someone did it to him."

"Someone opened him," Seolha whispered. "Not with a blade. With needles. With a pattern similar to the prison in your body, but reversed. Whoever wrote this report saw signs of a forced extraction."

Behind Jin's left ear, the implant pulsed.

**Keyword correlation: extraction. Host series relevance: high.**

Jin forced his face still.

"They killed him for something he knew."

"Or something he hid." Seolha folded the paper along its old crease. "And now they are offering me the rest of the truth in exchange for you."

There it was.

Plain as bone.

Jin felt fear first.

Not betrayal. Not anger.

Fear.

Because he wanted to trust her, and wanting made the possible loss sharper.

He thought of the first night in the Medicine Pavilion, when she had placed needles into his torn meridians and told him not to die before she understood him. He thought of her blocking Kang's inspection with nothing but status and a voice too cold to ignore. He thought of her hands trembling only when Yun Saja's name appeared.

Everyone had a door.

The Skyless Directorate had found hers.

"What will you do?" he asked.

Seolha looked at him for a long time.

"I do not know."

The answer was worse than a promise.

A promise could be false.

This was honest.

Jin lowered his head once.

"Then choose after you know what you are paying."

Her eyes sharpened.

"Meaning?"

"If you expose me, expose a useful truth. Not a cheap one. Do not sell me for a copied scrap they may have written themselves."

"You are giving advice on how best to betray you?"

"I am giving advice on how not to get cheated."

For the first time that night, Han Seolha truly looked like she might break.

Then she turned away.

"Leave."

This time, Jin stood.

His legs protested. The Ghost Core offered a stabilization pattern. He denied it and used the edge of the table instead.

At the door, he stopped.

"Lady Han."

"What?"

"If you choose your master, I will not hate you."

She did not turn.

"Fool."

The word was soft.

"If I choose my master properly, I cannot sell the kind of boy he died protecting."

Jin's throat tightened.

Seolha added, colder now, "But do not mistake that for comfort. I am going to answer them."

The door closed between them.

Only after it shut did Jin realize his hand was shaking.

Doksu was waiting in the medicine courtyard with both hands folded behind his head and the expression of a man pretending not to listen while standing directly beneath a paper window.

Baek stood beside the herb rack, holding a bottle he had not opened.

Neither asked what happened.

That made Jin more grateful than he wanted to be.

"She sent you out," Doksu said.

"Yes."

"Good. She sent us out too. That means she hates all of us equally. Healthy physician behavior."

Baek chuckled. "The large one grows wiser."

"I grow hungry. Sometimes wisdom falls into the same bowl."

Jin leaned against a post.

The night smelled of wet stone and bitter herbs. Beyond the Medicine Pavilion walls, the Obsidian Meridian Sect slept uneasily. Jin could feel it now, not through some mystical sense, but through ordinary things he would once have ignored: the doubled footsteps near the outer path, the lamp that remained lit inside the Discipline Hall long after curfew, the way servants whispered his name and then lowered their voices when they saw him.

Broken Pulse Dog no longer meant what it used to.

That made it more dangerous.

**Risk environment elevated. Recommended action: maintain proximity to trusted allies.**

Jin almost laughed.

Trusted allies.

The machine made it sound simple.

Doksu watched his face. "You look like you swallowed a live coal."

"The message was for Lady Han."

"I guessed that from how the air stopped moving."

Baek tapped his cane once. "And?"

Jin hesitated.

"They offered her truth about Yun Saja in exchange for exposing me."

Doksu's expression changed.

The foolishness left it so completely that Jin saw, for a moment, why the large boy had survived years in a sect that fed on weakness. Doksu was not stupid. He simply refused to give cruelty the dignity of his full face unless necessary.

"Will she?" he asked.

Jin looked at the closed door.

"She said she does not know."

Doksu breathed out slowly.

"I hate honest answers."

Baek uncorked his bottle at last, sniffed it, then frowned and corked it again. "Good."

Jin looked at him. "Good?"

"People who swear too quickly in front of bait often already have the hook in their mouth." Baek turned his blind face toward the pavilion. "She did not know because she still has enough respect for her wound not to cover it with pretty words."

"That is supposed to comfort me?"

"No. Comfort makes boys careless."

Doksu raised a hand. "As the only boy here who enjoys comfort, I object."

Baek ignored him. "Listen carefully, Jin Ryu. If Han Seolha walks away tonight, do not follow her."

Jin stiffened.

"Why?"

"Because trust is not a leash."

The words entered quietly.

Then cut deep.

Baek's cane clicked again.

"The Directorate will expect you to watch her. They will expect fear to make you reach. If you reach, they learn the shape of your hand."

Jin understood.

If he used Ghost Core to track her, the Veil might detect the pattern. If he physically followed, he might be seen. If he ordered Doksu to follow, he would turn friendship into surveillance. Every path poisoned the relationship before Seolha made her choice.

"And if she betrays me?" he asked.

Baek's smile was tired.

"Then you will learn that survival sometimes includes heartbreak."

Doksu scowled. "That is terrible advice."

"It is true advice. The terrible kind usually is."

Jin closed his eyes.

Behind his left ear, the Ghost Core waited like a blade held still.

**Covert monitoring of allied party possible through residual implant resonance traces in treatment needles. Probability of undetected observation: sixty-one percent.**

No.

**Probability of betrayal event—**

No.

This time the denial rang hard enough to make his skull ache.

The characters disappeared.

For several breaths, there was only night.

Then Doksu's large hand landed on Jin's shoulder with careful heaviness.

"If she sells you," he said, "I will be upset."

Jin opened one eye.

"Only upset?"

"Very upset. Possibly impolite."

"You? Impolite?"

"I have hidden depths."

The absurdity helped.

Not enough.

But enough to let Jin breathe.

Seolha did not leave through the front door.

She waited until Jin's footsteps faded, then crossed to the medicine cabinet at the rear wall. The cabinet held dried ginseng, fever bark, clotting powder, low-grade qi stabilizers, and three jars of powders so bitter that even Master Baek refused to steal them. Behind the bottom drawer was a brass hook no patient knew existed.

She pulled it.

A panel opened without sound.

Inside lay Yun Saja's old case.

Not the notebook she had already shown Jin. That notebook had been meant to be found by a careful student. This case had been meant to stay hidden even from her.

Seolha had discovered it only three months after Yun Saja's death, when grief had made her clean the Medicine Pavilion until her fingers bled. She had not opened it often. Most days, knowing it existed hurt less than reading what it contained.

Tonight, she set the case on the table and unlocked it.

The smell of old paper rose like dust from a grave.

Inside were three things.

A roll of black-thread acupuncture diagrams.

A sealed pouch of silver needles wrapped in oilskin.

And a small wooden tablet carved with Yun Saja's final private rule.

**A physician must never confuse the patient with the secret inside the patient.**

Seolha touched the tablet.

"Master," she whispered, "they came."

No answer came from the wood.

She had not expected one.

That did not stop the child inside her from listening.

Seolha unfolded the black-thread diagrams. Each sheet mapped meridian points in a different ink color. Normal flow in blue. Blockage in red. Reversal patterns in black. The final sheet showed a body with seven strange knots drawn around the spine, heart, throat, and skull.

A containment lattice.

Not identical to Jin's.

Older.

Rougher.

At the bottom, Yun Saja had written a single note.

**If the cage is perfect, the boy dies. If the cage is broken too quickly, the thing inside wakes hungry. Therefore delay both death and awakening. Make him useless to the hunters. Let him hate weakness if he must. Hatred is lighter than harvest.**

Seolha read it twice.

Then a third time.

The boy.

Not a boy.

The boy.

Jin Ryu had not been a strange accident that resembled her master's old research.

Her master had known him.

Her master had helped hide him.

Her breath left her slowly.

The copied death report on the table suddenly looked smaller.

The Skyless Directorate was not offering her a truth.

They were offering her the shape of a wound and hoping she would rush toward it.

Still, the wound was real.

Yun Saja had been opened.

Someone had taken something from him.

Seolha lifted the oilskin pouch and untied it.

Inside were twelve silver needles darker than her own, their shafts etched with almost invisible grooves. Silent Meridian Needles. True ones. Not the training copies she had shown Jin.

A folded strip of paper fell from the pouch.

Her master's handwriting.

She almost did not open it.

Then she did.

**Seolha, if you are reading this, I failed to bury the past deeply enough. Do not chase my death before you understand my work. Revenge is a crude needle. It enters loudly and heals nothing.**

Her fingers tightened.

Below that was another line.

**If the black ring offers my truth, ask for the part they cannot know: the name I gave Host-02 when he cried.**

Seolha went very still.

Host-02.

The Directorate's bait had mentioned Host-03 because Jin was alive.

But Yun Saja's note knew there had been another.

A child who cried.

A child her master had named.

The copied death report in the black-wax message did not mention Host-02. It offered only injury pattern and a threat.

That meant one of two things.

Either the Directorate had only fragments.

Or the person contacting her was not fully trusted within the Directorate.

Both possibilities were dangerous.

Both were useful.

Seolha folded the note back along its creases.

Her face had regained its stillness now, but it was no longer emptiness.

It was decision shaped into skin.

She took a clean sheet and began to write.

Not a betrayal.

Not a refusal.

A hook.

The meeting place was the old Rain Cistern beneath the laundry terraces.

Every sect had places no one liked to clean. The Rain Cistern was one of them: a half-collapsed stone reservoir built to catch mountain water before the sect expanded upward. Now the drains clogged every spring, the lower steps grew moss thick enough to hide bones, and servants claimed they heard voices under the water when the moon was thin.

Seolha arrived during the hour before midnight wearing a plain gray outer robe over her physician's white.

She carried no lamp.

That was both caution and insult.

If her visitor needed darkness, he could provide his own.

The cistern smelled of wet stone, old algae, and rust.

At the bottom step, water reflected a strip of sky like a black blade.

"Lady Han," said a voice behind her.

She did not turn.

"Physician Lu."

A soft laugh. "You recognized me without seeing my face."

"Your medicine smells of cheap clove and dead ethics."

The man stepped from behind a pillar.

He wore the same physician's robe he had worn at Kang's banquet, though tonight the sect badge was absent. His hair was tied neatly. His smile was mild. His eyes had the careful patience of someone who enjoyed watching animals decide whether the cage door was open.

On the inside of his wrist, three broken rings marked the skin in faint black lines.

"Cruel," he said. "But accurate enough to interest me."

"You sent the message."

"I delivered an opportunity."

"Opportunities do not usually arrive wrapped in threats."

"Then your life has been unusually kind."

Seolha looked at him fully now.

"Say what you want."

Physician Lu's gaze flicked to her sleeves, her throat, the way her fingers rested empty at her sides. Searching for needles. Finding none visible. That made him more cautious rather than less.

Good.

"Three confirmations," he said. "No more. Provide them before dawn, and I will provide the full sealed report regarding Yun Saja's final procedure."

"Final procedure," Seolha repeated.

Something dark moved behind her ribs.

He smiled gently. "A more accurate term than murder."

She did not let her face change.

"The confirmations."

"First: Host-03's current pulse map after the Thousand-Step Trial. Second: the substance you intend to use to contaminate battlefield blood. Third: the precise location of the interface point."

Behind Seolha's calm, three thoughts struck like needles.

They knew about the blood contamination.

They knew Jin's trial changed his pulse.

They did not know the implant mark well enough to name it.

Not yet.

"And if I refuse?"

"Medicine Pavilion irregularity review begins at dawn. Your authority to treat the boy is suspended. His wounds are examined by a neutral physician." Lu spread his hands. "Neutrality is such a flexible word when written by powerful people."

"And if I accept?"

"You receive your master's truth. You continue treating Host-03 under Directorate observation. Perhaps, eventually, you gain a position suited to your talents."

"Selling patients is not my talent."

"No. Curiosity is." His smile sharpened. "That is why you came."

The water at the bottom of the cistern did not move.

Seolha reached into her sleeve.

Lu's fingers twitched.

She withdrew a folded paper.

"A pulse map," she said.

He did not take it immediately.

"Complete?"

"Complete enough to be useful. False enough to punish greed."

His brows lifted.

That, she decided, was satisfying.

"You admit deception before handing it over?" he asked.

"I admit I am not a fool. You offered me a copied fragment anyone with old ink and a corpse could forge. You want medical truth from me? Pay with medical truth first."

Lu's mildness thinned.

"You are not in a position to negotiate."

"I am a physician at a cistern with no witnesses, speaking to a man wearing no official badge while he requests illegal information about a sect disciple under active treatment." Seolha's voice remained pleasant. "If you wanted obedience, you would have come with soldiers. If you wanted secrecy, you need me alive. If you wanted precision, you should have respected my intelligence."

For a moment, only water breathed.

Then Lu laughed softly.

"Yun Saja raised a thorn."

"Yun Saja raised a physician. Thorns are simpler."

Lu held out his hand.

Seolha did not give him the pulse map.

"One question first."

"Ask."

"The name my master gave Host-02 when he cried."

The effect was small.

But Seolha had trained her life on small effects.

Lu's pupils tightened.

He knew Host-02 existed.

He did not know the answer.

Or he knew and had not expected her to know to ask.

Either way, the balance shifted.

"Where did you hear that?" he asked.

"From the dead, apparently." She folded the pulse map once more. "Answer."

Lu's smile did not return.

"Host-02 did not cry."

Wrong.

Seolha felt the old note in her sleeve like a second pulse.

**The name I gave Host-02 when he cried.**

She kept her face perfectly still.

"Then you do not have my master's truth."

"I have enough."

"Enough to bait me. Not enough to buy me."

His gaze cooled.

"Do you think loyalty will save the boy?"

"No."

"Then why protect him?"

Seolha considered the question.

A lesser man might have expected anger. A better one might have expected love.

Physician Lu expected leverage.

So she gave him truth, because truth was the one shape liars often misread.

"Because my master hid something from monsters," she said, "and until I know whether that thing is a weapon, a patient, or a child, I will not hand it back to them."

Lu studied her.

"And which is Jin Ryu?"

Seolha thought of Jin sitting on the treatment table with bruises across his ribs, advising her not to be cheated while offering himself as payment. She thought of him refusing Ghost Core's help to read a paper she had covered with her hand. She thought of the boy on the Thousand-Step Trial forcing his ruined body upward because someone else had decided where he was allowed to fall.

"All three," she said.

That answer irritated Lu more than refusal.

Good.

She extended the folded paper.

"Here is my counteroffer. A pulse map from before the trial, a list of substances any incompetent physician would use to contaminate blood, and three false interface locations. In exchange, you will delay Medicine Pavilion review until after the dawn assessment and deliver one verifiable line from the sealed report. Not the whole truth. One line."

Lu looked at the paper as if it were a snake.

"This is worthless."

"Then refuse."

"You misunderstand danger."

"No," Seolha said. "I diagnose it."

The cistern seemed colder.

Lu took the paper.

Not because it was valuable.

Because refusing would admit he had lost all control of the conversation.

"One line," he said. "Yun Saja died with eleven needles in his spine and one in his tongue."

Seolha's body wanted to react.

She did not allow it.

One in his tongue.

To stop speech.

Or to extract something from the meridian beneath it.

"That is an image," she said. "Not a verifiable line."

His mouth tightened.

"The official corpse examiner's private mark was a half-moon cut behind the right ear."

Seolha knew that mark.

Not from the official death report.

From the body.

She had washed her master's corpse.

She had seen the small half-moon cut and been told it was an old scar.

Her fingers did not move.

"Review delay," she said.

"Until noon. Not longer."

"After the dawn assessment."

"If he bleeds, all agreements end."

"If your people collect blood from a patient under my treatment, all agreements end."

Lu smiled again, and this time it showed teeth.

"The arena is a hungry place, Lady Han. It drinks what falls."

"Then I will make sure it chokes."

She turned to leave.

His voice followed her up the steps.

"You have not chosen him over your master. Do not comfort yourself with that lie."

Seolha paused.

Rain began to fall through the broken cistern roof, touching the black water below in small silver wounds.

"I chose my master," she said without looking back. "You simply forgot what kind of man he was."

Jin did not sleep.

He tried.

For three breaths.

Then pain, worry, and the Ghost Core's quiet readiness made the attempt insulting.

The guest cot in the side recovery room was narrow, but he had slept in worse places: grain sheds, kitchen corners, under stairways during winter, the floor beside Doksu when fever had made the larger boy snore like a dying ox. The problem was not the cot.

It was the space where trust had to sit while unanswered.

Doksu slept on the floor across the room with one arm flung over his face and one hand suspiciously close to a wooden stool he could use as a weapon. Every few breaths he muttered something about dumplings. Baek sat outside the door, pretending to nap while absolutely awake.

Jin lay on his back and stared at the ceiling beams.

**Rest deficiency will reduce dawn performance by twenty-three percent. Recommend induced micro-sleep.**

No.

**Emotional agitation identified. Suggested protocol: temporary suppression—**

No.

The word came out between his teeth this time.

Doksu snorted, rolled over, and mumbled, "Don't eat the red mushrooms."

Jin froze.

Nothing else happened.

The Ghost Core waited.

Jin closed his eyes.

It would be so easy.

Let the machine fold worry away. Let it cool the fear of Seolha's absence into usable distance. Let it turn trust into probability, affection into leverage, betrayal into damage forecast. He could become efficient. Calm. Hard to hurt.

Less human.

Seolha had warned him.

Baek had warned him.

Even his own aching skull had warned him.

A weapon that saved him by removing everything worth saving would one day look at his heart and call it unnecessary weight.

"No emotional suppression," he whispered.

**Restriction remains active.**

"No monitoring Lady Han."

A pause.

**Restriction remains active.**

"No prediction of whether she betrays me unless I ask."

Longer pause.

**Host command conflicts with survival optimization.**

"Register it anyway."

Pain flickered behind his ear.

**Registered. Conditional alert only: if allied party action directly threatens host's immediate physical survival.**

Jin released a breath.

That was not trust.

But it was a fence.

Sometimes fences kept monsters out.

Sometimes they kept monsters in.

He sat up carefully and reached for the cracked saber beside the cot. Keeper Wen had returned it through a servant just after sunset, wrapped in old cloth and carrying no message. The blade looked uglier in moonlight: short, chipped, stained along the edge, its crack running like a frozen lightning vein through the metal.

Jin placed it across his knees.

"Show me nothing new," he told the Ghost Core.

**Clarify.**

"No reconstruction. No hidden technique. No dead master's saber style. Only what I already practiced."

**Efficiency loss significant.**

"Good."

**Good?**

"If I cannot survive the dawn assessment with what I own, then anything more is borrowed debt."

The Ghost Core did not answer immediately.

Then:

**Training mode adjusted: owned-pattern reinforcement.**

Blue lines appeared in the dark behind his eyes.

Not commands.

Reminders.

Bucket weight shifts.

Floor-scrubbing kneel angles.

Kitchen kick avoidance.

Broken Star Step: crooked half-step, pressure exhale, shoulder misdirection.

Black Needle Cliff lesson: step where death is thinner.

Baek's insults appeared nowhere, but Jin heard them anyway.

*If you move like a genius, the fools will look up. Move like a stubborn idiot who tripped correctly.*

Jin almost smiled.

The motion pulled at his bruised cheek.

He practiced sitting down.

Not standing.

Standing would wake Doksu.

He moved his wrist one finger-width. The cracked saber angled. His shoulder remained loose. His breath moved low. The Ghost Core counted tremors and then stopped counting when he told it to.

One motion.

Then another.

Owned patterns.

Ugly patterns.

Human patterns.

At some point, Baek spoke from outside the door.

"If you cut my wall, I will make you pay in chores."

Jin lowered the saber.

"I thought you were asleep."

"I am blind, not dead, and certainly not trusting."

Jin hesitated.

"Did she leave?"

Baek's silence answered before his voice did.

"Yes."

Jin's grip tightened.

**Conditional alert threshold not met.**

He exhaled slowly.

"Do you know where?"

"Yes."

Jin looked at the door.

Baek did not continue.

"Are you going to tell me?"

"No."

"Because trust is not a leash?"

"Because you listen occasionally, and I wish to encourage the disease."

Jin leaned his head back against the wall.

"You are terrible at comfort."

"Again, comfort makes boys careless."

"What makes boys strong?"

Baek's cane tapped once outside.

"Choosing who they refuse to become."

Jin looked at the saber in his lap.

Behind his left ear, the implant pulsed like a second heartbeat.

He whispered, "I refuse to become a machine that survives alone."

The Ghost Core recorded the sentence.

It did not understand.

Not yet.

Seolha returned less than an hour before dawn.

Jin knew because Baek stopped pretending to sleep.

The pavilion door opened without its usual soft chime. Seolha must have caught the bell with her fingers to silence it. Her steps entered the hall, paused outside the recovery room, then continued toward the treatment chamber.

Jin remained seated.

Doksu woke instantly despite the fact that thunder would not have disturbed him ten minutes earlier.

"Food?" he whispered.

"Lady Han," Jin whispered back.

"Ah. Worse."

Baek's cane tapped from outside. "Both of you, stop whispering like thieves who failed school."

The door opened.

Seolha stood in the frame.

Her gray outer robe was damp from rain. Her hair had loosened at one side. She looked exhausted, pale, and sharper than before, as if some soft surface had been burned away during the night.

In her hand was a rolled paper sealed with her own white medicine mark.

Jin stood too quickly.

Pain bit his ribs.

Seolha's eyes flicked to the movement.

Physician first, even now.

"Sit," she said.

He sat.

Doksu slowly pushed himself upright. "Should I be here or very far away?"

"Here," Seolha said. "You are a witness."

Doksu straightened as if someone had promoted him to general.

Baek entered behind her, expression unreadable.

Seolha placed three papers on the table.

The first was the arena medical clearance.

The second was a treatment restriction addendum.

The third was folded inward so no one could read it.

Jin looked at them, then at her.

"You answered them."

"Yes."

Doksu swallowed.

Baek said nothing.

Seolha met Jin's eyes.

"I did not sell you."

The words entered the room and changed its shape.

Jin did not realize how tightly his chest had been held until it hurt to let go.

Seolha continued before relief could become easy.

"I also did not refuse completely. Refusal would have triggered review before dawn. They wanted a pulse map, our blood contamination method, and the implant location. I gave them a useless old pulse map, a list of obvious fake contaminants, and three false locations."

Doksu blinked. "So you lied."

"Professionally."

"Is that different from normal lying?"

"It uses cleaner ink."

Baek made a pleased sound.

Jin kept watching her.

"What did they give you?"

Seolha's face tightened for half a breath.

"One verifiable line. Enough to confirm the message was connected to my master's death. Not enough to trust them."

"What line?"

"Not now."

The answer was firm, but not a wall. A covered wound.

Jin nodded.

Seolha's fingers touched the folded third paper.

"They know about the resin bandages."

Baek's head turned slightly.

Doksu cursed under his breath.

Jin felt the Ghost Core pulse.

**Blood contamination countermeasure compromised.**

Seolha nodded as if she had heard it. "Which means we change the plan."

She unfolded the third paper.

It was not a pulse map.

It was an arena procedure clause, written in Seolha's narrow precise script and stamped with the Medicine Pavilion seal.

"By my authority as attending physician," she said, "all visible blood shed by Jin Ryu during the dawn assessment is to be considered medically contaminated by stabilizing compounds and unsuitable for disciplinary testing. Any official sample must be drawn from the inner elbow after the assessment, in my presence, using Medicine Pavilion instruments."

Doksu stared.

"You are telling them exactly where to take blood?"

"Yes."

"Why?"

Seolha reached into her sleeve and withdrew a small sealed packet.

Inside, Jin saw three thin patches of pale medicinal film.

"Because they will not use the official location if they think I prepared it. They will attempt to collect blood elsewhere during the fight. Ear, mouth, shoulder, old wound, torn bandage. Anywhere that feels like a clever theft." Her eyes cooled. "So we make every clever theft worse than useless."

Jin understood slowly.

"False contamination?"

"Layered contamination." Seolha placed the packet on the table. "Your arm bandages still contain bitter resin. Your collar will contain fever ash. The inside of your mouth will be rinsed with blue lotus salt, which ruins qi-stability readings for six hours. Behind your left ear—"

Jin went still.

Seolha noticed.

Her voice softened by one degree.

"Behind your left ear, I will place a fever blister patch. It will look like an infected bruise from impact. If they already suspect the interface point is there, it makes the location medically noisy. If they do not, it makes them avoid the area because infection ruins clean sampling."

**Countermeasure effective. Residual scan interference likely.**

Jin touched the skin below his ear.

The implant mark had been his most terrifying secret since the Starless Furnace.

Now Seolha was not just hiding it.

She was making it look too disgusting to touch.

Doksu nodded with solemn admiration. "Lady Han, you are frightening in a very clean way."

"Thank you."

"It was not completely praise."

"I accepted only the useful half."

Baek chuckled.

For a moment, the room breathed easier.

Then Seolha looked at Jin.

"There is more."

Of course there was.

"The assessment opponent has been changed."

Jin's hand found the edge of the table.

"To who?"

"Not Cho. Not Hwan. Not Rong Bei."

Doksu's face darkened. "Then Mu Sanchul."

Seolha nodded.

The name landed like a stone.

Mu Sanchul was not popular enough to be famous, not cruel enough to be feared like Kang, and not stupid enough to be trapped by simple pride. That made him worse. He had once been an inner-yard candidate before a tendon injury dragged him back down. He knew how to damage without looking like he meant to. He knew how to lose without losing face. He knew how to obey hidden orders and make them appear as bad luck.

Doksu rubbed his jaw. "Sanchul breaks joints. He calls it correcting posture."

"He also uses hooked strikes," Seolha said. "Small cuts. Ears, gums, scalp, knuckles. Blood without obvious cruelty."

Jin understood the shape of the trap.

The Directorate did not need him dead.

It needed him bleeding in public, under pressure, while Ghost Core either hid or reacted.

**Opponent profile requested.**

Jin did not deny it.

Not fully.

Only bounded it.

Owned patterns plus available verbal data. No memory stealing. No full reconstruction.

**Acknowledged.**

Lines appeared behind his eyes. Not enough to make Mu Sanchul into a solved puzzle. Enough to mark the likely shape of danger.

Hooked strikes.

Joint locks.

False stumble into elbow cut.

Crowd pressure.

Blood-seeking angles.

Jin breathed in.

Out.

"Can I win?" he asked.

Seolha answered first. "No."

Doksu winced.

Baek grinned.

Jin looked at her.

Seolha did not soften the word.

"Not cleanly. Not without revealing too much. Not without damaging your body beyond what I can repair before the next trap. Your goal is not victory. Your goal is to complete the demonstration assessment, avoid official disqualification, prevent clean blood collection, and make the crowd believe your survival is ugly enough to be natural."

"So I lose?"

Baek's cane tapped.

"Did you learn nothing yesterday?"

Jin thought of the old man's lesson.

Choosing defeat as a tool.

Not being assigned defeat as a leash.

He looked at Seolha.

"What does chosen defeat look like?"

Her expression changed.

Approval, maybe.

Or fear wearing approval's face.

"Like a weak disciple who refuses to fall where they want him to."

Doksu raised a finger. "I can cheer in a way that makes everyone think you are terrible but stubborn."

"That may be the most useful thing you have ever offered," Seolha said.

"Again, half praise. I am improving."

Jin picked up the cracked saber.

Its damaged edge caught lantern light.

"Lady Han."

She looked at him.

"Earlier," he said, "you said if you chose your master properly, you could not sell the kind of boy he died protecting."

Doksu looked between them.

Baek became very still.

Seolha's face did not move, but something behind her eyes trembled.

"Yes."

"Did you choose?"

Rain ticked against the window paper.

Seolha took the fever blister patch from the packet and held it between two fingers.

"I chose my master," she said. "And because I chose him, I chose not to mistake you for the secret inside you."

The answer settled into Jin more deeply than comfort.

Comfort soothed.

This strengthened.

He bowed his head.

Not as Broken Pulse Dog to a physician.

Not as Host-03 to an observer.

As Jin Ryu to Han Seolha.

"Then treat me like a patient who intends to be very difficult."

For the first time since the black-wax message arrived, Seolha smiled.

It was small.

Dangerous.

Real.

"Gladly."

The hour before dawn belonged to preparation.

Seolha worked quickly.

Blue lotus salt burned Jin's tongue until his mouth watered bitter. Fever ash dusted the collar beneath his robe, hidden where a blade might nick skin and ruin a stolen sample. The blister patch behind his left ear stung sharply when she pressed it down, then settled into a hot false throb that made the whole area feel inflamed.

"Do not scratch it," she said.

"I was not going to."

"Your face considered it."

Doksu leaned close, squinted at the patch, and recoiled. "That looks disgusting."

"Good," Seolha said.

"No, I mean very disgusting. If I saw that on a corpse, I would accuse the corpse of poor discipline."

"Better."

Baek circled Jin once, blind eyes half-lidded.

"You look like a boy held together by poor choices."

"Is that good?"

"Convincing."

Jin flexed his fingers.

The bandages pulled. Pain answered from his ribs, shoulder, thigh, and the deep places where the Thousand-Step Trial had scraped his meridians raw.

**Functional capacity: forty-eight percent. Emergency override remains available.**

No emergency override unless death is immediate.

**Define immediate.**

If my heart stops, you may argue.

**Definition medically suboptimal.**

Jin almost smiled.

Seolha noticed. "What?"

"The Ghost Core dislikes my definitions."

"Good. Make it suffer socially."

Doksu pointed at Jin. "See? Even the machine has better survival habits than you."

"I am beginning to think everyone in this room insults me as treatment."

"It is a low-cost method," Seolha said.

"And abundant," Baek added.

The absurd rhythm steadied him.

Then the first arena bell rang.

Low.

Metallic.

Hungry.

The room lost its fragile humor.

Seolha handed Jin the medical clearance.

Her seal gleamed white against the dark ink.

"This keeps me in the assessment space," she said. "Not beside you. Not protecting you. Watching. If I intervene without cause, they can remove me. If you collapse naturally, I can stop the match. If you pretend to collapse too perfectly, they will know."

"So I must nearly fail convincingly."

"Yes."

Doksu clapped his shoulder carefully. "You have trained your whole life for this."

Jin stared at him.

"That was encouragement," Doksu said.

"Was it?"

"Mostly."

Baek opened the door.

Cold dawn air entered the pavilion.

Mist covered the sect paths. The mountain peaks beyond the rooftops were still black against a pale iron sky. Lanterns burned along the road to the Outer Yard arena, their light blurred by fog. Voices drifted upward already: disciples gathering early, hungry for spectacle, wagers changing hands, rumors sharpening like cheap knives.

Broken Pulse Dog had survived the Thousand-Step Trial.

Now everyone wanted to see whether survival had been miracle, fraud, or beginning.

Jin stepped outside.

His body hurt.

His fear walked with him.

So did his friends.

Doksu on his left, large and scowling at anyone who stared too long. Baek behind him, tapping his cane as if the whole sect were an inconvenient hallway. Seolha on his right, white-robed and silent, carrying her medical case like a weapon more dangerous than steel.

The Ghost Core remained quiet.

Not absent.

Quiet.

For once, Jin appreciated the difference.

They descended toward the arena.

Outer Yard disciples turned as he passed.

Whispers rippled.

"He looks half-dead."

"He always looks half-dead."

"Thousand-Step Trial broke the stele."

"No, the stele was old."

"Kang arranged this, right?"

"Shut up if you like your teeth."

At the far side of the arena square, Kang Taejun waited beneath the Iron Moon Hall banner.

He wore formal outer-yard combat robes today, dark blue with silver edging. His hair was tied high. His face looked calm enough for ceremony, but his eyes found Jin's bandages, his collar, his mouth, and finally the patch behind his left ear.

For half a breath, Kang's gaze lingered.

Then moved away.

Not enough to expose knowledge.

Enough to prove interest.

Beside Kang stood Seo Yiran, hands folded into her sleeves, expression unreadable.

Behind them, half-hidden near a pillar, Physician Lu watched with the mild patience of a man counting drops before rain became flood.

Jin felt Seolha's presence at his side change by the smallest amount.

Not fear.

Recognition.

Lu's eyes met hers across the mist.

He smiled.

Seolha did not.

The Arena Clerk climbed onto the platform with a scroll in hand and a face that suggested he would rather be anywhere with fewer politics and more properly aligned forms.

"Dawn demonstration assessment," he announced, voice carrying over the square. "Subject: Jin Ryu, servant disciple, Broken Pulse status under review. Attending physician: Han Seolha of the Moonlit Medicine Pavilion. Assessment witness: Outer Yard Procedure Office. Combat correction assistant: Mu Sanchul."

A murmur moved through the crowd.

Mu Sanchul stepped out from the opposite gate.

He was lean, not tall, with narrow shoulders and a face too ordinary to remember. His right sleeve hung slightly looser than his left, hiding the old tendon injury everyone whispered about. He carried no weapon.

That made him more dangerous.

His eyes went to Jin's bandages.

Then to his mouth.

Then behind his ear.

Jin's skin crawled beneath the blister patch.

**Opponent confirms blood-targeting intent.**

I know.

**Recommended—**

Owned patterns only.

The Ghost Core stopped.

Then displayed three quiet lines.

**Protect mouth.**

**Protect left ear.**

**Fall badly, not falsely.**

Jin breathed out.

At the platform edge, Seolha accepted the physician's marker stone from the clerk. It gave her the right to stop the assessment once.

Only once.

Baek leaned near Jin's shoulder.

"Remember," the old man murmured, "if you look brilliant, you die later. If you look pathetic, you may live long enough to become troublesome."

Doksu cupped his hands around his mouth and shouted, "Jin Ryu! Try not to embarrass the people who already think you are embarrassing!"

Several disciples laughed.

The laugh eased the crowd's hunger into mockery.

Mockery was safer than awe.

Jin stepped into the arena.

The sand was cold beneath his shoes.

Mu Sanchul bowed with perfect shallow courtesy.

"Junior Brother Jin," he said. "I will correct your posture gently."

Jin bowed lower.

Weak. Respectful. Almost clumsy.

"Senior Brother Mu," he said, "please correct only what I can afford to lose."

A few more laughs.

Mu Sanchul's smile did not reach his eyes.

Above the arena, dawn broke thin and gray over the Obsidian Meridian Sect.

The clerk raised his hand.

Physician Lu watched.

Kang watched.

Seolha watched.

The Ghost Core waited behind Jin's left ear like a silent algorithm holding its breath.

The hand fell.

"Begin."
