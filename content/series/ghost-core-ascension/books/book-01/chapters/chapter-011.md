Jin Ryu did not dream after the Thousand-Step Trial.

Dreams belonged to sleeping people.

What held him beneath consciousness was colder than sleep: a room made of scattered light, silent enough to hear pain think.

He stood barefoot on a black floor that reflected no face. Above him, pale blue characters drifted in circles too large to read, their edges cracked like old bones. Every few breaths, something struck the darkness beyond the room.

A knock.

A pulse.

A signal.

Each impact drove a thin thread of pain through the back of his skull.

`[Host neural activity unstable.]`

The Ghost Core's voice came from everywhere and nowhere. It should have sounded the same as always: cold, flat, exact. Yet inside the darkness, it carried a faint distortion, like a blade dragged across stone.

`[Memory bleed detected.]`

Jin tried to speak.

His mouth did not exist.

The black floor trembled.

A second pulse struck the dark.

This time, an image opened in front of him.

A woman knelt beside a low table. Her hair was loose. Her hands were covered in blood, but her face was calm in the way cliffs were calm before rain split them apart. A child lay upon the table, too small to understand knives, too quiet to understand mercy.

The woman pressed two fingers to the child's chest.

"Forgive me," she whispered.

Jin knew that voice.

He had heard it once before in the trial stair, buried beneath pressure and lightning pain.

Aera.

His mother.

The image shuddered.

A man's voice answered from outside the memory, old and shaking.

"Lady Jin, if you seal him this deeply, the boy may never cultivate."

"Good."

"He will be mocked. Beaten. Treated as trash."

The woman's hand trembled once.

Only once.

"Good," she said again, and this time the word sounded like a rib breaking inside her chest. "Let the world mistake him for trash. Better trash than harvest."

The child on the table moved.

Jin felt pain in places he did not have.

The memory broke.

The dark room folded inward.

`[External relay echo attempting classification.]`

A blue symbol appeared above Jin: three rings inside a starless circle. He did not know it. He hated it anyway.

`[Countermeasure active.]`

"What is that?" Jin tried to ask.

This time his voice returned, but it came out thin and distant.

The Ghost Core answered too slowly.

`[Designation fragment recovered: Host-03.]`

Jin looked up.

The dark beyond the room looked back.

Then pain opened his eyes.

The real world struck him in pieces: lamp smoke, bitter medicine, wet cloth on his forehead, the sour iron taste of blood between his teeth, the hard bed beneath his back, and Han Seolha's voice cutting through everything like a needle through infected flesh.

"Hold him down. If he tears that channel again, I will personally break the other leg to keep him still."

"That seems harsh," Ma Doksu said somewhere nearby.

"His body survived a thousand pressure steps and then tried to keep standing out of spite. Harsh is the language it understands."

"Fair."

Jin tried to lift his head.

The ceiling spun away from him.

A hand pushed his shoulder back down before the room could finish tilting. It was not gentle, but it knew exactly where not to press.

"Do that again," Seolha said, "and I will put you to sleep with a needle."

Jin blinked until her face stopped splitting into three.

She stood over him in pale Medicine Pavilion robes stained at the cuffs with dried red and black powder. Her hair had been tied in a quick knot, but loose strands had escaped around her face. Her eyes were calm.

That was how Jin knew she was furious.

Doksu hovered behind her like a worried mountain. He held a bowl of water in both hands, though half of it had already splashed onto his sleeves.

"You are alive," Doksu said, as if reporting a miracle to himself. "I checked twice."

"You checked by poking his cheek," Seolha said.

"Softly."

Jin tried to answer, but his throat scraped like sand.

Doksu lunged forward with the bowl.

Seolha snatched it before he drowned the patient.

"Small sips," she said.

The first sip tasted like smoke, roots, and humiliation.

Jin swallowed anyway.

Pain dragged itself awake in his body. His palms burned beneath thick bandages. His right knee throbbed with a deep, swollen heat. His ribs pulsed with every breath. His back felt as if someone had poured molten lead along his spine and let it cool wrong.

Behind his left ear, the implant mark prickled.

`[Diagnostic summary available.]`

Jin kept his eyes on Seolha.

Not now.

`[Noted.]`

The fact that the Ghost Core could answer an unspoken refusal should have bothered him more.

It did bother him.

He simply did not have spare strength to show it.

Outside the paper windows, voices moved through the courtyard. Too many voices. Too late at night.

"How long?" Jin rasped.

"You collapsed before sunset," Seolha said. "It is almost midnight."

"Trial?"

"Completed. Officially recorded. Publicly witnessed. Impossible to erase without insulting three elder seals, the pressure stele, and half the Outer Yard who lost money betting against you."

Doksu's face lit with savage satisfaction. "Elder Kang's people looked like they swallowed hot ash."

"Doksu," Seolha said.

"Right. Patient room. Quiet."

He lowered his voice to a whisper that was somehow still too large for the room. "But they did."

Jin closed his eyes for one breath.

Completed.

The word should have filled him with triumph.

Instead, it sat inside him like a stone.

He remembered the stele cracking beneath his bloody hand. He remembered a signal moving through the mountain. He remembered the Ghost Core saying relay response detected.

He opened his eyes.

"Who saw the stele?"

Seolha's mouth tightened.

"Everyone."

Doksu looked down at the bowl.

Jin understood.

The thousand steps had not made him safer. They had made him visible.

As if summoned by the thought, a sharp knock struck the outer door.

Doksu stiffened.

Seolha turned her head.

A Medicine Pavilion assistant slid the door open just wide enough to bow. Her face was pale.

"Senior Healer Han. Elder Kang Taejun waits outside with two Discipline Hall recorders. He requests confirmation of Jin Ryu's condition and access to the trial injuries for official documentation."

"No."

The assistant froze. "Senior Healer?"

"A patient under emergency care is not a public scroll for old men to read. Tell him no."

The assistant swallowed. "He says the trial result may involve formation irregularity."

Seolha's smile did not reach her eyes.

"Then tell Elder Kang that if his formation irregularity is bleeding internally, feverish, and one bad breath away from channel collapse, he may apply for inspection after I finish keeping it alive."

The assistant bowed so low her forehead almost hit the floor and vanished.

Doksu exhaled.

"Can she say no to Elder Kang?"

"I already did."

"Can she survive saying no to Elder Kang?"

Seolha looked at him.

Doksu coughed. "I will guard the door."

"You will not fight anyone."

"I will guard it emotionally."

He shuffled outside.

The door closed.

Silence pressed into the room after him.

Seolha waited until his footsteps settled near the threshold. Then she leaned over Jin and placed two fingers against the pulse point below his jaw.

Her voice changed.

Not softer.

More dangerous.

"What happened on the thousandth step?"

Jin stared at the ceiling.

"I touched the stele."

"Do not waste breath lying with half-truths. I felt the formation wake from the base of the stair. So did Master Baek. So did every elder with enough cultivation to hear stone remember something it should not know."

Stone remember.

The phrase matched the short draft of fear that had formed in Jin before he collapsed.

"The stele scanned me," he said.

"The stele scans every disciple who finishes. It records pressure tolerance, pulse stability, and inner force reaction. Yours did something else."

Jin turned his head toward her.

The movement made sparks flash white at the edge of his vision.

"What?"

Seolha removed her fingers from his pulse and lifted a small tray from the bedside table. On it lay a broken sliver of black stone no longer than a fingernail. Blue veins crawled faintly beneath its surface, fading and brightening like a dying insect.

"This came loose from the completion stele after you collapsed."

Jin's breath stopped.

The Ghost Core spoke immediately.

`[Material match: pre-sect relay lattice. Similarity to Black Needle Cliff fragment: 87.4%.]`

Jin did not move his eyes.

Seolha watched him watch nothing.

"The second pulse is speaking, isn't it?"

"It says the stone is old."

"I knew that without your ghost whispering in your skull."

She set the tray down and crossed her arms.

"Try again."

Jin almost smiled.

The attempt hurt too much, so he stopped.

"It says the stele fragment matches the piece under Black Needle Cliff."

Seolha went very still.

For a moment, the whole pavilion seemed to listen.

Then she turned away and walked to the inner wall.

Jin had seen that wall before. It held neat shelves, herb drawers, needle cases, clean cloth, and scroll boxes marked with boring medical labels. Seolha ignored all of them. She knelt by a low cabinet beneath a rack of dried lunar fern and pressed her thumb against three different knots in the wood.

A hidden panel clicked.

Jin tried to sit up again.

Pain stopped him before Seolha needed to.

"Stay down," she said without looking back.

"Secret cabinet?"

"All good medicine pavilions have at least one."

"For illegal herbs?"

"For illegal truths."

She drew out a narrow lacquered box wrapped in yellowing talisman paper. The paper was old enough that the red ink had faded brown at the edges. Three seals crossed the lid. One was Medicine Pavilion standard. One belonged to no sect authority Jin recognized.

The third had been cut in half.

Seolha placed the box on the table between them but did not open it.

Her fingers rested on the lid.

They looked steady.

Her breathing did not.

"My master left this," she said. "Physician Yun Saja. Officially, he died of fever after overworking himself during winter sickness. Unofficially, he spat black blood for three days after treating a patient he refused to name."

Jin said nothing.

The name Yun Saja had appeared before, in Seolha's warnings, in her closed expression, in the way she carried grief like a hidden blade.

"He taught me," she continued, "that a physician should never fear blood. Blood is honest. It tells you what the mouth cannot. But during his last month, he stopped trusting even blood records. He wrote in codes. He burned patient ledgers. He made me memorize antidotes for poisons no one in the sect admitted existed."

Her thumb pressed harder against the box.

"Two days before he died, he sealed this and told me not to open it unless I found three signs."

Jin forced his voice through the tightness in his throat.

"What signs?"

Seolha looked at him.

"A second pulse that did not belong to qi. A black-metal residue in a wound. And a child with sealed stars climbing the first stair."

The room lost its shape.

Jin heard Doksu outside telling someone in a low voice that the patient was sleeping. He heard wind worrying the paper windows. He heard the Ghost Core go quiet.

That frightened him more than any warning it had ever spoken.

Seolha broke the first seal.

The paper gave with a dry whisper.

She broke the second.

The room seemed to grow colder.

Her hand hovered above the third cut seal.

"Jin Ryu," she said, using his full name as if placing it on a scale. "Once I open this, I cannot unknow what is inside. Neither can you."

"You already know enough to be in danger."

"That is not an answer."

He stared at the box.

His body was useless beneath bandages and splints. His pride had been stripped away by pain. His little victory at the trial already felt far away, like a lantern seen across a river.

But beneath all of that, a different thing had begun to burn.

Not anger.

Anger was too loud.

This was colder.

A need to know why his life had been built as a cage.

"Open it," he said.

Seolha cut the final seal.

Inside the box lay three things.

A thin notebook bound in dark thread.

A folded cloth pouch heavy with old needles.

And a strip of pale paper covered in dots, slashes, medical shorthand, and tiny star diagrams arranged along what looked like a child's meridian map.

Seolha did not touch the needles.

She lifted the notebook first.

The first pages were ordinary enough: fever formulas, bone-setting notes, pulse studies, herbal substitutions during famine seasons. The handwriting was small and disciplined. A physician's hand. A man used to fighting death with measurements.

Then the ink changed.

Not color.

Pressure.

The characters grew smaller, tighter, as if Yun Saja had been writing while looking over his shoulder.

Seolha turned pages until she reached a section marked only with seven dots surrounding an empty circle.

She breathed in.

"This is his private cipher."

"Can you read it?"

"Some. He taught me the fever variation, not this one."

Jin looked at the page.

At first it was nonsense: dots beside old anatomical symbols, broken characters hidden inside dosage marks, lines crossing meridian diagrams at angles no healing text should use.

Then the Ghost Core stirred.

`[Encoded medical shorthand detected.]`

Jin kept his face still.

`[Pattern origin: hybrid physician cipher. Probability of concealment from sect recorders: 94.1%.]`

The marks rearranged inside his sight.

Dots became numbers.

Dosage marks became names.

Star diagrams became injury maps.

The first translated line appeared so clearly that Jin forgot to breathe.

**The boy with sealed stars still lives.**

Seolha watched him.

"What do you see?"

His first instinct was to lie.

It arrived fast and familiar, the instinct of a boy who survived by making himself smaller than other people's attention. Say nothing. Look confused. Let stronger people think you knew less than you did.

But the box was open.

The seal had been cut.

Trust, Jin realized, was also a cliff.

Jin swallowed.

"The first line says the boy with sealed stars still lives."

Seolha's face changed.

Only slightly.

Enough.

"You can read it."

"Not me."

"The second pulse."

He nodded.

Her fingers tightened on the page until the paper bent.

Fear crossed her eyes, quick and controlled.

Not fear of him.

Fear for what he had become.

"Read," she said.

So he did.

Slowly at first.

Then with growing dread.

Yun Saja had begun the notes seventeen years ago, during the storm season, when a woman named Jin Aera arrived at the lower medicine gate carrying an infant wrapped in black cloth and fever talismans. She had not come through the main sect road. She had come through the burial ravine, half-dead, with three arrows in her back and a child who did not cry.

The child had a pulse like a broken instrument.

No.

Yun corrected himself in the margin.

Not broken.

Hidden.

Aera had begged for treatment but refused official registration. She had paid with a jade pin, six pages of forbidden anatomical research, and a single sentence Yun underlined three times.

**If they know he can carry it, they will not let him remain human.**

Seolha stopped him there.

"Carry what?"

Jin looked at the next line.

The Ghost Core hesitated.

`[Term damaged.]`

Characters flickered.

Possible meanings unfolded: core, seed, furnace, ghost, mandate, algorithm.

Jin's skin went cold beneath the blankets.

"The word is damaged," he said. "Core, maybe. Or ghost core."

Seolha's gaze went to the faint mark behind his ear.

"Continue."

He did.

Yun's notes described Aera not as a helpless mother but as a person trained in wounds no sect healer should know how to make. She understood meridian surgery, formation suppression, poison sequencing, and old machine terms that Yun wrote phonetically because he did not understand them. She had brought diagrams of a containment lattice designed to make a child appear worthless to every ordinary pulse test.

The seal would damage his growth.

It would invite ridicule.

It would make cultivation painful.

It would create false records of congenital weakness.

It would save his life.

Jin's hand curled around the blanket.

His bandaged palm screamed.

He did not let go.

Seolha read his face and lowered her eyes to the page.

"Your mother chose the cage."

"To hide me."

"From whom?"

The next section answered.

Yun Saja had written the words in darker ink.

**The Skyless Directorate does not seek disciples. It seeks vessels.**

Seolha went pale.

Jin heard the name before his mind fully accepted it.

The Directorate.

The Veil.

The shadow behind poison.

The hand behind false clerks.

The thing Seolha's dead master feared enough to bury forbidden notes inside hidden cabinets.

Jin continued reading because stopping would not make the words untrue.

There were children across the southern provinces, born with abnormal meridian resonance. Some had pulses that reacted to ancient relic sites. Some could survive formation exposure longer than trained adults. Some woke second pulses during fever, trauma, or surgery. Most vanished before the age of twelve.

One was recorded as dead.

One was transferred to the capital.

One was hidden inside the Obsidian Meridian Sect under the name Jin Ryu.

Host-03.

The Ghost Core did not speak, but the designation burned at the back of his skull.

Seolha pressed two fingers against her own wrist, grounding herself like a physician treating shock in another body.

"Host," she whispered. "Not disciple. Not patient. Host."

"Vessel," Jin said.

The word tasted worse.

Seolha turned the page.

A dried brown smear crossed the margin. Blood, probably. Old enough to have become part of the paper.

Yun's handwriting grew uneven there.

**Subject J.R. containment stable. Artificial weakness accepted by sect registry. Outer Yard labor assignment recommended. Do not allow inner sect examination. Do not allow full-pressure trials. Do not allow Iron Moon Hall access to blood samples.**

Jin almost laughed.

A dead man had warned everyone not to let him take the Thousand-Step Trial.

Seolha did not laugh.

Her lips thinned until they were nearly white.

"Kang forced you into a full-pressure trial."

"He wanted me crushed."

"Or someone wanted your seal stressed."

The room seemed to narrow around that sentence.

Jin remembered Kang's face at the base of the stair. Rage. Confusion. Pride wounded in public.

Kang was cruel.

Kang was arrogant.

Kang was dangerous.

But was he clever enough to arrange exactly the test the dead master's note warned against?

`[Assessment: Kang Taejun personality profile indicates high cruelty, moderate tactical planning, low patience. Probability he acted alone in selecting Thousand-Step Trial: 38.6%.]`

Jin almost answered the voice aloud.

Seolha noticed.

"It spoke again."

"It thinks Kang may not have acted alone."

"I agree with the thing in your skull. I hate that."

A faint sound escaped Jin.

It might have been a laugh.

Pain punished it instantly.

Seolha pushed a cup toward his mouth.

"Drink."

"More medicine?"

"No. Ink-water. Since you apparently enjoy reading your own disaster while bleeding into my bedding."

He drank.

It was medicine.

She lied without blinking.

They continued.

The next pages described containment details in language so complex that even Ghost Core's translation came in broken fragments.

Seven Star Meridian Lock.

False congenital fracture.

Pain-triggered dampening.

Memory veil around early surgery.

Auxiliary channels preserved for emergency survival.

Suppression of external relay handshake.

Warning: if first stair awakens, retrieval protocol may activate.

Jin stopped reading.

Seolha saw why.

"First stair," she said.

Neither of them looked toward the mountain, but both felt it there: the trial stair beneath moonlight, the cracked stele, the hidden architecture humming under stone like a sleeping beast that had opened one eye.

"The monitoring plate," Jin whispered.

Seolha frowned.

"What plate?"

He explained what he had felt before collapsing: the signal moving beyond the northern ridge, the relay response, Ghost Core's warning.

He did not mention the full dream at first.

Then he remembered Aera's bloodied hands.

He told Seolha that too.

By the end, the healer's face had settled into something colder than anger.

"Your mother did not simply hide you," she said. "She sabotaged their ability to recognize what they wanted."

"She made me useless."

"She made you survive."

The correction struck him harder than he expected.

All his life, weakness had been a room others locked him inside.

Now Seolha was telling him that same room had been built as a shelter.

He did not know whether to feel grateful or destroyed.

Maybe both were the same wound viewed from opposite sides.

A knock struck the outer door again.

This one was heavier.

Doksu's voice rumbled through the wood.

"Senior Healer Han, there is an Elder Kang problem."

Seolha closed the notebook so quickly the pages snapped.

She slid the strip of cipher paper beneath the tray and drew a plain fever chart over it.

"How large?" she called.

"Elder sized. With recorders. And a man I don't recognize wearing Discipline Hall gray but standing too straight."

Seolha's eyes met Jin's.

Fake clerk.

Or worse.

"Hide your pulse," she whispered.

Jin almost said he did not know how.

The Ghost Core answered before panic could rise.

`[Emergency concealment protocol available.]`

Cost?

`[Increased neural fatigue. Temporary auditory distortion. Possible vomiting.]`

Wonderful.

`[Proceed?]`

Jin gave the smallest nod.

Behind his ear, cold pressure spread through his skull.

The world tilted.

Sounds flattened, as if the room had been pushed underwater. Seolha's heartbeat became distant thunder. His own pulse grew sluggish and uneven. The second pulse folded inward like a snake hiding beneath ash.

Pain followed.

Not sharp.

Sickening.

His stomach clenched.

Seolha lifted a basin just in time.

He vomited black-tinged medicine into it.

"Good," she said loudly. "If he had any hidden strength, it has clearly chosen a pathetic place to appear."

The door slid open.

Cold air entered first.

Kang Taejun followed.

He wore formal elder robes of Iron Moon Hall, dark red beneath black outer layers, embroidered with a silver moon crest at the collar. His face had recovered from the public shock of the trial, but not entirely. Pride still bled from him in controlled drops.

Behind him stood two Discipline Hall recorders carrying tablets.

And behind them stood a gray-robed man Jin had never seen before.

Plain face.

Plain hands.

Plain eyes.

Too plain.

The Ghost Core remained silent, but a tiny line of text appeared at the bottom of Jin's vision.

`[Threat: observer trained to suppress identifying habits.]`

Kang looked at Jin on the bed.

His smile was thin.

"Jin Ryu. You gave the sect quite a surprise today."

Jin let his head roll slightly toward the elder. He let his eyes appear unfocused. He let shame, pain, and exhaustion cover the colder things underneath.

It was not difficult.

Most of it was true.

"Elder," he rasped.

Kang studied him.

"You do not look like a miracle."

Seolha stepped between them.

"He looks like a patient who completed an idiotic trial while injured. That is not miraculous. That is negligent."

One recorder's brush paused.

Kang's gaze moved to her.

"Senior Healer Han, I understand your emotional attachment to patients may interfere with judgment, but official matters require clarity."

"Then clarity should begin with why an Outer Yard laborer recovering from recent trauma was forced into a full-pressure stair under three seals."

The air sharpened.

Kang's smile did not move.

"The boy was registered for assessment according to law."

"Law also allows Medicine Pavilion review before high-risk trials. My office received no request."

"An oversight."

"A convenient one."

Doksu made a tiny choking sound outside the door.

Kang's eyes hardened.

The gray-robed man watched all of it without expression.

Jin watched him from beneath half-lowered lids.

The man did not look at Seolha when she challenged Kang.

He did not look at the recorders.

He did not even look much at Jin's face.

His gaze rested near Jin's neck.

Pulse point.

Jin forced his breathing to remain uneven.

The concealment protocol pressed colder.

For one terrifying breath, his hearing vanished entirely.

Then sound returned as a thin ringing.

Kang gestured to the recorder.

"We require a simple pulse confirmation. If Jin Ryu used no forbidden stimulant, there is nothing to fear."

Seolha laughed once.

It was not a kind sound.

"An Iron Moon elder who forced a half-dead servant onto the trial stair now wishes to touch his pulse in the middle of the night. I refuse."

"On what authority?"

"On the authority of the Medicine Pavilion's emergency care statute. Challenge it in morning court if you want the entire sect to hear me explain why your seals nearly killed a registered patient."

Kang stepped closer.

For a moment, all masks thinned.

"Be careful, Han Seolha."

"I am. That is why he is still alive."

Silence.

Then the gray-robed man spoke for the first time.

His voice was mild.

"No need to disturb the patient. A visual record may suffice for tonight."

Kang glanced back.

Just once.

Too quick for most people to notice.

Jin noticed.

Seolha noticed.

The recorders raised their tablets and wrote his visible injuries: bandaged hands, swollen knee, fever flush, weak response, suspected channel strain.

The gray-robed man looked at Jin one last time.

Their eyes almost met.

Almost.

Jin let his focus slide away like a coward's.

The man smiled faintly.

Not pleased.

Curious.

Then the visitors left.

The moment the door closed, Jin rolled to the side and vomited again.

Seolha caught the basin with one hand and steadied his shoulder with the other.

Doksu burst back inside.

"Did we win?"

"No," Seolha said.

Jin wiped his mouth with the back of his bandaged hand and whispered, "We were measured."

No one argued.

Seolha sealed the outer room, checked the paper screens, and sent Doksu to stand at the courtyard gate with instructions to shout if anyone breathed suspiciously. Doksu accepted this mission with grave seriousness.

When they were alone again, Seolha reopened the notebook.

Her hands moved faster now.

"We need the part my master wrote after the first stair warning. There must be a response plan."

Jin's head throbbed.

`[Neural fatigue: high.]`

Can you still translate?

`[Yes.]`

Will it hurt?

`[Yes.]`

He almost smiled again.

At least the Ghost Core was honest.

Seolha saw his expression.

"No."

"I did not say anything."

"Your face said something stupid."

"My face is injured. Do not trust it."

"I trust injured faces more than proud mouths. Rest."

"If the Directorate is already inside the sect, rest becomes a luxury."

She stared at him for a long moment.

Then she turned the notebook toward him.

"Five lines only. Then I put you to sleep whether you agree or not."

"Ten."

"Three."

"Five is fair."

"Five, and if you bleed from the nose again, I stop at two."

He nodded.

The next section was worse because it was written like instructions for a funeral that had not yet happened.

**If Subject J.R. reaches first stair exposure, assume one or more Directorate listeners will receive a relay echo. Do not panic. Panic wastes the first night.**

Jin paused.

Seolha whispered, "Master."

Her voice carried something soft enough to hurt.

He continued.

**Hide by appearing documented. Make the impossible boring. Pain, fever, injury, childish luck, pressure anomaly, elder negligence. Give recorders many ordinary explanations. Too many truths invite curiosity. Too many lies invite knives.**

Seolha's mouth pressed tight.

"He knew exactly how they think."

"He was hiding me for years."

"And dying for it."

The words landed between them.

Neither picked them up.

Jin read the third line.

**Do not let Iron Moon Hall isolate him through apology, reward, banquet, punishment review, or blood compensation. Their tables are cleaner than their blades.**

Seolha went still.

Jin looked at her.

"Banquet," he said.

"Iron Moon Hall holds private banquets after public trials," she said slowly. "Usually for favored disciples, allies, or people they wish to humiliate under silk."

"Kang would not invite me."

"If someone advises him to, he will."

The fourth line translated in fragments.

**If invited, refusal marks fear. Acceptance marks exposure. Best option: attend while empty, record everything, eat nothing untested, bleed nowhere, speak little.**

Jin closed his eyes.

Of course.

His life had become a path where every correct step still led closer to a knife.

"Fifth line," Seolha said.

He opened his eyes.

The symbols on the page blurred.

For a second, they were not symbols.

They were stars.

Seven of them, arranged above a black table, while Aera's bloody hand pressed a seal into a child's chest.

Jin gritted his teeth.

The Ghost Core translated.

**Tell the boy this if he wakes angry: your weakness was not your failure. It was a lock placed on a door monsters wanted open.**

Jin stopped breathing.

The pavilion vanished.

Seolha's hand appeared on the notebook, covering the next line.

Not to hide it.

To give him somewhere to look.

"Enough," she said.

He could not answer.

His throat had closed around something too old to swallow.

All those years.

All those morning punishments. All those bowls kicked over, names spat after him, fists thrown because he could not throw them back. Every sneer of Broken Pulse Dog. Every elder who had looked at him as wasted grain. Every night he had pressed his hand to his chest and hated the weak rhythm beneath it.

Not failure.

A lock.

He wanted to be grateful.

He wanted to scream.

He wanted to find Aera and ask why survival had to feel so much like abandonment.

Seolha did not comfort him.

Maybe she understood that comfort would be too small.

Instead, she closed the notebook and wrapped it again with the cut talisman paper.

"My master left that for you," she said.

"He did not know me."

"He knew enough to die afraid for you."

Jin turned his face toward the window.

Outside, the moon hid behind clouds.

His reflection in the dark paper was pale, bruised, and unfamiliar.

`[Emotional instability detected.]`

Jin almost laughed at that too.

Not now.

The Ghost Core went quiet.

Seolha put the notebook back into the box but did not return the box to the cabinet. Instead, she slid it beneath a loose floorboard under the patient bed.

"Temporary," she said. "No one searches under the body they think they already inspected."

"You are good at hiding illegal truths."

"I had a good teacher."

A tiny crack appeared in her composure there.

Not tears.

Not yet.

But something close enough that Jin looked away.

The outer door slammed.

Doksu shouted, "Message! Not breathing suspiciously! Actually, breathing very officially!"

Seolha closed her eyes.

"That boy will kill us through description."

A moment later, Doksu slid the door open with a stiff paper envelope pinched between two fingers, as if it might bite him.

"Delivered by Iron Moon Hall servant," he said. "He said refusal requires formal reply by dawn. Also he looked smug, so I disliked him immediately."

Seolha took the envelope.

Black paper.

Silver moon seal.

Kang's faction.

She did not open it at first.

She looked at Jin.

Jin already knew.

The dead master's warning had arrived before the trap, but only barely.

Seolha broke the seal.

The invitation unfolded with the soft confidence of expensive paper.

Her eyes moved across the lines.

Her expression did not change.

That made Doksu panic more.

"Bad?"

Seolha handed the paper to Jin.

His fingers were too weak, so she held it where he could read.

The handwriting was elegant, formal, and poisonous.

**Iron Moon Hall congratulates Jin Ryu on his unexpected completion of the Thousand-Step Trial. In honor of this rare achievement, Elder Kang Taejun invites him to attend a reconciliation banquet tomorrow evening, where misunderstandings may be corrected and proper rewards discussed.**

At the bottom, beneath Kang's seal, a second mark had been pressed so faintly most eyes would miss it.

A circle inside three broken rings.

Jin's implant burned cold behind his ear.

The Ghost Core spoke with perfect clarity.

`[Symbol match: relay observer mark.]`

Doksu looked between them.

"So... we are not going?"

No one answered.

Outside, thunder rolled over the northern ridge.

Jin stared at the invitation until the silver moon seal blurred.

The dead master had written: refusal marks fear, acceptance marks exposure.

The trap had two jaws.

He had one broken body.

Seolha folded the invitation.

"You are too injured to attend."

Jin looked at her.

"That is why they invited me."

"You cannot fight."

"Then I will not fight."

"You cannot eat."

"Then I will not eat."

"You can barely walk."

He shifted his gaze toward the foot of the bed, where his old cracked saber leaned against the wall wrapped in dark cloth. Someone had brought it from the armory or the trial grounds. He did not ask who.

The blade slept silently.

For now.

"Then," Jin said, "I will make them carry their own knives to me."

Seolha stared at him as if deciding whether to heal him or strangle him.

Doksu whispered, "That sounds dangerous and impressive. I hate it."

Jin's hand found the edge of the blanket.

His body shook from fever, exhaustion, and fear he refused to name.

But beneath the shaking, a lock inside him had begun to understand itself.

Not open.

Not yet.

Understanding was not freedom.

But it was the first insult a prison ever received.

Outside the Medicine Pavilion, in the black space between lanterns, the gray-robed observer stood beneath the eaves and watched the room's shadow.

Rain began to fall.

He lifted one hand to a small jade plate hidden inside his sleeve.

Cold blue script formed across its surface.

**HOST-03 HAS RECEIVED INVITATION.**

The observer waited.

A reply came from somewhere beyond the northern ridge.

Only four words.

**LET HIM CHOOSE HUNGER.**

The observer smiled.

Inside, Jin Ryu read the Iron Moon invitation again.

The silent algorithm listened.

And beneath the mountain, where old stone dreamed in blue-black lines, the cracked stele remembered his blood.
