The Waste Armory smelled like rust, old sweat, and futures the sect had already thrown away.

Jin Ryu stood beneath its sagging wooden sign with the afternoon sun burning pale behind him and the taste of medicine still clinging to the back of his tongue. His left side ached where the knife wound under his robe had begun to close badly. His palms were wrapped. His ribs complained whenever he breathed too deeply. His meridians, those narrow invisible roads inside his body, pulsed as if someone had threaded hot wire through them and pulled whenever he tried to stand straight.

A healthy disciple would have called the pain inconvenient.

For Jin, it was proof.

He had been poisoned, hunted, cut, watched, and still remained alive enough to choose a weapon.

The sect had not expected him to need one.

The armory doors were open, but only because the Waste Armory had nothing worth guarding. Real disciples went to the Iron Hall, where polished spears rested on lacquered racks, where sabers were oiled with scented cloth, where swords hung beneath name tablets and lineage marks. A promising inner disciple did not choose a weapon. A promising inner disciple was matched with one.

The sect measured talent even in metal.

Those with futures received steel that sang.

Those without futures came here and listened to dead iron cough in the dust.

Inside the Waste Armory, broken weapons leaned against cracked walls in barrels like dead branches after a storm. Spear shafts without heads. Saber blades with chipped edges. Swords bent from failed tempering. Practice knives with loose handles. Training staffs split along the grain. A pair of iron gauntlets sat on a low shelf, one missing three fingers and the other stained so deeply at the knuckles that no amount of scrubbing had cleaned it.

Dust floated in the slanting sunlight.

So did mockery.

“Look who came to choose a weapon.”

The voice came from the left, lazy and loud enough to invite witnesses.

Jin did not turn immediately. Turning too quickly showed reaction. Not turning at all showed fear. He let one breath pass, then glanced over his shoulder.

Three outer disciples lounged near a rack of discarded polearms. Two were boys he knew by face but not by name, the kind of people who laughed when Kang Taejun laughed and became silent when Kang stopped smiling. The third was Min Hojun, one of the disciples who had thrown stones at Black Needle Cliff.

Min’s cheek was still bruised from slipping during the cliff incident. He had told others that the wind betrayed him.

Jin had not corrected him.

Min smiled now, showing a little too much teeth.

“Did someone tell you the trash bins had started accepting trash?”

One of the others snorted.

Jin looked at the barrels, not at Min.

“I was told I needed a weapon.”

“For what? To cut vegetables?” Min stepped away from the wall. His robe was clean, his belt new, his boots soft. Someone had paid for comfort before he earned it. “Or are you planning to duel Kang Taejun with a broken spoon?”

The name moved through the armory like a cold draft.

Kang had not appeared since morning training, but his attention had been everywhere. Jin had felt it in the sudden silence when he walked past groups. In the way servants avoided meeting his eyes. In the way Cho had rubbed his bruised wrist while pretending not to look at Jin’s feet.

The Skyless Directorate watched from behind masks.

Kang watched from behind pride.

Both wanted the same answer.

Would Jin Ryu still break like a servant, or had something inside him begun to break back?

Jin crouched beside the nearest barrel and pulled out a short saber with a missing guard. The blade had a deep bite near the middle, as if someone had tried to cut stone and lost the argument. It was too heavy near the tip and too loose at the handle.

`[Weapon integrity: poor.]`

Ghost Core’s voice was cool inside his skull.

`[Estimated failure under moderate impact: 63%.]`

Jin slid the saber back into the barrel.

Min laughed. “Even the broken blade rejected him.”

Another disciple joined. “Maybe give him a broom. He already has training.”

Jin moved to the next rack.

Hope, he was learning, was not a warm feeling.

Hope was sharp.

It made the gap between what he needed and what the world offered more painful. It made every cracked blade feel like an insult placed in his hand. Before the Ghost Core, he would have looked at the barrels and seen proof that the sect had judged him correctly. Garbage for a garbage disciple. A broken tool for a broken pulse.

Now he saw data.

A bent sword told him about impact angle. A chipped spearhead told him where a previous user had overextended. A split staff showed the weakness of dry wood when struck against iron at the wrong rotation. The Waste Armory was not empty.

It was a graveyard of mistakes.

And mistakes left bones.

Jin lifted a narrow practice sword. Too light. The edge had been ground dull, but that was not the real problem. The tang clicked inside the handle when he shifted his wrist.

`[Handle separation likely. Recommended rejection.]`

Rejected.

He picked up a crescent knife. The curve did not match his current reach. Rejected.

A spear shaft. Too long for his injured side. Rejected.

A short axe. Too much shoulder strain. Rejected.

A straight sword with a hairline crack near the base. The Ghost Core displayed a pale red line across Jin’s vision, tracing the invisible weakness.

`[Catastrophic fracture probability under deflection: 78%.]`

Jin put it down.

The disciples’ laughter grew bored.

Bored cruelty was worse than angry cruelty. Anger at least had direction. Bored cruelty searched for entertainment.

Min wandered closer and kicked a cracked shield aside. It clattered across the floor and struck Jin’s heel.

Jin’s body reacted before his pride did.

His left foot shifted half a finger width. His weight lowered. His right hand opened.

`[Threat posture detected.]`

“I am not attacking you,” Min said, smiling.

“You are standing too close.”

The smile thinned.

The other two disciples straightened.

In the old days, Jin would have apologized. He would have bent his head and swallowed the insult because survival had demanded it. Even now, part of him wanted to. The part that remembered hunger. The part that remembered being ten years old and learning that a servant disciple’s bones were cheaper than a senior disciple’s mood.

But another part of him listened to the shape of Min’s breath.

Short inhale. Held throat. Loose shoulders. Right foot eager.

Not an attack yet.

A question.

Min wanted to know whether Jin would flinch.

Jin looked at him.

“I am still standing.”

The words came out quietly.

That made them worse.

Min’s face changed. Not much. Just enough.

The boy who had laughed at a dog suddenly disliked hearing the dog speak like a person.

“What did you say?”

“I said I am still standing.” Jin reached for another barrel. “If that bothers you, ask yourself why.”

The armory fell silent.

For one dangerous moment, the air became a blade drawn halfway from its sheath.

Then a dry cough came from the back room.

“Fight in my armory,” an old voice rasped, “and I will charge both corpses for floor damage.”

The disciples turned.

An old man sat behind a counter half-hidden by stacked crates, though Jin was certain the chair had been empty when he entered. His hair was tied in a gray knot. One eye was clouded white. The other was sharp enough to cut thread. A ledger lay open before him, but the brush beside it had no ink.

Waste Armory Keeper Wen.

Everyone called him Old Wen when he was not listening.

No one called him that when he was.

Min clicked his tongue. “Keeper Wen, we were only giving advice.”

“Advice has never smelled that stupid before.”

One of the boys choked on a laugh and swallowed it when Min glared.

Keeper Wen scratched the side of his nose. “Choose, borrow, break, return. Those are the rules. If you want to posture, do it outside. The weapons here have already suffered enough from fools.”

Min’s jaw tightened. He looked at Jin one last time.

“You can pick any trash you want,” he said. “The Thousand-Step Trial will still make you kneel.”

There it was.

The reason.

Jin’s fingers paused on a cracked spear shaft.

The Thousand-Step Trial had been a public progress test for outer disciples since before he entered the sect. Once every season, junior disciples climbed the Thousand Steps from the lower yard to the Meridian Gate while carrying assigned weight and defending themselves against staged pressure. It was supposed to measure endurance, footwork, circulation, and will.

Servant disciples were not usually included.

Servant disciples carried water to the bottom and swept blood from the top.

Min saw the flicker in his eyes and smiled.

“You didn’t know?”

Jin said nothing.

“Senior Brother Kang petitioned the instructors this morning. Said the sect should reward sudden improvement. Said even broken dogs deserve a chance to show loyalty.” Min leaned closer. “You are listed as a special participant. Three days from now. Public yard. Everyone will watch.”

One of the boys added, “If you reach the top, you get official outer disciple consideration.”

The other laughed. “If.”

Min’s smile widened. “If you collapse, everyone sees where you belong.”

Jin’s fingers closed around the spear shaft until the splinters pressed through his bandage.

So that was Kang’s move.

Not a back-alley beating. Not yet.

A stage.

A public place where humiliation could wear the robe of fairness.

If Jin refused, he was a coward.

If he accepted and failed, he became proof that his recent survival had been luck.

If he performed too well, the Skyless Directorate would receive more data.

A trap with three open doors, each leading into teeth.

Kang had not built a cage.

He had built a stage and called it fairness.

`[Social pressure construct identified.]`

“I noticed,” Jin thought.

`[Recommended response: acquire appropriate weapon. Reduce visible anomaly. Maintain moderate performance threshold.]`

Moderate performance.

Not too weak.

Not too strong.

A dog learning where the leash began.

Jin released the spear shaft and moved deeper into the armory.

Behind him, Min waited for anger. For fear. For any reaction he could carry back to Kang like a fresh bone.

Jin gave him none.

That annoyed him more than an insult would have.

“Enjoy your scraps,” Min said.

His group left with noisy steps and louder laughter.

Only after the doors creaked and the voices faded did Keeper Wen speak again.

“You have a talent for making people want to hit you.”

Jin looked over.

The old keeper had gone back to staring at his blank ledger.

“I thought that was my natural charm.”

Keeper Wen’s cloudy eye remained still. The sharp eye moved.

“Hm.”

It was not quite a laugh.

Jin returned to the barrels.

The silence after mockery always felt larger than silence before it. It gave the insults room to echo. Broken dog. Trash. Spoon. Kneel. Jin let the words strike once, then filed them away.

Not because they did not hurt.

Because hurt was also information.

His anger made his pulse jump. His sealed meridians tightened in response. When they tightened, the Ghost Core’s interface flickered faintly at the edge of his vision. Emotional instability worsened system strain. Pride could kill him as surely as poison.

He inhaled through his nose.

Held.

Released.

Baek’s cork was gone, lost in Chapter Eight’s desperate distraction during Doksu’s rescue, but the lesson remained. Breath did not need the cork to be trained. The cork had only made stupidity obvious.

His pulse lowered by a fraction.

The Ghost Core’s red warning dimmed.

Keeper Wen watched without watching.

Jin pretended not to notice.

He searched for another quarter of an hour.

By then, his back had dampened under his robe. Afternoon heat pooled beneath the armory roof. Dust stuck to the sweat at his temples. Every weapon he lifted told the same story: too broken, too heavy, too long, too dangerous, too obvious.

That last part mattered.

An impressive weapon invited attention. A strange weapon invited questions. A weapon that required rare skill invited suspicion. Jin needed something low enough to be ignored but useful enough to keep him alive.

The Ghost Core displayed rough outlines as he worked.

Weight.

Balance.

Reach.

Grip condition.

Failure risk.

Compatibility with damaged meridians.

The last category remained mostly red.

His body was the worst weapon in the room.

After rejecting a cracked staff, Jin noticed the practice posts.

They stood in the rear of the armory, half-forgotten behind stacked shields and a torn canvas screen. There were six of them, each thick as a man’s thigh and bolted to heavy stone bases. They were not for current disciples. The newer training yard outside used polished posts, painted rings, and chalk marks for instructor corrections. These old posts were scarred black and brown from decades of bad cuts, good cuts, desperate cuts, and bored cuts.

Jin walked toward them.

`[Objects: wood training posts. Condition: obsolete.]`

“Not obsolete,” Jin thought.

He stopped before the first post.

The cuts layered over one another like old arguments. Shallow horizontal scratches near shoulder height. Deep diagonal bites. A cluster of hacked marks too high to be practical unless the attacker had jumped. Faint grooves near the base where someone had trained low sweeps until the wood dented inward.

Damage contained memory.

He had thought it earlier without understanding the full weight of it.

Now the Ghost Core seemed to wake more sharply.

`[Pattern density detected.]`

Pale lines flickered across his vision.

The post changed.

Not physically. Not truly.

But in the Ghost Core’s overlay, each cut gained direction, force, probable weapon curvature, estimated user height, estimated stance, and decay age.

Jin sucked in a breath.

The display multiplied.

`[Historical impact reconstruction possible.]`

His head throbbed.

The world dimmed at the edges.

The Ghost Core could read wounds in wood the way a physician read bruises in flesh, but every answer demanded space inside Jin's skull. Space he did not have.

`[Warning: reconstruction load may aggravate neural inflammation.]`

“Limit it.”

`[Define limit.]`

Jin stared at the post.

Too much information would drown him. He was learning that the Ghost Core did not always know what mattered to a human body unless he told it. It could calculate the shape of a storm and still forget that a boy had only two lungs.

“Only saber cuts. Only the cleanest ones. No more than three patterns.”

A pause.

`[Constraints accepted.]`

Most of the glowing lines vanished.

Three remained.

One near the center, a diagonal cut from high right to low left. Deep at the beginning, lighter at the end. Powerful entry. Weak finish.

One horizontal, shallow but perfectly level. Less force. Better control.

One low rising slash that had bitten upward from knee height, cutting deeper than it should have. The Ghost Core marked it in pale blue instead of white.

Jin leaned closer.

`[Anomalous efficiency. Force output exceeded expected muscular contribution by 41%.]`

A real technique.

Not a full manual. Not a secret art carved politely into bamboo slips. Just the scar it had left behind.

But scars were honest.

Jin reached out and touched the low rising slash.

The wood was old and smooth within the groove. Many years had passed since the cut was made. Maybe decades. Maybe before he was born. The disciple who made it might be dead, crippled, promoted, forgotten, or buried without a name.

But one movement remained.

Ghost Core expanded the line.

For an instant, the dusty armory vanished.

A translucent figure stood before the post. Faceless. Built from probability and old damage. The figure held a saber low, weight sinking into the rear leg, front foot turned inward at an ugly angle. The shoulder did not lift. The elbow did not flare. The cut began not from the arm but from the waist, spiraling through the hip, rib, shoulder, elbow, wrist, and finally blade.

It was not beautiful.

It was efficient.

Jin watched once.

The figure reset.

Watched twice.

Reset.

The third time, pain shot behind his left eye.

The translucent figure blurred.

`[Neural strain rising.]`

Jin gritted his teeth.

“Enough.”

The overlay disappeared.

The armory returned all at once: dust, rust, heat, old wood, Keeper Wen’s quiet breathing from the counter.

Jin’s hand was trembling.

A warm drop touched his upper lip.

He wiped it away.

Blood.

Nosebleed.

Keeper Wen said, “Old posts are boring enough to make boys bleed now?”

Jin kept his back turned and wiped again.

“The dust is strong.”

“Dust usually attacks the lungs. Yours attacked your nose.”

“It was ambitious dust.”

This time, Keeper Wen did laugh. Once. A dry crackle.

Jin lowered his hand and looked around for a saber.

Not any saber.

One that matched the reconstructed cut.

Low center of gravity. Slightly forward balance. A curve that could rise from below without demanding too much shoulder rotation. Short enough for his weak reach. Heavy enough to teach his wrist truth. Broken enough that no one would envy it.

He searched the racks again with new eyes.

The first candidate was too long.

The second too straight.

The third had a loose grip.

The fourth was a butcher’s mistake pretending to be a saber.

The fifth lay beneath a pile of cracked scabbards in a corner where the sunlight barely reached.

Jin almost missed it because it looked dead.

The saber was short, ugly, and dark. Not black like lacquer. Black like metal that had spent too long inside smoke. The edge had been sharpened unevenly, then neglected. The tip was chipped. The guard was plain and dented. The handle wrap had rotted away in two places, exposing old wood beneath. A hairline crack ran from the spine toward the middle of the blade, thin as a strand of hair and dangerous as a secret.

Jin picked it up.

His injured side immediately complained.

The saber was heavier than it looked.

Not much heavier. Just enough to make honesty unavoidable.

`[Weapon integrity: compromised.]`

The Ghost Core drew the crack in red.

`[Estimated failure under direct clash: 49%.]`

“Only direct clash?” Jin thought.

`[Correct.]`

“What about deflection?”

`[Controlled glancing contact below twenty-seven degrees: tolerable.]`

“What about cutting?”

`[Edge irregular. Cutting efficiency reduced. Training utility: moderate.]`

Training utility.

Not victory utility.

Good.

A weapon that promised victory would lie to him.

A weapon that admitted it might break could be planned around.

Jin shifted his grip.

The balance pulled slightly forward. The low rising slash from the practice post hummed in his memory. Not in the blade. In his body. Hip. Rib. Shoulder. Elbow. Wrist. Blade.

He lifted the saber, then lowered it quickly when pain flashed through his sealed meridians.

`[Warning: right shoulder compensation will reopen side wound.]`

“I know.”

`[Then do not repeat.]`

Jin stared at the saber.

Sometimes the Ghost Core sounded less like a machine and more like an irritated physician.

He tried again.

Slower.

This time he did not lift from the shoulder. He let the blade hang low near his right thigh. He bent his knees only as much as his meridians allowed. His rear foot settled against the dusty plank floor. His front foot turned inward a little.

Ugly.

Embarrassing.

Alive.

He moved his waist.

The saber rose three inches.

Pain tightened around his ribs.

He stopped.

`[Movement incomplete.]`

“I noticed.”

`[Cause: insufficient hip-spine transmission. Cause: sealed meridian obstruction at left lower channel. Cause: fear response.]`

Jin’s jaw tightened.

“Remove the last one.”

`[Statement inaccurate.]`

“I said remove it.”

`[Fear response contributes to movement restriction by 12%.]`

The number annoyed him more than the accusation.

Fear, quantified, became harder to deny.

Jin exhaled.

His side hurt. His head hurt. His palms hurt beneath the bandages. Doksu’s split lip flashed in his memory. Seolha’s warning followed. Poison. Pressure. Isolation. Affection. Betrayal.

The Thousand-Step Trial waited in three days.

Kang waited above the steps.

The Skyless Directorate waited behind the trial.

A cracked saber waited in his hand.

Fear was reasonable.

The problem was not having fear.

The problem was letting fear choose the angle.

He reset his stance.

“Again.”

`[Recommended: five repetitions only.]`

“Ten.”

`[Five.]`

Jin almost laughed.

“Seven.”

A pause.

`[Six. Rest interval required.]`

“Six.”

He performed the first repetition.

It was terrible.

The saber wobbled halfway through. His wrist bent. His shoulder tried to steal the movement. The blade rose too high and cut empty air with a weak hiss.

`[Failure.]`

“Useful.”

Second repetition.

His hip moved too late.

`[Failure.]`

Third.

He remembered the faceless figure. Rear leg. Inward foot. Waist first. The saber rose from below like a question.

Still wrong.

But less wrong.

`[Deviation reduced by 9%.]`

Fourth.

Pain flared sharply through his left lower channel. His sealed meridian clenched, and the blade path collapsed.

He dropped to one knee before he could stop himself.

Dust puffed around him.

The saber point struck the floor.

From the counter, Keeper Wen sighed.

“Try not to die on the boards. Blood stains are paperwork.”

Jin breathed through his teeth.

“I will avoid inconvenience.”

“Kind of you.”

`[Continue?]`

The Ghost Core did not recommend continuation.

It asked.

That was new.

Jin noticed even through the pain.

A tool gave commands.

A teacher gave corrections.

A watcher asked questions.

What was the Ghost Core becoming inside his skull?

No. Later.

He pushed himself up.

“Continue.”

`[Acknowledged.]`

Fifth repetition.

He lowered the starting point, reduced the arc, and allowed the saber to be ugly. The reconstructed pattern wanted a complete body he did not have. Jin did not have the luxury of copying dead men perfectly.

He needed the version a broken body could survive.

The blade rose.

Shorter.

Crooked.

But this time, the movement did not tear his side.

`[Adapted path registered.]`

Sixth repetition.

Hip. Rib. Shoulder. Elbow. Wrist.

Not a slash.

A breath with metal attached.

The saber moved through the air and made a sound so faint Jin almost missed it.

Hnn.

Not a whistle. Not a ring.

A thin vibration, like a thread being plucked far away.

The armory seemed to listen.

Jin froze.

Keeper Wen’s brushless ledger remained open.

The old man’s sharp eye had lifted.

“What did you do?” he asked.

Jin looked at the saber.

The blade was still.

“I moved it.”

“Obviously.”

“I do not know.”

Keeper Wen stood slowly.

He was taller than he looked while sitting, though age had bent him forward. He walked around the counter with a limp that did not match weakness. One step dragged. The next landed silently. Drag. Silence. Drag. Silence.

Ghost Core marked the rhythm.

`[Gait irregularity artificial. Combat concealment probability: 68%.]`

Jin lowered his eyes.

Of course even the armory keeper was not simple.

No one who survived long among broken weapons remained simple. Broken things taught people where the sharp edges hid.

Keeper Wen stopped two paces away and pointed at the saber.

“Where did you find that?”

“In the corner.”

“That corner has many things.”

“Under cracked scabbards.”

“Hm.”

He reached for the saber.

Jin hesitated.

The old man’s brow rose.

“You think I will steal garbage from you?”

“No.” Jin handed it over. “I think garbage becomes expensive when someone notices it.”

Keeper Wen’s mouth twitched.

“Smart dog.”

The words should have angered him.

They did, a little.

But Keeper Wen said it differently than Min. Less like an insult. More like a category of animal that had learned where humans hid knives.

The old keeper turned the saber under the light.

His thumb ran near the crack but did not touch it. His cloudy eye stayed fixed. His sharp eye narrowed.

“Bad tempering,” he said. “Bad maintenance. Bad luck.”

Jin waited.

“And?”

“And bad things sometimes survive because no one wants to stand close enough to finish breaking them.” Keeper Wen returned the saber. “It is worthless.”

“That sounds useful.”

“To a strange boy, perhaps.”

“What was it?”

Keeper Wen’s expression closed.

“A saber.”

“I noticed.”

“Then your eyes work.”

Jin looked at the blade again. Along the spine, beneath rust and dust, there were faint markings. Not decorative. Not sect script. Tiny square impressions, almost too shallow to see.

Ghost Core flickered.

`[Marking pattern partially compatible with pre-sect artifact encoding.]`

Jin’s pulse stuttered.

Keeper Wen’s sharp eye caught it.

“You see something?”

“Rust.”

“Rust makes boys nervous now?”

“Ambitious rust.”

Another dry almost-laugh.

Keeper Wen stepped back.

“Take it if you want. Deposit is two copper strings.”

Jin stared.

“I do not have two copper strings.”

“Then one.”

“I have no copper.”

“Of course you don’t.” The old man scratched his chin. “Then leave something.”

“What?”

“Anything you value.”

Jin’s first instinct was to say he owned nothing valuable.

That was not true.

Poor people did not lack valuables.

They simply owned things rich people did not know how to price.

He reached into his inner robe and touched the thread charm from his mother.

No.

Never.

His fingers moved away.

He had a bone needle Seolha had given him for emergency pressure points. Too useful. He had a small folded cloth with dried herbs. Too necessary. He had a strip of bandage Doksu had torn from his own sleeve after the ambush. Worthless to anyone else. Not worthless to Jin.

Then his fingers found something hard and smooth in the corner of his pocket.

A fragment of cork.

Not the original cork Baek had given him. That one was lost. This was a small broken piece Jin had cut from a stopper in the kitchen after returning, embarrassed by how much he missed the training weight between his teeth. A replacement. Ugly. Uneven. Still marked by the pressure of his bite.

He placed it on the counter.

Keeper Wen looked at it.

“A chewed cork.”

“Yes.”

“You value this?”

“I trained with it.”

“That is not what I asked.”

Jin looked at the small object.

Baek's laughter. Breath through pain. The first time someone had given him a tool without asking him to kneel for it.

The first proof that training did not have to begin with humiliation.

“Yes.”

Keeper Wen studied him for a long moment, then swept the cork into a drawer.

“Deposit accepted.”

The transaction felt ridiculous.

It also felt binding.

Jin tied the saber at his waist with a strip of spare cloth. The weapon hung awkwardly, pulling his robe to one side. He looked like a servant wearing an older brother’s discarded pride.

Good.

Let them laugh.

Laughter made people lazy.

Before leaving, he glanced once more at the practice post.

The low rising slash waited there, patient as an old ghost.

He bowed to Keeper Wen.

The old man waved him away.

At the door, Keeper Wen spoke again.

“Boy.”

Jin stopped.

“If you use that saber to block a strong strike, it will break.”

“I know.”

“If you swing it like a hero, it will break.”

“I know.”

“If you trust it, it will break.”

Jin looked back.

Keeper Wen’s sharp eye held him in place.

“So do not trust it.”

The words settled somewhere deep.

Not trust the saber.

Use it.

Understand it.

Know where it would fail before failure arrived.

That was not cynicism.

That was survival.

Jin nodded.

“I understand.”

“No,” Keeper Wen said. “You don’t. But you might before it kills you.”

Outside, the afternoon had shifted toward evening. The air smelled of pine smoke and boiling millet. Disciples crossed the lower yard in loose groups, carrying training packs and gossip. Jin kept his head lowered as he walked, but not because he wanted to hide.

He was watching feet.

People showed truth in their feet.

Those who felt safe walked wide. Those carrying secrets stepped lightly near corners. Those looking for a fight placed weight forward. Those afraid of being seen moved too quickly through open space.

Jin saw more than he used to.

That was the problem.

Every improvement opened another layer of danger.

Near the water yard, Ma Doksu waited beside a stack of empty buckets with a strip of cloth pressed to his split lip. He straightened when he saw Jin.

Then his eyes dropped to the saber.

For one heartbeat, Doksu stared.

Then his face twisted.

“What is that?”

“A saber.”

“No.” Doksu came closer, horrified. “No, that is a metal illness with a handle.”

Jin looked down at it.

“It has training utility.”

“Did you just compliment it like a sick goat?”

“It was affordable.”

“You stole it?”

“I paid a deposit.”

“With what money?”

“A cork.”

Doksu stared at him for a long time.

Then he pressed the cloth to his lip and groaned.

“I was hit in the mouth for you yesterday. Maybe the strike shook my brain loose, because I understood none of that.”

Jin felt something loosen in his chest.

Not laughter exactly.

Close.

“How is your lip?”

“Still attached. Unlike your judgment.” Doksu leaned closer and lowered his voice. “People are saying Kang put your name on the Thousand-Step list.”

“I heard.”

“You cannot refuse?”

“I can.”

Doksu looked hopeful.

“And?”

“And then Kang wins without touching me.”

The hope died.

Doksu kicked a pebble across the dirt. “I hate when you say things that sound clever and terrible.”

“So do I.”

For a moment they stood in the space between buckets, smoke, gossip, and the mountain’s long shadow.

Doksu looked at the saber again.

“Can you even use it?”

“No.”

“That was not encouraging.”

“I can learn one movement.”

“One?”

“One movement used correctly is better than ten movements used to impress witnesses.”

Doksu frowned. “That sounds like something Master Baek would say after drinking bad wine.”

“It might be.”

“Did he teach you saber?”

“No.”

“Did anyone?”

“No.”

Doksu closed his eyes.

“I am going to pretend I did not ask.”

Jin’s gaze drifted past him.

Across the yard, on the upper walkway, a clerk in gray robes stood beneath the eaves. Not the same thin fake kitchen worker. Not the same false Discipline Hall clerk. This one was broader, older, and carried a bamboo scroll case against his hip. His face was ordinary in the practiced way of someone who had trained to become forgettable.

He was not looking directly at Jin.

That made it worse.

`[Surveillance probability: 74%.]`

Jin shifted his stance so his body blocked Doksu’s line of sight.

Doksu noticed anyway.

“What?”

“Nothing.”

“That word has become very expensive recently.”

Jin looked at his friend.

That made him dangerous to Jin’s enemies.

And vulnerable because of Jin.

Because they attacked the part of Jin that thought isolation was kindness.

Ghost Core said nothing.

Good.

Some things did not need calculation.

“I do not want you hurt because of me.”

Doksu smiled as if he had won something.

“You still look ridiculous.”

“I know.”

“Good. As long as you know.”

The clerk on the walkway had disappeared.

Jin did not chase him with his eyes.

`[Threat pattern recorded. Survival route recalculating.]`

“Later,” Jin thought.

He left Doksu near the water yard and went behind the old grain store where the wall met a narrow strip of dirt too poor for weeds. No one trained there because the ground sloped badly and smelled faintly of damp sacks. That made it useful.

Good training places were watched.

Bad places belonged to people with no options.

Jin drew the cracked saber.

The blade came free with a scrape, not a ring.

He faced the wall.

No audience.

No Min.

No Kang.

Rear leg. Inward foot. Low blade. Loose shoulder.

Fourth.

Four repetitions.

Kang could probably do two hundred while laughing.

Low.

Host-03 obtained discarded saber.
