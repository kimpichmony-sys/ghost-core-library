Fog made liars of distances.

It slipped over the roofs of the Outer Yard before dawn, softened the punishment posts, blurred the black training stones, and swallowed the muddy paths until the Obsidian Meridian Sect looked less like a fortress and more like a memory trying to hide from itself.

Jin Ryu moved through that memory with a water bucket in each hand.

Both buckets were empty.

That was the useful part.

Empty buckets betrayed everything. Their iron handles knocked if a person hurried. Their hollow bellies caught rhythm. Their rims clicked when fear tightened the fingers. A strong disciple could walk with full buckets and call it training. A servant disciple with empty ones only looked like someone assigned useless work before sunrise.

Jin needed to look useless.

The black wine cork Master Baek had given him sat between his teeth.

Not bitten.

Not chewed.

Held.

There was nothing mystical about it. No scripture. No hidden formation. No glow. No legendary material waiting to recognize a chosen owner. It was only an old cork, warped from rice wine and smoke, soft enough to destroy by accident and stubborn enough to expose every mistake his jaw made.

That somehow made it more insulting than a spirit treasure.

Jin walked with his shoulders rounded, left leg dragging a fraction more than necessary, buckets swinging with servant rhythm, and the cork held between his teeth as if his life depended on not crushing it.

His jaw trembled.

His tongue ached.

The muscles beneath his ears burned with small, humiliating fire.

`[Mandibular tension exceeds recommended threshold.]`

The Ghost Core’s voice sounded colder in the fog.

Or perhaps Jin’s head was simply too tired to warm anything.

`[Respiration irregular. Right-hand grip force increased by twelve percent. External disguise remains functional. Internal strain accumulating.]`

Jin exhaled through his nose.

The cork shifted.

His teeth closed by instinct.

A tiny crack snapped through the mist.

He froze.

One bucket bumped his knee.

The sound was nothing. A bird could have made more noise landing on a branch. But to Jin it rang like a bell in a punishment hall.

He removed the cork slowly.

A bite mark crossed one side.

Not broken.

Not spared either.

“Failure,” he whispered.

`[Correction: partial failure.]`

“That sounds worse.”

`[Partial failure preserves training object. Total failure destroys training object. Distinction useful.]`

Jin stared at the cork.

Somewhere behind him, a rooster screamed at the morning as if it had discovered betrayal. Soon the kitchen boys would wake. The water yard would fill. Doksu would stumble downhill half-asleep and pretend he had not stolen steamed buns the night before. The sect would become noise again.

For now, Jin had only fog, empty buckets, and a ridiculous cork teaching him that control was not stiffness.

He put it back between his teeth.

This time he did not force his jaw still.

He let it rest.

The difference was small enough that most people would not have cared.

His body cared.

The trembling eased.

`[Adjustment effective.]`

Jin continued walking.

Cold collected in his sleeves. His ribs complained with every step. The wound behind his left ear throbbed beneath skin that looked too unmarked for what had entered him. The tunnel, the black chamber, the voice in his skull, Master Baek’s laughter, Kang Taejun’s shadow, the false clerk’s polite smile—every danger circled his thoughts like crows.

He wanted to think about all of them.

He could not afford to.

The cork demanded too much attention.

That, Jin realized, was the lesson.

A fist could be dodged by instinct. An insult could be swallowed by habit. Pain could be endured because pain had been eating meals with him since childhood.

But walking, breathing, pretending to limp, holding empty buckets, watching the fog for witnesses, and keeping a cork unbroken required his whole body to stop arguing with itself.

Jin hated how useful it was.

He reached the first turn near the medicine path when the Ghost Core spoke again.

`[Approaching individual. Pulse signature: Han Seolha. Risk category: high. Concealment recommended.]`

Jin stopped.

The fog ahead parted.

Han Seolha stood beneath a leafless persimmon tree beside the path, white robe half-hidden by gray mist. A medicine basket hung from one arm. Her hair was tied simply today, not with jade or silver, but with a plain dark cord. That made her look less like an inner disciple physician and more like a spirit that had been awake all night counting the sick.

Her eyes moved first to the buckets.

Then to his limp.

Then to the cork between his teeth.

Jin slowly removed it.

For a moment, neither of them spoke.

The fog curled between them like it was listening.

Seolha’s gaze sharpened.

“Is that wine cork medicinal?”

Jin hid it in his sleeve.

“No.”

“Then why were you biting it?”

“I was not biting it.”

“There are teeth marks on it.”

“I was failing not to bite it.”

Her expression did not change.

That made it worse.

Jin lowered his head in the posture of a servant disciple receiving instruction. Small. Obedient. Harmless.

Seolha looked past the posture as if it had no skin.

“You received my message?”

Jin glanced at the basket.

At dawn, he had found a folded scrap of paper under his sleeping mat. No seal. No signature. Only three lines written in clean, narrow characters.

Medicine Pavilion.

Before sunrise.

You still owe unpaid work.

Doksu had been snoring beside the stove, mouth open, one hand clutching half a bun like he expected thieves to invade his dreams. No one else had stirred. Whoever placed the note had entered the servant quarters without waking anyone.

Jin did not like people who could do that.

“Yes,” he said.

Seolha turned without waiting for him to agree.

“Bring the buckets.”

“They are empty.”

“I know.”

Jin looked at her back.

`[Command structure detected. Subject assumes compliance.]`

He almost smiled.

The Ghost Core could analyze movement, breathing, threat patterns, and probably the precise angle at which a man intended to break his nose. Yet it still sounded faintly offended by social hierarchy.

Jin picked up the empty buckets and followed.

The path to the Moonlit Medicine Pavilion curved away from the Outer Yard and climbed toward a cluster of buildings built against the mountain’s western ribs. During the day, disciples came here groaning from bruised bones, cracked knuckles, poison training accidents, fever, failed qi circulation, and the many creative injuries produced when young people were given swords and pride before wisdom.

Before sunrise, the pavilion belonged to fog, herbs, and secrets.

Jin had been here before after the tunnel.

But unconscious did not count as visiting.

Now he saw the details.

The outer courtyard was paved with pale stone that reflected moonlight even when the moon had already faded. Small channels of water ran between the stones, carrying fallen petals from herb trees planted in square beds. Bundles of dried roots hung under the eaves. Bronze wind bells shaped like cranes chimed whenever the mountain breathed.

The place smelled of bitter leaves, clean ash, old blood, and something sweet enough to make suspicion feel reasonable.

Seolha led him through a side entrance rather than the main hall.

A sleeping junior physician sat at a desk near the wall with his forehead on an open ledger. Seolha passed him without slowing. Jin softened his steps.

`[Obstacle: sleeping witness. Noise threshold low. Recommended foot placement highlighted.]`

Pale lines appeared across Jin’s vision.

Not real lines. Not exactly. More like suggestions made of cold light.

Step near the crack.

Avoid loose board.

Shift bucket angle.

Jin obeyed without thinking.

The bucket handle almost clicked against the rim.

He caught it with his thumb at the last instant.

Seolha’s head turned slightly.

Not enough to show surprise.

Enough to show she had noticed.

They entered a narrow treatment room at the rear of the pavilion.

Seolha shut the door.

Then she placed one finger on the wooden frame.

A soft click sounded from inside the walls.

Jin’s skin prickled.

`[Formation activity detected. Function unknown. Escape routes reduced.]`

The room was small but painfully clean. A low examination bed stood in the center. Shelves of labeled jars lined the left wall. A brazier burned with smokeless blue flame. Beside it, a table held silver needles arranged by length on dark cloth. There were also knives thin as fingernails, threads of pale silk, porcelain cups, dried herbs, a basin of clear water, and three folded white towels.

No windows.

One door.

One physician between him and the only way out.

Jin set the buckets down.

The hollow sound felt like the room accepting him.

Seolha picked up one empty bucket and placed it near the wall.

“Good,” she said.

“For what?”

“If anyone asks, you came to carry waste water after a difficult night treatment.”

“You planned an excuse before I arrived.”

“I planned three.”

Jin looked at the door.

“Should I be grateful or afraid?”

“Yes.”

The answer was so calm that Jin did not know whether to laugh.

Seolha gestured to the bed.

“Sit.”

He did not move.

Her eyes lifted.

“I am not asking twice.”

Jin stood in the center of the room, hands at his sides, sleeves hiding the cork and the trembling in his fingers.

“You said unpaid medicine work.”

“I lied.”

“I noticed.”

“Then sit.”

`[Risk high. Subject Han Seolha possesses medical authority and possible evidence. Noncompliance may increase exposure.]`

Jin hated that the voice was right.

He walked to the bed and sat.

The wood was cold beneath him.

Seolha rolled up her sleeves.

“Remove your outer robe.”

“No.”

Her fingers paused above the needle cloth.

The silence sharpened.

Jin kept his eyes down but his voice steady.

“I know what you found in my blood cloth.”

Seolha did not answer.

“Black dust,” he continued. “Metallic. Not from normal injury. You kept it. You checked something after I left the infirmary.”

Her gaze became very still.

`[Warning: information disclosure exceeding safe threshold.]`

Jin ignored it.

He had spent the last two days learning a simple truth: when a person held a knife to your throat, begging did not make the blade disappear. Sometimes it only told them your throat was worth pressing.

Seolha held something over him.

He needed to make her understand that he had seen the shadow of her hand.

“Your master’s notebook,” Jin said softly. “You found a line inside it.”

Seolha moved.

Not toward the needles.

Toward him.

Her hand flashed so fast the Ghost Core only managed half a warning.

`[Threat—]`

Two fingers touched his throat.

Not hard.

Precisely.

Jin’s breath stopped.

His right arm twitched.

Seolha’s other hand caught his wrist before he could raise it.

The room froze around them.

Her fingers rested over a point below his jaw. One press, and he knew without needing the Ghost Core that his legs would fail.

“How much did you see?” she asked.

Jin could not swallow.

His eyes met hers.

For the first time since he had known her, Han Seolha looked less like a careful physician and more like someone who had survived by cutting the truth out of people before they could use it against her.

He should have been afraid.

He was.

But beneath the fear, something colder rose.

If she wanted to kill him, she would not have asked.

“Enough,” he said.

Her fingers pressed a fraction deeper.

Pain sparked down his neck.

`[Nerve compression. Motor function compromise imminent.]`

Jin forced himself not to move.

“I did not enter your room,” he said. “I did not steal your notebook. I saw your face when you left the infirmary. I heard one sentence before I lost consciousness.”

“What sentence?”

He breathed shallowly.

“Hide him before the Skyless Directorate comes.”

Seolha’s fingers did not leave his throat.

But something in her eyes changed.

Not softness.

Recognition.

Jin continued, carefully now.

“I do not know what the Skyless Directorate. I do not know what your master saw. I do not know why my blood looked wrong. But I know this: if you wanted to expose me, you would have done it yesterday. If you wanted to protect me, you would not have brought me into a sealed room and put needles on the table before explaining why.”

Seolha stared at him for a long time.

Then she removed her fingers from his throat.

Air returned to Jin’s lungs with humiliating hunger.

She stepped back.

“You are worse than I thought.”

Jin rubbed his neck.

“Weak?”

“Observant.”

“That is not usually an insult.”

“It is when you are too injured to survive what you notice.”

That silenced him.

Seolha turned and lifted a small porcelain cup from the table. She poured dark tea from a covered pot and handed it to him.

Jin did not take it.

“Poison?”

“Pain medicine.”

“You can call poison pain medicine if the patient stops hurting after death.”

A faint line appeared between her brows.

“Do you argue with everyone who treats you?”

“Only the ones who lock the door.”

Seolha placed the cup beside him instead of forcing it into his hand.

“Fair.”

The word startled him more than a denial would have.

She returned to the needle table.

“I locked the door because the walls of this sect have ears. Some belong to disciples. Some belong to elders. Some belong to people who are not supposed to be inside our sect at all.”

The false clerk’s face passed through Jin’s memory: ink-stained fingers, ledger smile, eyes too flat.

Seolha saw the flicker in his expression.

“You have seen one.”

“Maybe.”

“You lie badly when you are tired.”

“I lie better when people are not holding needles.”

“Then remove your outer robe before I lose patience.”

Jin looked at the tea.

The Ghost Core spoke before he could ask.

`[Vapor analysis: bitter root, snow mint, whitebone bark, trace mineral salts. No lethal toxin detected. Sedative probability: low. Analgesic probability: high.]`

It could analyze steam now.

That should have comforted him.

Instead, it made the room feel smaller.

Every new ability was a door opening in his skull, and he did not know who had built the corridor behind it.

Jin drank the medicine.

It was bitter enough to make his soul step backward.

Seolha watched him with the slightest satisfaction.

“Good. Now robe.”

This time, Jin obeyed.

He removed his outer robe and folded it beside him. The cold room air touched the thin inner shirt clinging to his bruised body. He hesitated before pulling that open too.

His chest and ribs were a map of recent damage.

Yellowing bruises spread beneath purple ones. A long scrape cut diagonally across his side from the tunnel collapse. Needle marks from the infirmary dotted his collarbone. The place behind his left ear still ached where the Ghost Core had buried its invisible root.

Seolha’s expression did not show pity.

Physicians learned not to waste pity where hands were needed.

But her fingers tightened around the first silver needle.

“Who treated your ribs when you were younger?”

“No one.”

“That was not a question that allowed pride.”

“It was not pride.”

She glanced up.

Jin stared at the wall.

“It was an answer.”

Silence.

Then Seolha stepped closer and placed two fingers against his sternum.

Her touch was light. Clinical. Still, Jin’s body nearly flinched away.

He hated that too.

Pain was one kind of wound.

Expecting pain from every hand was another.

Seolha noticed, of course.

She noticed everything.

“I am going to test your meridian response,” she said. “If you move suddenly, the needle may pierce wrong and damage you.”

“Will it hurt?”

“Yes.”

The honesty made him breathe out through his nose.

“How much?”

“Less than dying. More than sleeping.”

“Your bedside manner is famous, I assume.”

“No. My patients complain after they survive.”

Jin looked at her.

For half a breath, something almost like humor existed between them.

Then Seolha placed the first needle.

Fire entered his chest.

Jin’s hand clamped on the edge of the bed.

The wood creaked.

`[Pain spike. Meridian response detected. Foreign obstruction near Lung Gate node.]`

Seolha’s eyes narrowed.

She touched the needle’s end and turned it.

The fire became ice.

Jin’s vision blurred white at the edges.

“Breathe,” she ordered.

He tried.

His breath scraped.

The Ghost Core threw cold instructions across his mind.

`[Inhale four counts. Hold one. Exhale six. Do not contract abdomen. Relax jaw.]`

The cork.

Baek’s ridiculous cork.

Jin realized his jaw had locked tight enough to crack teeth.

He released it.

The pain did not lessen.

But it stopped owning him completely.

Seolha placed a second needle below his right collarbone.

Then a third near his ribs.

Each one awakened a different language of pain.

Some burned. Some numbed. Some made old memories flash without permission: mud under his cheek, boys laughing, Kang’s boot pressing his wrist, a woman’s hand tying red thread around his arm, the smell of smoke, the sound of someone whispering, “Do not let them measure him.”

Jin’s eyes snapped open.

Seolha looked up.

“What did you see?”

“Nothing.”

Her hand stopped over the fourth needle.

“Jin.”

It was the first time she said his name without rank or distance.

That almost made him answer.

Almost.

“I said nothing.”

`[Memory fragment detected. Origin: pre-implant neural scarring. Associated identity: Aera. Retrieval unstable.]`

His mother’s name struck harder than the needle.

Aera.

Master Baek had said it too.

Aera, what did you hide in your son?

Jin closed his eyes.

He remembered almost nothing of her clearly. A voice. Warm hands. The scent of rain on travel cloth. Red thread. A lullaby with no words, only humming. Sometimes, when the sect boys called him stray dog, he tried to remember her face and found only light behind a door.

Now strangers were saying her name like she had left knives buried under the world.

Seolha placed the fourth needle.

This one did not hurt at first.

Then something inside him answered.

A pulse.

Not his heartbeat.

Not qi.

A cold, hidden knock from behind his bones.

The silver needle vibrated.

Seolha went utterly still.

The Ghost Core’s voice cut through his skull.

`[Warning. External diagnostic contact interacting with seal lattice.]`

The needle bent.

Not much.

Only a finger’s width.

But silver should not bend that way inside flesh without force.

Seolha withdrew it immediately.

A bead of blackened blood rose from the puncture.

Jin stared at it.

Seolha caught the bead on white cloth.

The cloth stained red first, then gray at the edges.

Metallic dust shimmered inside.

“Again,” she whispered.

Jin’s mouth went dry.

“That is not a good physician word.”

She ignored him.

Her focus had changed. Before, she had been cautious. Now she was careful in a more dangerous way, like someone approaching a sleeping snake whose pattern matched a drawing in a dead person’s journal.

She lit another lamp.

Blue flame touched the room.

She took a fresh needle from a sealed case, not the ordinary cloth roll.

This needle was darker than silver.

Almost white, but with a moon-gray sheen.

`[Unknown alloy. Density estimate abnormal. Avoid contact if possible.]`

Jin leaned back.

“No.”

Seolha stopped.

“Do you know what is happening inside your body?”

“No.”

“Then let me find out.”

“No.”

The word came sharper this time.

Seolha’s eyes lifted to his.

Jin’s fingers curled around the bed edge.

“You said if I move, I could be damaged. You said there are ears in the walls. You said people who should not be here are here. Now your needle bent and my blood turned into dust, and you want to put a stranger needle into me because you recognize something you have not explained.”

His voice did not rise.

That made each word heavier.

“I have spent my life being measured by people who never asked permission. My strength. My weakness. My food. My punishment. My worth. You do not get to become another one because you are scared.”

The room became very quiet.

Seolha lowered the moon-gray needle.

For the first time, her face showed something unguarded.

Not shame exactly.

But impact.

Jin’s chest hurt with every breath. The ordinary needles still stood in him like small flags marking conquered ground. He felt ridiculous and exposed and suddenly afraid he had said too much.

Then Seolha bowed her head.

Not deeply.

Not like a disciple to an elder.

Just enough to acknowledge a line had been crossed before she crossed it.

“You are right,” she said.

Jin did not know what to do with that.

Most people in the sect, when told they were wrong by someone below them, responded with fists, laughter, or punishment. Seolha simply accepted the blade and did not pretend it had missed.

It made him more wary, not less.

She placed the moon-gray needle back in its case.

“I will explain what I know. Not everything. Some things are guesses. Some are dangerous to say aloud even here.”

“Start with the Skyless Directorate.”

Seolha’s mouth tightened.

“Not its full name. Names invite attention when formations are involved.”

“That sounds like superstition.”

“It is worse. It is administration with assassins.”

Despite himself, Jin almost laughed.

Seolha removed one of the needles from his chest with a swift twist.

Pain flashed, then dulled.

“My master, Physician Yun Saja, served the sect for thirty-two years,” she said. “Officially, he died from qi deviation after treating an elder. Unofficially, he died after investigating three patients with unnatural meridian damage.”

Jin listened.

The blue lamp hummed.

“Unnatural how?”

“Children born with healthy pulses who became crippled before they were old enough to cultivate. Servants with scars around meridian gates but no memory of surgery. Disciples whose bodies rejected ordinary qi in patterns too precise to be disease.”

She folded the stained cloth over the blackened blood.

“My master believed someone was writing restrictions into living bodies.”

Jin’s ribs felt suddenly too tight.

“Writing?”

“That was his word.”

`[Correlating: artificial seal lattice. Probability increased.]`

Jin wished the Ghost Core would stop sounding pleased when nightmare patterns became more accurate.

Seolha moved behind him.

“Do not move.”

Her fingers parted the hair behind his left ear.

Jin went rigid.

The implant site was small. Too small for what had happened. No obvious wound, no metal plate, no ugly scar. Only a faint dark line shaped like a curved thorn disappearing beneath the skin.

Seolha did not touch it at first.

She only looked.

Then she inhaled softly.

Jin’s stomach dropped.

“What?”

“I saw something similar once.”

“Where?”

“In a corpse.”

The room cooled.

Jin’s pulse pounded against the needles still in his body.

Seolha stepped back before he could demand more.

“It was not the same. The mark was larger, infected, and the patient had died before my master could open the skull. But the curve was similar.”

“What happened to the corpse?”

“Gone by morning.”

“Stolen?”

“Removed by people with authority to make theft look like procedure.”

The Skyless Directorate.

Jin did not need to hear the name.

He looked down at his hands.

They were shaking.

He curled them into fists until the trembling hid itself inside tension.

Seolha saw. Of course she saw.

But she did not comment.

Instead, she returned to the front and picked up a charcoal brush.

“I am going to map your response with ordinary needles only. You may refuse any point. If you lose consciousness, I stop. If black dust increases, I stop. If you tell me to stop, I stop.”

Jin studied her face.

“Why?”

“Because you were right.”

The answer landed awkwardly between them. In the sect, stronger people did not usually admit a weaker person had drawn a line. They stepped over it and called the crushed place discipline.

Seolha did not step.

That made her harder to understand.

“No. Why help me?”

Her hand stilled over the paper.

For a moment, the physician mask returned.

Then she set the brush down.

“My master raised me after my family sold me to pay tax debt.”

Jin had not expected that.

Seolha looked at the blue flame instead of him.

“I was eight. Too old to forget. Too young to protect myself. He bought my contract from a merchant caravan because he needed a herb girl and because, according to him, I looked at people like I wanted to poison them.”

“You still do.”

“I know.”

The corner of her mouth moved faintly and vanished.

“He taught me medicine. He taught me letters. He taught me to ask why a wound exists, not only how to close it. When he died, the sect told me grief made me imagine conspiracy. Then his notebooks disappeared from the archive. Then two of his former patients vanished. Then I found one page hidden behind the binding of an old pharmacology text.”

“The warning.”

“Yes.”

Hide him before the Skyless Directorate comes.

Jin felt the words settle differently now.

Not protection born from kindness.

Protection born from unfinished debt.

He understood debts.

They were cleaner than mercy.

Seolha picked up the brush again.

“So I am helping you because you may be the answer to the question that killed him. And because if I do nothing, you will either be taken, dissected, or turned into a weapon by men who write reports while children scream.”

Jin’s throat tightened.

The Ghost Core spoke, calm as winter.

`[Threat pattern recorded. Survival route recalculating.]`

He almost told it to be silent.

Then he realized something.

Seolha had not heard the voice.

Of course she had not. But when the Ghost Core spoke inside him, his eyes sometimes shifted. His breathing changed. His focus sharpened too suddenly.

Seolha was watching him.

A little too closely.

Jin lowered his gaze.

“I am still standing,” he said.

It was not an answer.

It was the only truth he could safely offer.

Seolha looked at him for a long second.

Then she nodded.

“For now.”

The needle map began.

She worked from the outer gates inward, placing and removing needles with terrifying precision. Each point asked his body a question. Each answer was pain. At the left wrist, Jin’s fingers went numb. At the right shoulder, old pain flared in his lower back. At the base of the sternum, he tasted iron. Near the navel, the Ghost Core’s voice fractured into static for three breaths.

`[Signal interference—seal lattice—adaptive resistance—]`

Jin gasped.

Seolha pulled the needle immediately.

The static vanished.

Sweat rolled down his temple.

Seolha drew a small mark on the paper.

“What happened?”

“Pain.”

“Do not lie.”

“Static.”

The word escaped before he could stop it.

Seolha’s brush stopped.

Jin cursed himself silently.

Her eyes lifted.

“Static?”

“In my ears,” he said.

“You heard it?”

“Yes.”

“Like insects?”

“No.”

“Like metal scraping?”

He hesitated.

She saw the hesitation and became still again.

“Like words failing,” Jin said.

The room seemed to lean closer.

Seolha did not ask the question he feared.

She wrote instead.

That mercy, if it was mercy, was sharper than kindness.

For the next hour, Jin learned new shapes of suffering.

Seolha found three major restrictions and seven smaller distortions. She did not call them blockages. Blockages were clumsy. These were not clumsy.

At the lung gate, qi entered and scattered.

At the lower dantian path, circulation turned aside before it could gather.

At the spine, near the seventh gate, something folded the meridian response back on itself like a road that punished travelers for reaching the bridge.

Jin watched the map take shape under her brush.

Lines. Dots. Notes. Cross marks.

His body turned into territory.

Not his territory.

Enemy territory.

Finally Seolha stepped back.

Her face had gone pale in the lamplight.

Jin tried to sit straighter and failed.

The medicine had dulled some pain, but exhaustion had teeth.

“What?” he asked.

She did not answer immediately.

She turned the paper toward him.

At first, he only saw a medical diagram: torso, arms, spine, meridian channels marked with careful characters. Then the Ghost Core highlighted patterns across his vision.

`[Detected structure: containment lattice.]`

The marks connected.

Not naturally.

Not randomly.

They formed a shape.

A cage.

Jin stared.

His mouth felt numb.

Seolha’s voice was very quiet.

“These were not made by accident.”

He already knew.

Hearing it still hurt.

“They are too old to be recent,” she continued. “Some scars are from early childhood. Others were reinforced later. Whoever did this understood meridians better than most sect physicians. They did not destroy your cultivation completely.”

Jin looked up.

“Why not?”

“Because destruction is easy to notice. This was designed to make failure look natural.”

Natural.

Broken Pulse Dog.

Trash root.

Servant bones.

The words returned one by one, each wearing the face of a verdict he had mistaken for truth.

A hundred insults returned, each one wearing a new face.

Not truth.

Evidence planted by someone else’s hand.

Jin laughed once.

It came out wrong.

Seolha did not move.

Jin covered his face with one hand.

For years, he had hated his body.

Not disliked. Hated.

He had cursed weak lungs that burned too quickly, meridians that refused qi, limbs that shook, bones that healed slow, a pulse that made elders look away with bored disappointment. He had swallowed humiliation because some part of him believed the sect was only saying aloud what his body had always known.

Useless.

Defective.

Born wrong.

Now a map on a physician’s table suggested something worse.

He had not been born a cage.

Someone had built one inside him.

The anger that rose did not feel hot.

It was colder than the Ghost Core.

Seolha must have seen something in his expression because she reached for the remaining needles.

“Jin.”

He lowered his hand.

His eyes were dry.

That seemed to worry her more than tears would have.

“Who?” he asked.

“I do not know.”

“Guess.”

“Guesses can kill the wrong person.”

“Then make a useful one.”

Her jaw tightened.

“The work is too advanced for Outer Yard physicians. Too precise for ordinary poisoners. It could be imperial medical officers, hidden Directorate technicians, a black clinic, or someone inside the sect with access to forbidden records.”

“Inside the sect.”

“I said could.”

Jin looked at the map again.

A cage.

Not a metaphor.

Not pity from a physician trying to soften a diagnosis.

A cage written in flesh.

`[Emotional spike detected. Decision-making impairment likely. Recommend stabilization.]`

Not now, Jin thought.

`[Clarify command.]`

Not now.

The Ghost Core fell silent.

Seolha removed the last needle from his side.

A thin line of blood followed. Normal red this time.

She pressed cloth to the point.

“Listen carefully,” she said. “This does not mean you can rip the seals open. If you force circulation through these paths, you may cripple yourself permanently or die before anyone can help. Whatever changed in you recently is interacting with the cage. That may give you chances. It also gives the cage something to resist.”

“The cage is not alive.”

“No. But good traps do not need to be alive.”

Jin hated that sentence because it sounded like Baek would approve of it.

Seolha began applying salve to the needle points.

The room’s blue flame softened toward gray.

Outside, the first true morning sounds reached the pavilion: footsteps in the main hall, someone coughing, the distant knock of a pestle grinding herbs.

Their hidden hour was ending.

Jin looked at the map.

“Can you remove them?”

Seolha did not answer quickly.

That was answer enough.

“Not now,” she said.

He closed his eyes.

“Can you help me use them?”

That made her pause.

“Use them?”

“If the cage redirects flow, then it has doors. If it has doors, it has hinges. If I cannot break it, maybe I can learn where it turns.”

Seolha stared at him.

Then, slowly, she gave him the look adults gave children who had said something either brilliant or stupid enough that history would decide later.

“You think like a thief.”

“I grew up with nothing.”

“That is not a denial.”

“No.”

For a moment, the Ghost Core’s interface flickered across his vision, outlining the diagram, rotating it, drawing ghost-lines through hidden paths.

`[Strategy viable in limited scope. Objective: exploit redirection rather than rupture restriction. Training protocol requires medical monitoring.]`

Jin kept his face still.

Seolha looked back at the map.

“I can reduce inflammation. I can strengthen the surrounding channels. I can hide some symptoms from ordinary examination. I can perhaps loosen minor distortions over time.”

“Major ones?”

“No.”

“Not ever?”

“I said not now.”

It was strange how much difference that made.

Not now was not no.

Not now was a door seen from far away.

Seolha folded the map once, then stopped.

Instead of giving it to him, she copied three simplified marks onto a smaller scrap.

“This is all you carry. If anyone finds the full map on you, you die and I die after answering questions.”

“Only after?”

“They are thorough.”

She gave him the scrap.

It showed three points: breath, spine, lower gate.

No full cage.

No names.

No obvious meaning.

Jin tucked it into the inner seam of his robe.

Seolha then handed him a small cloth packet.

“Powder. Mix a pinch with warm water after nightfall. It will make your pulse look duller during casual examination and reduce swelling.”

“Side effects?”

“Bitter mouth. Mild dizziness. If you take too much, vomiting, fever, and possibly death.”

“Medicine Pavilion never disappoints.”

“Do not be clever with doses.”

“I am clever with survival.”

“Survival includes reading instructions.”

He accepted the packet.

Then she held up a thin red thread.

Jin stopped breathing for half a second.

It was not his mother’s thread charm.

This thread was new, dyed with medicine pigment and tied around a small bone bead.

Seolha noticed his reaction.

“You recognize the color?”

“No.”

A lie.

Not his best.

She did not push.

“This bead contains bitter ash. If you crush it, the smell will make most tracking insects lose your scent for a short time. Only use it if you believe someone from outside the sect is following you.”

“Outside only?”

“Inside trackers use people more than insects.”

He took the thread.

Their fingers touched for the briefest moment.

Seolha’s hand was cool.

Jin expected himself to pull away.

He did not.

That small failure of suspicion disturbed him.

Seolha stepped back first.

“From today, you come here every third dawn to carry waste water.”

“Every third dawn.”

“If anyone asks, you are repaying medicine costs.”

“I have no money.”

“That is why it is believable.”

He almost smiled.

Then a sound came from beyond the door.

Two knocks.

Pause.

One knock.

Seolha’s face changed.

Not fear.

Calculation.

She swept the full meridian map into a drawer and closed it with her hip. The stained cloth went into the brazier. Blue flame swallowed it without smoke. The moon-gray needle case disappeared into her sleeve.

Jin reached for his robe.

`[Approaching individuals: two. One known voice probability: junior physician. Second unknown. Threat level uncertain.]`

Seolha pointed to the empty buckets.

“Stand there. Head down. Limp worse.”

Jin obeyed.

He hated how easily the shape returned.

Small.

Harmless.

Almost invisible.

Seolha opened the door just wide enough to fill the gap with her body.

A junior physician stood outside, eyes still heavy with sleep. Behind him waited a man in gray clerk robes holding a sealed tablet.

Not the same false clerk from before.

This one was older, with a mole near his lip and hands too clean for someone who claimed to handle records.

Jin’s vision tightened.

`[Unknown individual. Movement economy high. Ink stains artificial. Threat probability: elevated.]`

The junior physician bowed.

“Senior Sister Han, apologies. The Discipline Hall sent a records clerk. He says there are questions about the injured servant from the north storage incident.”

The gray clerk smiled politely.

“Only routine, Physician Han. A missing tunnel, broken seals, injured boys, and one servant disciple who seems to keep surviving accidents. You understand.”

Jin looked at the floor.

His grip tightened around the bucket handle.

Seolha’s voice remained cool.

“I understand that routine questions are usually asked during routine hours.”

The clerk’s smile did not move.

“Discipline does not sleep.”

“Then it must be very tired.”

The junior physician’s eyes widened like he wanted to be elsewhere immediately.

The clerk chuckled.

“A sharp tongue heals nothing, Senior Sister.”

“It keeps fools from touching clean rooms.”

He looked past her.

His gaze landed on Jin.

For an instant, the room seemed to narrow into that look.

The clerk’s eyes traveled over Jin’s bent posture, thin robe, bruised neck, empty buckets, lowered head.

A servant.

A weak one.

A boy repaying debt.

Then his eyes paused.

Not on Jin’s face.

On his jaw.

Jin realized his teeth were clenched.

Not visibly much.

But enough.

The cork training struck him from memory like Baek’s cane.

Relax.

He softened his jaw.

Let his mouth hang slightly dull.

Let his shoulders sag.

Let one bucket tilt as if he were too tired to hold it correctly.

The clerk’s gaze passed on.

`[Disguise correction effective.]`

Seolha stepped further into the doorway, blocking the view again.

“If Discipline wants my patient records, send a sealed elder order.”

“Patient records?”

“He came for medicine debt work. Unless Discipline Hall now investigates mop water.”

The clerk tilted his head.

“Medicine debt. Of course.”

His hand moved under his sleeve.

Jin saw only the start.

The Ghost Core saw more.

`[Object retrieval. Probable scent tablet or detection charm.]`

Jin’s heart lurched.

Seolha moved first.

She coughed into her sleeve.

The sound was small.

But the herb pouch on her belt burst between her fingers.

A wave of bitter, eye-stinging powder drifted into the hall.

The junior physician sneezed violently.

The clerk’s hand stopped.

Seolha frowned as if annoyed with herself.

“Clumsy. I warned them not to over-dry feverleaf.”

The clerk’s polite expression cracked for the first time. Only slightly. His nostrils tightened.

Tracking scent disrupted, Jin realized.

She had done it deliberately.

The clerk withdrew his hand empty.

“I will return with proper order.”

“During proper hours,” Seolha said.

He bowed.

Not deeply.

“Of course.”

Then he turned and walked away.

The junior physician sneezed twice more and stumbled after him, apologizing to everyone and no one.

Seolha closed the door.

The lock clicked.

For three breaths, neither she nor Jin moved.

Then Jin exhaled.

“That was not Discipline Hall.”

“No,” Seolha said.

“You recognized him?”

“No.”

“That is worse.”

“Yes.”

The Ghost Core whispered:

`[Threat pattern recorded. Survival route recalculating.]`

This time, Jin did not hate the sentence.

Seolha looked at him.

“Your appointments just became more dangerous.”

“Then cancel them.”

“No.”

He frowned.

“You just said—”

“I said more dangerous. Not optional.”

The answer should have angered him.

Instead, something in him settled.

Not trust.

Not yet.

But the shape of an agreement stood between them now, fragile and sharp.

Jin reached for his outer robe and dressed slowly. Every movement tugged at the needle points. Every breath reminded him that his body had been turned into a prison before he was old enough to name the bars.

When he finished, Seolha handed him the empty buckets.

“Take the rear path. Do not return directly to the servant quarters. Circle through the ash garden first. If anyone asks why you smell like feverleaf, say I made you clean a spill.”

“That is true.”

“Convenient lies often are.”

Jin took the buckets.

At the door, he paused.

The words came unwillingly.

“Thank you.”

Seolha looked at him as if he had offered her a weapon with the blade pointed both ways.

“You may hate me later.”

“I can do both.”

A faint breath escaped her. Almost a laugh. Almost not.

Then she opened the door.

Morning light entered thin and gray.

Jin stepped into it with empty buckets, sore ribs, three hidden points marked in his robe, medicine powder against his chest, a red thread around his wrist beneath his sleeve, and a cage drawn in memory behind his bones.

He walked the rear path as instructed.

The fog was lifting.

Disciples moved in the distance. Bells rang for morning drills. The sect woke, unaware that its Broken Pulse Dog had learned the shape of his leash.

At the edge of the ash garden, Jin stopped beneath a dead pine and looked back at the Medicine Pavilion.

For years, he had thought his body was a locked door.

Now he knew better.

It was a prison.

And prisons were made by people.

That was the first mercy of the diagnosis and the cruelest part of it.

That meant somewhere, someone held a key.

Jin’s fingers touched the small scrap hidden in his robe.

The Ghost Core’s voice returned, quiet and precise.

`[New objective available: map containment lattice.]`

Jin closed his eyes.

No.

Not map.

Maps were for people who wanted to understand roads.

He wanted to understand walls.

So he could break them later.

Behind him, inside the sealed treatment room, Han Seolha unfolded the full meridian diagram again.

The blue lamp guttered low. The silver needles lay cleaned and silent. The drawer that hid the moon-gray needle looked harmless, which made it more frightening.

Seolha stared at the pattern until the lines stopped looking like medicine and started looking like handwriting.

Then she dipped her brush and wrote one sentence in the margin.

Her hand did not tremble until the brush left the page.

Someone wrote a prison into your body.
