A banquet was just a battlefield that smiled before it cut you.

Jin Ryu learned this before the first lantern was lit.

He sat on the edge of a sickbed in the Moonlit Medicine Pavilion while Han Seolha wrapped his left forearm from wrist to elbow with cloth so white it made every bruise beneath his skin look like an accusation. The bandage was clean. The room was clean. Even the rain beyond the eaves sounded clean.

That was what made it feel wrong.

Clean things belonged to people who expected to survive the day.

"First rule," Seolha said. "Do not eat."

Doksu stood near the medicine cabinet, clutching three packets of herbs in both hands as if he had been asked to offer tribute to an angry god. He had not stopped frowning since the Iron Moon invitation arrived.

"That is not a rule," Doksu said. "That is common sense. The people who poisoned his rice are inviting him to dinner. Even I know the answer is no."

Seolha tied the bandage tight enough to make Jin's fingers twitch.

"The second rule," she said, ignoring Doksu, "is do not drink."

"Also common sense," Doksu muttered.

"The third rule is do not bleed."

Jin looked at her.

That one was harder.

Seolha's face did not change. The Moonlit Medicine Pavilion was quiet around her, washed in gray morning rain and the smell of boiled roots. Her pale sleeves were rolled to the wrist. A silver needle rested between her fingers, thin enough to disappear when she turned her hand.

"If you bleed inside Iron Moon Hall," she said, "they can collect it. If they collect it, they can test it. If they test it, they may confirm what Master Yun died trying to hide."

The Ghost Core stirred behind Jin's left ear with a cold needle of attention.

`[Risk assessment: blood exposure inside monitored space—critical.]`

Jin flexed his bandaged fingers.

"Then I will not bleed."

Doksu stared at him as if Jin had announced he would politely ask the rain not to fall.

"Brother," he said, "you bleed when you stand up too fast."

"Then I will stand slowly."

"That is not reassuring."

Seolha moved to Jin's right hand. The skin across his palm had split during the Thousand-Step Trial. Some cuts had closed. Others had not. She cleaned them again with a dark green paste that burned like fire being poured into a memory.

Jin did not flinch.

He had learned, over the past few days, that pain had different languages.

There was the blunt language of a fist.

The hot language of torn skin.

The deep, almost patient language of broken meridians trying to carry qi through a prison someone had written into his body.

And there was the newest language: the Ghost Core's silence whenever it calculated the chance of his death and found the number too high to comfort him.

It was silent now.

Seolha finished wrapping his palm and reached for a small porcelain cup.

"Rinse your mouth with this before you go. Do not swallow."

Doksu leaned forward. "What is it?"

"Bitter root, charcoal ash, and snow vinegar. It will dull his tongue."

Jin accepted the cup.

"Why would I want my tongue dulled?"

"Because if they use taste-based poison, you will not trust taste. If they use scent-based poison, you will not trust scent. If they use hunger, you will remember hunger is a hand they are trying to put around your throat."

Doksu's frown deepened.

"That is three different ways to make food scary."

Seolha looked at him.

"Food has always been scary. You were just lucky enough not to notice."

Doksu quietly set the herb packets down.

Jin lifted the cup and rinsed his mouth. The liquid was foul enough to make his eyes sting. He spat it into a basin. For several breaths, his tongue felt like dead wood.

The Ghost Core registered the change with clinical satisfaction.

`[Oral sensory interference detected.]`

`[Poison identification through taste: unreliable.]`

`[Recommend visual, thermal, vapor, and behavioral analysis.]`

Jin almost laughed.

Even his own tongue had become a battlefield.

Seolha brought a folded robe from the cabinet. It was not new, but it was cleaner than anything Jin owned. Dark gray cotton. Plain collar. No sect crest except the small black thread mark of an outer disciple stitched near the hem.

Jin touched the fabric.

"This is not mine."

"It belonged to a patient who died last winter. He has no need of it."

Doksu made a strangled sound. "Senior Sister Han, could you say that in a less cursed way?"

"No."

Jin took the robe.

The dead man's clothing fit better than his living body deserved.

While he dressed, Seolha turned away and spoke as if reciting a treatment protocol.

"Walk as if your right knee is worse than it is. Keep your left shoulder low. Let them think the Thousand-Step Trial damaged you more deeply than it did."

"It did damage me deeply."

"Then use the truth. Lies built on truth are harder to smell."

Jin tied the sash slowly.

`[Posture model adjusted.]`

A pale overlay formed across his vision: a ghost of his own body leaning slightly too much on the right, shoulder drawn inward, chin lowered to hide the steadiness in his gaze.

The model looked weak.

Not helpless.

Weak.

There was a difference.

Helpless invited kicks.

Weak invited confidence.

Confidence made enemies careless.

Doksu watched him from the cabinet.

"I should come with you."

"No."

The word left Jin before Seolha could answer.

Doksu's face tightened.

"You keep saying that."

"Because it keeps being true."

"If they drag you somewhere, who will shout for help?"

"If they drag me somewhere and you are there, they will drag you too."

"I am heavier."

"Doksu."

The larger boy looked away first.

For a moment the room held only the sound of rain tapping the eaves.

Jin softened his voice.

"I need someone outside the hall. Someone who can run to Seolha if I do not return by the second night bell. Someone Kang does not think is worth watching closely."

Doksu looked back.

"He thinks I am worth mocking. That is almost the same."

"Mocking is not watching."

Doksu swallowed.

"Second night bell?"

"If I am not out by then, do not come in. Run here. Tell Seolha. Then find Baek."

At the old man's name, Seolha's eyes narrowed slightly.

"You told him?"

Jin shook his head.

"He already knew."

"Of course he did," Doksu muttered. "The blind elder sees everything except his own wine stains."

A dry voice came from the open window.

"That is slander. I see my wine stains. I simply respect their right to exist."

Doksu jumped so violently he knocked one of the herb packets onto the floor.

Master Baek sat on the outer windowsill as if rain, height, and propriety were minor inconveniences intended for other people. His blind eyes faced the room. His wine gourd hung from one wrist. His robe was damp at the shoulders, but he looked more comfortable than anyone who had chosen to sit in rain had any right to look.

Seolha did not appear surprised.

Jin was not certain whether that meant she had heard him approach or had simply accepted that Master Baek appeared in places like weather.

Baek sniffed.

"Bitter root, charcoal ash, snow vinegar. Very good. He will hate everything he smells for six hours."

"That is the goal," Seolha said.

"Cruel woman. Useful woman. Same thing in medicine."

Doksu bent to pick up the herbs.

"Elder, are you here to stop him from going?"

Baek laughed softly.

"No. I am here to make sure he goes properly terrified."

Jin tied the cloth around his saber hilt.

"I am already properly terrified."

"No, you are privately terrified. That is different. Tonight you must wear fear where people can see it."

Baek hopped down from the windowsill into the room. His feet touched the floor without sound.

He walked toward Jin and tapped the old cracked saber at Jin's waist with one finger.

"Leave this behind."

Jin's hand tightened on the hilt.

"No."

"If you carry a blade into a banquet, they will call you disrespectful. If you leave it behind, they will call you harmless. Which insult feeds you more?"

Jin said nothing.

The saber had become more than a weapon after the Thousand-Step Trial. It was ugly. Cracked. Unsuitable for formal halls. But it had resonated with his damaged pulse. It had answered when the stele remembered his blood.

Leaving it felt like leaving a rib behind.

Baek's blind gaze rested on him.

"A blade can save your life once. Being underestimated can save it ten times. Tonight, choose the larger weapon."

The Ghost Core offered no contradiction.

`[Strategic assessment: visible weapon increases provocation probability by 41%.]`

`[Host survival improved by appearing unarmed.]`

Jin removed the saber from his waist.

His fingers lingered on the cloth.

Seolha took it from him and placed it beside the bed.

"If you need a weapon," she said, "use the room."

Baek grinned.

"Ah. The healer understands murder. Excellent."

"I understand anatomy," Seolha said.

"Same thing with better handwriting."

Doksu looked like he wanted to ask whether all adults in the sect were insane, then wisely decided he did not want the answer.

Baek reached into his sleeve and removed something small. He tossed it to Jin.

Jin caught it with his left hand.

A cork.

The same kind Baek had forced him to hold in his mouth while training his breath.

This one had a thin black thread tied around its center.

"Bite this before you enter the hall," Baek said. "Not inside your mouth. Between the back teeth. Light pressure. It will keep your jaw from tightening when they insult you. Anger shows first in the jaw."

Jin turned the cork between his fingers.

"You prepared this?"

"No. I prepared twelve. You keep ruining them."

Doksu whispered, "Why are corks becoming important to our lives?"

No one answered him.

Seolha stepped close one final time. Her voice lowered.

"Yun Saja's note said attend empty. Eat nothing untested. Bleed nowhere. Speak little."

"I remember."

"Remember one more thing."

Jin looked at her.

For a heartbeat, the cold healer mask slipped. Not much. Just enough for worry to show through the crack.

"If the Skyless Directorate confirms you tonight, the sect cannot protect you. Master Baek cannot protect you. I cannot protect you."

Baek's smile faded.

Doksu went still.

Jin felt the room tilt around those words.

Protection had never been something he trusted. The Obsidian Meridian Sect had protected his weakness only because weakness was useful to step on. The outer yard protected rules only when strong disciples used them as clubs. The Medicine Pavilion had protected him for three days, and even that had cost Seolha authority she might not get back.

But hearing her say cannot made the danger more honest.

Not dramatic.

Not story danger.

Real danger.

The kind that did not care if he had suffered enough already.

Jin bowed his head.

"Then I will make them doubt."

Seolha studied him for a long moment.

"Doubt is not safety."

"No," he said. "But it is time."

The Ghost Core pulsed once behind his ear.

`[Objective updated: survive banquet. Preserve blood secrecy. Acquire information. Maintain uncertainty.]`

Jin swallowed the taste of bitter root and fear.

Then he stepped into the rain.

Iron Moon Hall sat above the eastern training courts like a polished threat.

Most buildings in the Outer Yard had been repaired more times than they had been designed. Roof tiles never matched. Wall beams leaned in different directions depending on which storm had damaged them last. Doors stuck in summer and rattled in winter. The servant quarters smelled of old straw, smoke, sweat, and boiled millet.

Iron Moon Hall smelled of sandalwood, lamp oil, lacquered wood, and money.

Its black roof curved like a blade under the rain. Silver moon disks hung beneath the eaves. Lanterns burned behind red paper, turning the wet stone steps into a path of blood-colored reflections. Two inner disciples stood at the entrance in formal robes, swords at their hips and contempt hidden behind ceremony.

Jin stopped at the bottom of the stairs.

The Ghost Core began counting.

`[Visible sentries: 2.]`

`[Hidden breath signatures: 5 probable.]`

`[Roofline watcher: 1.]`

`[Unknown scan pulse: intermittent, low intensity.]`

Jin kept his head lowered.

His right knee trembled exactly as much as the posture model recommended.

Rain ran down his hair and along the side of his face. His clean borrowed robe was already dark at the shoulders. He looked like a patient who had escaped bed and wandered into a place too fine for him.

Good.

Let them see the wound.

Not the teeth.

Doksu waited two courtyards away near the old bell post, pretending to repair a broken umbrella. He had chosen the disguise himself. It was a terrible disguise. No one with eyes would believe Doksu knew how to repair an umbrella.

Luckily, no one important had ever believed Doksu was worth seeing clearly.

One of the entrance disciples looked Jin up and down.

"Outer disciple Jin Ryu?"

The title came out awkwardly, as if the disciple's tongue disliked touching Jin's name without adding dog after it.

Jin bowed.

"I received Senior Brother Kang's invitation."

The disciple extended a hand.

"Weapon."

Jin spread both hands slowly.

Empty.

The disciple's eyes sharpened with disappointment.

"No blade?"

"I was told this was a reconciliation banquet."

The second disciple laughed through his nose.

"You learn manners quickly after almost dying."

Jin lowered his gaze.

"Pain is a strict teacher."

The first disciple stepped forward to inspect him. Hands pressed along Jin's sleeves, sash, collar, boots. Rough enough to insult. Careful enough to search for needles, powders, hidden blades.

When the disciple's fingers brushed near the back of Jin's left ear, the Ghost Core chilled into absolute stillness.

Jin coughed.

Not a dramatic cough. A sick one. Wet enough to make the disciple recoil slightly.

`[Cough timing: successful.]`

The disciple wiped his hand against his own sleeve with open disgust.

"Filthy."

"I apologize," Jin said.

The apology tasted like ash. Useful ash.

They let him climb.

Each step hurt.

Not the theatrical limp he displayed for them. Real hurt. The Thousand-Step Trial had left his right knee swollen beneath the bandage. His ribs still complained when he breathed deeply. His meridians felt like old ropes dragged across stone.

The Ghost Core tracked his pulse, breath, temperature, muscular tremor, scan pulse timing, and the position of every visible blade.

It also tracked something harder to measure.

Hunger.

Jin had not eaten since morning. Seolha had forbidden solid food after noon, afraid the banquet would use his body against him. His stomach was a hollow animal by the time he reached the hall doors.

When the doors opened, the animal woke.

Warmth spilled out first.

Then light.

Then fragrance.

Roasted duck glazed in amber oil. Steamed river fish with ginger. White rice polished until each grain looked like a pearl. Beef ribs simmered until the meat fell soft from bone. Lotus root, sweet chestnuts, sharp pickled plum, wine warmed with cinnamon, sesame cakes glazed with honey.

The smell hit Jin so hard he almost forgot to limp.

Servant disciples ate what remained after everyone else. Thin millet. Sour vegetables. Rice scraped from pot corners. Meat only on festival days, and only if some senior disciple had lost interest in chewing.

Iron Moon Hall had placed a whole season's worth of food on low lacquered tables and called it apology.

Doksu's voice echoed in Jin's memory.

The people who poisoned his rice are inviting him to dinner.

Jin bit the cork hidden between his back teeth.

Light pressure.

Jaw loose.

Anger hidden.

Hunger hidden.

Fear visible.

The hall quieted when he entered.

Dozens of disciples sat along both sides, ranked by status. Outer disciples near the rear. Iron Moon Hall members closer to the central table. Two recorders from Discipline Hall sat beside a low writing desk, brushes ready. Musicians plucked strings near the far wall, playing a melody too gentle for the eyes watching Jin.

At the highest seat, Kang Taejun rose with a smile broad enough to shame a friend.

He wore dark formal robes embroidered with silver thread. His hair was tied high with a jade clasp. The bruising Jin had caused him indirectly during the recent disasters had faded, or had been hidden with powder. He looked handsome, controlled, generous.

That was the most dangerous mask Jin had seen him wear.

"Junior Brother Jin."

The hall shifted at the words.

Junior Brother.

Not dog.

Not servant.

A rank offered in public could become a collar if accepted wrong.

Jin bowed low enough to show respect, not low enough to show surrender.

His ribs stabbed him for the trouble.

"Senior Brother Kang honors me beyond my merit."

Kang's smile warmed.

"Nonsense. A disciple who completes the Thousand-Step Trial deserves recognition. Iron Moon Hall may be stern, but we do not deny achievement."

A few disciples murmured agreement.

Others watched Jin's face, waiting to see whether praise would make him greedy.

At Kang's right sat a young woman Jin had seen only from a distance: Seo Yiran, Iron Moon Hall's quiet blade.

She was not beautiful in the way people praised at market festivals. There was no softness arranged for comfort. Her face was clean, elegant, and still, like a polished mirror that reflected nothing unless it chose to. Her robes were pale gray with a single silver moon thread at the collar. Her fingers rested near a wine cup she had not touched.

Unlike Kang, she did not smile.

The Ghost Core marked her immediately.

`[Subject: Seo Yiran.]`

`[Breathing rate: stable.]`

`[Blink interval: controlled.]`

`[Threat type: analytical.]`

Jin did not look at her too long.

Analytical people noticed being analyzed.

Kang gestured toward the lowest table on the right side.

"Please sit. We prepared a place suitable for your condition."

Suitable meant visible to everyone.

Low enough to remind him of his station.

Near enough for Kang to study.

Far enough that if something happened, help would arrive late.

Jin walked there slowly. Two disciples near the table smirked as he lowered himself onto the cushion. He let the movement look painful because it was.

A servant placed dishes in front of him.

Too many.

Too rich.

Too fragrant.

The Ghost Core's overlay swept across each plate.

`[Steam pattern: normal.]`

`[Surface discoloration: none visible.]`

`[Powder residue: possible on left rim, confidence 32%.]`

`[Liquid analysis requires contact.]`

Contact meant risk.

Jin folded his hands in his lap.

Kang lifted his cup.

"Tonight," he said, voice carrying easily through the hall, "we correct misunderstandings. The Outer Yard has been shaken by rumors, accidents, and harsh words. A sect is a body. When one limb aches, the body does not cut it off. It treats the swelling."

A soft laugh moved through Iron Moon disciples.

Swelling.

That was what Jin had become to them.

An irritation.

Kang continued.

"Junior Brother Jin suffered many hardships. Some from his own weakness. Some, perhaps, from the carelessness of others. But after the Thousand-Step Trial, we must acknowledge that even a broken pulse can contain unexpected resolve."

Another murmur.

This one less amused.

Some disciples hated him more for surviving than they had for being weak.

Jin bowed his head from his low table.

"Senior Brother speaks generously."

"Generosity is easy between fellow disciples."

Kang's eyes glittered.

"Drink with me."

A servant stepped to Jin's side and filled his cup.

The wine was pale gold. Warm vapor rose from it in curling threads. It smelled sweet even through his dulled senses.

Every gaze in the hall found the cup.

Jin looked at it.

A cup could hold poison.

A refusal could hold insult.

An insult could hold punishment.

The Ghost Core whispered.

`[Decision node: drink / refuse / substitute / ritual disposal.]`

`[Optimal outcome uncertain due to social variables.]`

Baek's voice echoed from memory.

Being underestimated can save your life ten times.

Seolha's voice followed.

Speak little.

Jin lifted the cup with both hands.

Kang's smile deepened.

Seo Yiran's eyes narrowed by one breath.

Jin raised the cup toward Kang.

"Senior Brother Kang honors me."

Then he turned slightly, lowered the cup, and poured the wine onto the floor beside his table.

The music broke.

One string cried out of rhythm and died.

The hall went silent so completely Jin could hear rain striking the roof tiles.

Kang's smile remained.

Only the muscles near his left eye changed.

"Junior Brother Jin," he said softly. "Do you reject my toast?"

Jin placed the empty cup down and bowed his head until his forehead nearly touched his hands.

"I offer it first to those who could not attend."

Kang did not speak.

Jin kept his voice low. Low voices made listeners lean in. Leaning in made them complicit.

"Servant disciples who died carrying stones. Outer disciples who collapsed during winter drills. Weak children sent to trials before their bones finished growing. I have no family table to pour wine for ancestors, Senior Brother. Tonight Iron Moon Hall is generous enough to give me wine. I thought it proper to remember the weak before the strong drink."

No one laughed.

A few outer disciples near the back lowered their eyes.

Discipline Hall recorders hesitated, brushes hovering.

Kang's public face had three choices.

Punish Jin for honoring dead disciples.

Admit the dead were not worth wine.

Or swallow the insult and smile.

He swallowed.

Then laughed.

It was a beautiful laugh. Warm. Impressive. Almost convincing.

"Well said. Compassion survives even in harsh soil. Fill his cup again."

The servant obeyed.

Jin did not touch the second cup.

Kang drank from his own.

The hall drank with him.

Wine moved down throats. Food disappeared between teeth. Conversation returned, but it did not regain its old shape. It bent around Jin now.

He had poured one cup onto the floor and changed the temperature of the room.

Not victory.

Only disturbance.

Disturbance was enough to make hidden things shift.

The Ghost Core tracked reactions.

`[Kang Taejun: anger suppressed.]`

`[Seo Yiran: interest increased.]`

`[Discipline recorders: narrative uncertainty.]`

`[Gray-robed observer: not yet located.]`

Jin adjusted his sleeve and let his gaze wander like a nervous person's.

The hall was built for hierarchy. The central beams were black lacquer carved with moon patterns. Side cabinets held wine jars and scroll boxes. Behind Kang's seat, a silver screen showed the Iron Moon emblem: a crescent blade above dark water. Two side doors led to servant passages. Three incense burners stood at measured points, their smoke thin and pale.

The Ghost Core highlighted the smoke.

`[Airflow irregularity.]`

Jin breathed shallowly.

The bitter rinse dulled scent, but the Ghost Core did not need smell alone. Smoke moved according to heat, wind, bodies. The incense near the south pillar drifted left when it should have risen straight. A hidden gap behind the wall.

Someone watched from there.

Not a disciple seated in public.

A watcher.

Jin lowered his eyes to the food.

A servant placed a small dish of white fish closer to him.

"Senior Brother Kang asks that you taste the river fish," the servant whispered. "It is soft. Good for the injured."

The servant was young. Younger than Jin. His hand trembled.

Fear sweat shone at his temple.

Not poisoner.

Messenger.

Jin looked past him toward Kang.

Kang was speaking with a Discipline Hall recorder, but his attention rested here like a blade under silk.

Jin picked up his chopsticks.

The servant's shoulders loosened.

The hall watched.

Jin used the chopsticks to move one piece of fish from the plate to the small empty saucer beside it.

Then he set the chopsticks down.

He did not eat.

Kang's smile thinned.

Seo Yiran finally spoke.

Her voice was softer than Jin expected.

"Junior Brother Jin seems cautious."

Every conversation nearby died again.

Jin turned toward her and bowed from his seat.

"Senior Sister Seo is perceptive."

"Caution is wise after injury. But excessive caution can be mistaken for distrust."

"Then I hope Senior Sister will forgive my clumsiness. I have spent more years carrying food than receiving it. When too much is placed before me, I do not know which kindness to accept first."

A few disciples smirked.

Good.

Let them hear poverty instead of strategy.

Seo Yiran tilted her head.

"You completed the Thousand-Step Trial, yet cannot choose a piece of fish?"

"The stairs did not ask me to digest them."

Someone choked on wine.

Doksu would have loved that.

Kang's fingers tightened around his cup.

Seo Yiran did not laugh, but something near amusement passed across her eyes.

"Honest," she said. "Or practiced."

"I am too poor to practice honesty."

"Poverty practices everything."

That answer was too sharp.

Jin reassessed her.

Seo Yiran was not merely Kang's elegant ornament. Her mind moved independently. Maybe beside him. Maybe behind him. Maybe against him in ways Kang did not understand.

The Ghost Core marked her threat value higher.

`[Subject Seo Yiran: social probing advanced.]`

`[Recommend limited engagement.]`

Speak little.

Jin lowered his head.

"Then Senior Sister has seen more of the world than I expected."

Seo Yiran held his gaze a breath longer.

Then looked away.

The musicians resumed.

The banquet shifted into performances.

Iron Moon disciples demonstrated refined sword forms between courses. Not real fighting. Banquet fighting. Beautiful, controlled, harmless enough not to disturb the elders, sharp enough to remind everyone who owned the hall.

A disciple in blue performed the Crescent Returning Form. His blade curved through lantern light, each cut leaving silver afterimages. Another struck a sequence of palm techniques against hanging bronze disks, ringing them in order. Applause followed every flourish.

Jin watched like a weak disciple watching dreams beyond reach.

The Ghost Core watched like a thief.

`[Visual capture: Crescent Returning Form, sequence fragments 11%.]`

`[Bronze Disk Palm rhythm pattern mapped.]`

`[Warning: active reconstruction may trigger scan fluctuation.]`

Jin did not reconstruct.

Not here.

Not under hidden eyes.

But observation was not theft if no one noticed the hand.

He let his gaze drift to polished surfaces instead: bronze plates, wine cups, lacquered screens. Reflections showed angles he could not look at directly. The cabinet behind Kang opened twice. Once for wine. Once for a scroll tube.

On the second opening, the Ghost Core froze a sliver of reflected vision.

`[Document visible.]`

Columns of ink. Small characters. A seal.

Jin did not turn his head.

The reflection shimmered in the curved side of a wine jar.

`[Enhancement limited.]`

`[Attempting reconstruction.]`

Pain flashed behind his left eye.

He bit the cork.

Not now.

`[Partial capture.]`

A name appeared.

Not a full name.

A designation.

**Host-01**.

Then beneath it: **failed after third stair**.

The cabinet closed.

Jin's stomach turned colder than hunger.

Host-01.

If he was Host-03, there had been others.

And at least one had failed.

Failed did not mean returned to servant quarters.

Failed meant body count disguised as labor transfer.

The Ghost Core stored the fragment.

`[New data: Host-series confirmed.]`

`[Emotional response spike detected.]`

`[Suppress visible reaction.]`

Jin reached toward his cup as if thirsty, then remembered not to drink and let his hand tremble halfway there.

A weak person's hesitation.

A wounded boy's nerves.

A useful mask.

Kang noticed.

Of course he did.

"Junior Brother Jin," Kang called, "you seem unwell."

Jin bowed his head.

"My body has not fully recovered."

"Then perhaps entertainment will restore your spirit."

Kang clapped once.

The hall doors opened.

Two disciples stepped in carrying a low wooden frame. A cloth covered something placed upon it. They set it at the center of the hall.

Jin did not like the way Seo Yiran's fingers stilled.

She had not expected this.

Kang rose.

"As this banquet honors reconciliation, Iron Moon Hall has prepared a small gesture. Misunderstandings often begin with imbalance. One disciple suffers, another is accused, rumors grow teeth. To correct such imbalance, we offer public compensation."

He pulled the cloth away.

A tray lay beneath it.

On the tray: silver ingots, a folded outer disciple robe, three medicine bottles, and a narrow black case.

Gasps moved through the lower tables.

For a servant disciple, the silver alone was more wealth than Jin had ever held.

The robe was not servant gray. It bore a proper outer disciple mark.

The medicine bottles were labeled with Iron Moon's private seal.

And the black case...

The Ghost Core reacted before Jin understood why.

`[Material match: low-grade relay alloy trace.]`

Jin's left ear burned cold.

Kang smiled.

"Junior Brother Jin's completion of the Thousand-Step Trial proves his resolve. Therefore, Iron Moon Hall recommends that his status be formally reviewed. From servant labor rotation to recognized outer disciple training track."

The hall erupted.

Some outer disciples shouted in protest.

Others stared at Jin with naked envy.

Recognition.

Food.

Medicine.

Money.

A place closer to the fire.

All the things the weak were trained to crave.

Jin looked at the tray.

A trap did not always have teeth.

Sometimes it had warmth.

Kang continued over the murmurs.

"Of course, such promotion requires formal verification. A pulse review. A blood-stability test. Nothing invasive. Merely standard procedure."

There it was.

The hook inside the gift.

Seolha's rule became a blade in Jin's mind.

Do not bleed.

Kang gestured to the black case.

"Iron Moon Hall has invited a neutral medical assessor for fairness."

The south wall panel slid open.

Smoke bent toward the gap.

A man in gray robes stepped into the lantern light.

He looked ordinary at first. Middle-aged. Narrow face. Scholar's hands. A polite smile that did not belong to any emotion Jin recognized. His robe bore no sect crest, only a small jade plate at the waist carved with three broken rings.

The hall did not know him.

Several elders' attendants frowned.

Seo Yiran's expression cooled.

Jin's blood tried to hide inside his bones.

The Ghost Core went silent.

Not asleep.

Concealed.

The gray-robed man bowed to Kang, then to the hall.

"This humble one is Physician Lu, traveling assessor registered with the provincial medical office. I was asked to witness the stability of disciple Jin Ryu's channels before his status review."

Provincial medical office.

A clean lie.

Even Jin, who had spent most of his life being considered too stupid to educate properly, could feel how polished it was.

The man had not come to heal him.

He had come to confirm him.

Doksu waited outside.

Seolha was far away.

Baek had told him to go properly terrified.

Jin had thought he understood.

He had not.

Physician Lu opened the black case.

Inside lay three silver needles, a thin strip of pale paper, a small jade bowl, and a blade no longer than a fingernail.

Blood-stability test.

A slice small enough to call harmless.

Large enough to doom him.

Kang looked down from his raised seat.

"Junior Brother Jin," he said, voice smooth as oiled wood, "Iron Moon Hall offers compensation, recognition, and a chance to leave old humiliations behind. Surely you will not reject sincerity twice in one evening."

The hall watched.

This was better than poison.

Poison killed only the body.

This killed options.

If Jin refused, he insulted compensation and proved he was hiding something.

If he accepted, he bled.

If he accused Physician Lu, he challenged a neutral assessor without evidence.

If he ran, he became prey.

The Ghost Core did not offer an answer immediately.

It calculated.

Jin felt the weight of every gaze settle on his skin.

Then Seo Yiran spoke.

"Senior Brother Kang."

Kang's eyes flicked toward her.

"Yes?"

"You said compensation corrects imbalance."

"Indeed."

"Then should the compensation not be accepted before the test? Otherwise it is not compensation. It is payment for cooperation."

The hall shifted.

Kang's smile cooled by a fraction.

Jin did not move.

Seo Yiran lifted her cup, still untouched.

"Iron Moon Hall's reputation should not be dirtied by careless order of ceremony."

Kang stared at her long enough for three breaths to pass.

A crack.

Not friendship.

Not rescue.

Seo Yiran had not defended Jin.

She had defended form.

But form mattered in halls built on reputation.

Kang recovered.

"A fair correction. Junior Brother Jin may accept the compensation first."

The tray was carried to Jin's table.

Silver, robe, medicine, black case beside it.

Physician Lu remained standing at the center of the hall.

Patient.

Too patient.

Jin looked at the silver.

His stomach twisted again, but not from hunger this time.

He remembered being ten years old, standing outside the dining shed while older boys laughed and held rice cakes above his reach. He remembered counting copper coins in winter and deciding whether to buy thread for his torn shoe or save for medicine he would not be allowed to take. He remembered Doksu splitting a stolen bun in half and taking the smaller piece badly enough that Jin pretended not to notice.

Money could heal things.

Not everything.

But many things.

That was why it was dangerous.

Jin bowed toward the tray.

"Iron Moon Hall's generosity is more than I deserve."

Kang's expression brightened slightly.

"Then accept it."

Jin reached out.

Not for the silver.

Not for the robe.

Not for the medicine.

He picked up the folded document placed beneath the tray's edge.

A certificate of compensation.

His eyes skimmed the lines.

The Ghost Core captured everything.

`[Legal language detected.]`

`[Clause 4: acceptance confirms all prior conflicts settled.]`

`[Clause 5: recipient acknowledges injuries were accidental or self-incurred.]`

`[Clause 7: recipient consents to medical verification.]`

`[Clause 9: refusal after acceptance constitutes malicious slander.]`

Jin almost smiled.

The silver had been bait.

The robe had been bait.

The medicine had been bait.

The paper was the hook.

He placed the document back down.

"Senior Brother Kang is truly careful."

"Iron Moon Hall values proper records."

"Then I should be careful too. I am ignorant and injured. If I misunderstand such generous terms, I may shame both of us."

Kang's eyes narrowed.

"What do you mean?"

Jin bowed to the Discipline Hall recorders.

"May this low disciple request that the compensation document be read aloud for the hall before acceptance? Since Senior Brother Kang offers reconciliation publicly, accepting publicly should be proper."

The recorders froze.

Kang's smile died at the edges.

Several outer disciples leaned forward.

Seo Yiran looked at Jin again, and this time her expression changed.

Not amusement.

Recognition.

The hall had become a trap for the trap.

If Kang refused to read the document, generosity looked suspicious.

If he read it, everyone heard the clauses.

If he changed it, the recorders saw the change.

For the first time that night, Kang looked young.

Not weak.

But young enough to hate being outmaneuvered in public.

Physician Lu's polite smile did not move.

That was worse.

Kang slowly lifted one hand.

"The document contains standard language. No need to bore the hall."

Jin bowed lower.

"Then I accept Senior Brother's verbal generosity and request the written form be sent to Medicine Pavilion for review by Senior Sister Han. She has held my treatment authority since the trial. I fear signing while under treatment would make the record improper."

Treatment authority.

Seolha's shield.

A thin shield.

But official.

The Discipline Hall recorder on the left coughed into his sleeve.

"That is... procedurally sound."

Kang turned his head very slowly.

The recorder regretted existing.

Jin kept his forehead low.

Physician Lu finally spoke.

"The medical verification can proceed without signature. A simple prick of blood will clarify whether disciple Jin's channels are stable enough to receive such generosity."

His voice was pleasant.

A snake moving through grass would envy it.

Jin lifted his head just enough to look confused.

"This low disciple does not understand."

Physician Lu smiled.

"Blood reveals channel stress more accurately than touch."

"Then why does the Medicine Pavilion use pulse first?"

"Different schools have different methods."

"Then if Senior Sister Han's method differs, would testing me while I am under her emergency care not insult her school?"

A few disciples glanced toward the Discipline Hall recorders again.

Medicine Pavilion authority was not high in politics, but healers were dangerous to offend carelessly. Every disciple in the hall had been injured at least once. No one wanted their next fever treated by someone they had publicly humiliated.

Physician Lu's smile stayed fixed.

"I am sure Senior Sister Han would welcome additional certainty."

Jin nodded slowly.

"Then I will go ask her."

The simplicity of it landed like a stone dropped into a bowl.

Kang's fingers curled.

"Now?"

"This low disciple is ignorant. Senior Brother Kang offers kindness. Physician Lu offers testing. Senior Sister Han holds treatment authority. If all three are sincere, then all three can meet."

Silence.

A beautiful silence.

Terrible.

Thin as ice.

Jin could feel the hall deciding whether to laugh, gasp, or stop breathing.

Seo Yiran looked into her untouched wine cup.

Baek would have laughed openly.

Doksu would have fainted standing up.

Kang leaned forward.

For one moment, the mask almost tore.

Then he laughed again.

Not as warmly this time.

But enough.

"Junior Brother Jin has become very careful indeed."

"I learned from Senior Brother's example."

The compliment cut because it could not be punished.

Physician Lu closed the black case.

Jin heard the tiny click like a door locking.

The gray-robed man bowed.

"Then perhaps another evening. It would be improper to rush healing."

Improper.

The word everyone used when knives had to be hidden again.

Kang waved the tray away.

"Send the documents to Medicine Pavilion. The silver and medicine will wait until review. We would not want Junior Brother Jin overwhelmed by gratitude while ill."

Laughter returned to the hall, nervous and obedient.

Jin bowed.

"Senior Brother's consideration is boundless."

Kang's eyes promised that consideration had limits and Jin would meet them soon.

The banquet resumed.

But nothing tasted the same.

Not that Jin tasted anything.

He sat through two more performances, three more toasts, and one poem about moonlight reflected on loyal blades. He applauded when others applauded. He lowered his eyes when Kang looked his way. He trembled when expected. He breathed shallowly when scan pulses brushed the hall.

Physician Lu did not leave immediately.

He moved to a side table and accepted tea he did not drink.

From time to time, Jin felt his gaze touch the back of his skull.

Not the way Kang watched.

Kang watched like a man trying to decide where to strike.

Physician Lu watched like someone waiting for an instrument to produce a reading.

At the second night bell, the sound rolled faintly from the outer courtyard.

Doksu would be panicking.

Jin needed to leave.

Kang gave him the chance five breaths later.

"Junior Brother Jin," he called, "your face grows pale. Iron Moon Hall will not keep an injured guest past his strength."

A guest.

Now he was a guest.

Earlier he had been compensation.

Tomorrow he would be rumor.

Jin bowed from his seat.

"Forgive my weakness."

"Two disciples will escort you."

There it was.

The second trap.

Jin looked toward the doors.

Two Iron Moon disciples rose. Both familiar. Cho and Min.

Cho's knuckles were still bandaged from punching the stone basin after Jin's first calculated dodge. Min's mouth twisted with restrained delight.

They were not there to help him walk.

They were there to walk him somewhere else.

Jin bowed again.

"Senior Brother is kind."

He stood slowly.

His knee nearly buckled for real.

Cho grabbed his left arm.

Too tight.

A bruise opened under the pressure, but skin did not break.

Min took the right.

They guided him out beneath the watching hall.

At the threshold, Jin felt Seo Yiran's gaze follow him.

Not pity.

Calculation.

Perhaps warning.

Perhaps interest.

Perhaps both.

Rain had softened to mist outside.

The stone path shone black beneath the lanterns. Cho and Min walked him past the main stairs.

Not down toward the outer courts.

Left.

Toward the side passage between Iron Moon Hall and the old record storehouse.

`[Route deviation detected.]`

Jin let his feet drag.

"The Medicine Pavilion is down."

Cho smiled.

"Shortcut."

Min leaned closer.

"You like rules now, Broken Pulse? Let's see how well rules hear you in the dark."

Jin's breath stayed uneven.

Fear visible.

Hands loose.

Jaw loose.

Cork hidden.

The side passage narrowed between wet stone walls. Lantern light faded behind them. The rain smell gave way to moss and old paper. Somewhere inside the record storehouse, wood creaked.

`[Potential ambush zone.]`

`[Visible enemies: 2.]`

`[Unknown presence: probable ahead.]`

They shoved him through a half-open gate into a small courtyard used for storing broken benches and empty wine jars. The moon was hidden. The only light came from the hall behind them and a small covered lamp near a drainage channel.

Cho released Jin by throwing him forward.

Jin stumbled, caught himself on a broken bench, and let a hiss of pain escape.

Min shut the gate.

Cho cracked his neck.

"You embarrassed Senior Brother."

Jin straightened slowly.

"He invited me."

"He invited a dog to sit. Dogs should know better than to speak."

Min stepped to the side, cutting off the drainage path.

"No blood," Cho said. "Orders. Bruises only."

Jin's thoughts sharpened.

Orders.

Kang wanted punishment.

But no blood.

Because blood was dangerous to collect here? Or because Physician Lu still needed him unspoiled?

Either way, their restriction was a gift.

Cho lunged.

The Ghost Core offered three options.

Dodge left: knee failure probability 63%.

Duck: rib compression severe.

Fall.

Jin fell.

Not collapsed. Chosen.

Jin rolled badly, ugly, shoulder striking stone. Pain burst white across his vision.

Min kicked at his ribs.

Jin dragged a wine jar into the path.

Min's foot hit ceramic instead. The jar shattered. Wine dregs spilled across the stone.

`[Surface slickness increased.]`

Jin coughed and tried to rise.

Cho grabbed him by the collar.

"Still doing tricks?"

Jin's left hand found a shard of broken ceramic.

Too sharp.

If he used it, blood.

Bad.

He let it go.

Cho lifted his fist.

A voice spoke from beyond the gate.

"If you break his face, the records will not match."

Cho froze.

Min stepped back.

Physician Lu stood outside the gate, gray robes untouched by mist. He had moved without sound.

Jin remained half-collapsed against the stone.

His breath hitched for real this time.

"Disciple Jin. You seem prone to accidents."

Jin lowered his head.

"This low disciple has always been clumsy."

Read.

Not heard.

Read.

Not respectfully.

Fearfully.

The Ghost Core went dead silent.

No blood.

Only bitter saliva.

Then smiled.

Aera.

Yun Saja.

Sealed stars.

Host-01.

Physician Lu did not.

Not touching.

Almost.

A test of flinch distance.

No blood.

"Fearful. Hungry. Proud only when watched. Weak when alone."

"Yes?"

Host-01.

Failed after third stair.
