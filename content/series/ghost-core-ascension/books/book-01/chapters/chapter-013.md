The Moonlit Medicine Pavilion was the only hall in the Obsidian Meridian Sect that feared silence more than screams.

After the second night bell, the rest of the mountain pretended to sleep. Training Yard lanterns sank into darkness. The Outer Yard dining hall became a black shape beneath the pines. Iron Moon Hall stayed bright longer than most, because rich disciples could turn wine, threats, and humiliation into something they called cultivation.

But the Medicine Pavilion never truly closed.

Poison did not wait for morning permission. Broken bones did not grow patient because an elder had retired to his chamber. A dying man did not knock politely before staining the floor.

So when Jin Ryu limped through the courtyard with Ma Doksu's arm under his shoulder and his pride held together by bitten cork and stubborn silence, three lamps were already burning beneath the eaves. Their pale light passed through oiled paper screens painted with bamboo shadows, turning the treatment room beyond into a place that looked gentle from far away.

Jin had learned not to trust gentle-looking places.

Han Seolha stood beneath the center lamp.

She had changed out of her ordinary outer robe into a narrow-sleeved medical robe tied tight at the waist. Her hair was lifted and pinned with a silver needle. A tray waited beside her with bowls of clear water, black cloth, thin knives, rolled bandages, and two sealed needle boxes.

She did not ask whether the banquet had gone well.

Her eyes moved once over Jin's face, throat, hands, ribs, knees, sleeves, shoes, and the false fever blister behind his left ear.

Then she looked at Doksu.

"Inside," she said.

Doksu swallowed. "Senior Sister Han, he did not eat. He did not drink. He did not bleed. Mostly."

"Mostly is not a medical category."

"He also insulted them while pretending not to."

"That is not a medical category either."

"It should be," Doksu muttered. "It explains many injuries."

Jin's mouth twitched.

Seolha's gaze cut to him. "Do not smile. Your lower lip is split inside."

Jin stopped smiling.

Doksu helped him across the stone threshold into the treatment room. Bitter herbs, rain-damp wood, and clean cloth wrapped around Jin with such force that, for one foolish moment, he almost mistook the smell for safety.

The Ghost Core corrected him without mercy.

`[Safety estimation: unstable.]`

A translucent diagram flickered across his vision.

`[Confirmed external threats: Iron Moon Hall. Directorate-linked physician. Unknown host-series monitoring. Poison protocols active. Social containment active.]`

Then a smaller line appeared beneath it.

`[Current local trust anchors: Han Seolha. Ma Doksu. Master Baek: conditional.]`

Jin almost laughed again.

Conditional was a polite word for an old drunk who could either save his life or throw him off a cliff and call it instruction.

“Sit,” Seolha ordered.

Jin obeyed because his legs had already begun negotiating surrender.

The room was warmer than the courtyard. A brazier burned low in one corner. Dried herbs hung from the beams in bundles, their shadows long and thin across the walls. A screen divided the treatment bed from the shelves where jars, scroll tubes, and sealed medicine boxes stood in rows. Everything was clean. Everything had a place.

Jin envied the jars.

At least they knew what they contained.

Doksu tried to remain standing beside the door, but Seolha pointed at the bench without looking at him.

“You too.”

“I am not injured.”

“You walked him through the rain while pretending you did not care. Sit before you fall down and make me treat stupidity.”

Doksu sat.

Jin stared at the floor.

Seolha knelt in front of him and began unfastening the borrowed gray outer robe. Her hands were efficient. Not cold, not gentle enough to be comforting, not rough enough to be cruel. She touched cloth, not skin, whenever she could. When she did touch injury, it was only long enough to learn what the body refused to say.

“Ribs,” she said.

“Bruised.”

“Worse.”

“Aggravated bruising.”

“Again.”

Jin sighed. “Cho and Min pressed me into a side courtyard and hit me without drawing blood.”

Doksu’s hands curled into fists.

Seolha’s expression did not change, but the needle pinned in her hair trembled once.

“Names confirmed?”

“Yes.”

“Witness?”

“Physician Lu arrived after.”

That did change her expression.

Only slightly.

The room seemed to become colder around the edges.

“Lu?”

“He called himself a provincial assessor.”

“Describe him.”

Jin did. Gray robe. Clean hands. Flat eyes. Three broken rings carved into pale jade at his belt. Voice like a covered blade. Words arranged to measure response instead of meaning.

Doksu added what he had seen from a distance. The angle of Lu’s entrance. The way Cho and Min stopped breathing when he appeared. The fact that Kang’s men looked less like disciples and more like dogs hearing a whistle.

Seolha listened with the stillness of a pond before a stone drops.

When they finished, she opened the first needle box.

Inside were ordinary treatment needles, silver and thin as winter grass.

She did not use them.

She opened the second box.

Doksu leaned forward despite himself.

The needles inside were black.

Not iron. Not lacquered wood. Not anything Jin had seen in the Medicine Pavilion before. They were shorter than ordinary needles, matte rather than shining, with tips so fine his eyes struggled to keep them in focus. Each needle rested in its own groove on white cloth. There were only nine.

The Ghost Core reacted before Jin could.

`[Unknown medical instrument detected.]`

`[Composition scan blocked.]`

A faint pressure built behind Jin’s left ear.

Seolha’s gaze snapped to his face.

“Stop.”

Jin froze. “I did not do anything.”

“I am not speaking to you.”

The pressure vanished.

Doksu stared between them. “Should I pretend that made sense?”

“Yes,” Jin and Seolha said together.

Doksu leaned back. “Good. I am very good at surviving by not understanding things.”

Seolha lifted one black needle with tweezers carved from bone.

“These are Silent Meridian Needles,” she said, and her voice lost the last trace of ordinary physician calm.

Jin looked at the needle. “They sound illegal.”

“They are not illegal.”

“That means worse.”

“They are restricted.”

“That means illegal for poor people.”

For the first time since he entered, Seolha almost smiled.

Almost.

“Silent Meridian Needles do not stimulate qi the way normal needles do. They do not open channels. They do not guide medicine. They do not force circulation.” She held the needle between them. It drank the lamplight. “They interrupt.”

Jin’s humor faded.

Seolha placed the needle on black cloth and pulled his sleeve back from his bruised forearm. “Used properly, they can quiet a trembling meridian long enough to prevent internal damage. Used improperly, they can silence a limb. Used cruelly, they can stop a heart without leaving a wound large enough for an untrained coroner to see.”

Doksu stood halfway.

“Sit,” Seolha said.

He sat, but his eyes stayed on the needle.

Jin’s own eyes stayed on Seolha.

“Why are you using them on me?”

“Because normal treatment will light you up.”

The Ghost Core displayed a pulse diagram beside her words.

Seolha pointed to Jin’s wrist. “Your body has two problems now. First, the banquet injuries. Second, concealment strain. Whatever happened when Physician Lu looked at you caused your pulse to fold inward.”

`[Concealment protocol triggered: external host-series pattern recognition risk.]`

Jin kept his face still.

Seolha watched him anyway.

“I will not ask what your thing called it,” she said quietly. “But I can feel the aftermath. Your meridians are behaving like men trapped in a burning house pretending the windows are walls.”

Doksu stared at the floor as if trying very hard not to hear the shape of the truth.

Jin lowered his voice. “Can you fix it?”

“No.”

The answer landed cleanly.

No kindness. No false hope. No pretty cloth wrapped around a blade.

Seolha continued, “I can prevent the damage from becoming permanent. I can make the bruising heal cleanly. I can reduce the risk that your sealed channels tear themselves open at the wrong moment. I can hide some of the noise your body is making from people who know how to listen.”

“And the rest?”

“The rest is why my master died.”

Silence settled.

Outside, rain slid along roof tiles.

Jin remembered Yun Saja’s hidden note. The boy with sealed stars. A vessel, not an accident. His weakness not as failure but as construction.

A prison written into flesh.

He looked at the black needle again.

“What do you need me to do?”

“Do not move. Do not let the Ghost Core assist unless I tell you. Do not try to memorize the pattern.”

The pressure behind his ear stirred again.

Seolha’s eyes hardened. “I mean it.”

Jin swallowed. “Why?”

“Because if it learns this without understanding restraint, it will give you a killing method before you have the heart to survive using it.”

The Ghost Core said nothing.

That frightened Jin more than argument would have.

He placed both hands on his knees.

“I will not memorize it.”

Seolha looked at him for a long moment.

“Say it properly.”

Jin understood.

Not to her.

To the thing inside his skull.

He exhaled slowly.

“Ghost Core. Do not record Han Seolha’s needle pattern without permission.”

`[Command conflict: preservation of host survival priority.]`

Jin’s jaw tightened.

“Override. Do not record.”

A pause.

`[Manual host restriction accepted.]`

Another pause.

`[Observation mode limited.]`

Seolha’s shoulders loosened by the smallest degree.

Doksu whispered, “I am going to pretend the air did not just become more obedient.”

“Wise,” Jin murmured.

Then Seolha inserted the first needle.

Pain did not come immediately.

That was the worst part.

Jin felt only pressure at first, a soft black punctuation mark placed between two beats of his pulse. Then the needle seemed to disappear. Not physically; he could see it standing from the inside of his wrist. But his body forgot to notice it.

A warm line that had been throbbing up his forearm went quiet.

Too quiet.

His fingers numbed.

His breath caught.

“Do not panic,” Seolha said.

“My hand vanished.”

“It did not.”

“It has different information.”

“Then believe your eyes until your nerves stop lying.”

She inserted the second needle near his elbow.

This time pain arrived in a thin white thread that ran straight to his teeth. Jin bit down on the cork Baek had given him. The cork had already saved him from anger at the banquet; now it saved Seolha from hearing a noise he did not want to make.

The third needle went near the collarbone.

The room tilted.

`[Neural distress rising.]`

Jin ignored it.

The fourth needle touched the line beneath his ribs where Cho’s fist had landed.

For one instant, the locked prison inside his meridians became visible—not as an image, but as a shape his body remembered. Threads. Rings. Cold knots wrapped around channels that should have grown with him. He saw a diagram in darkness: his own body drawn by someone who did not love him, then corrected by someone who did.

A woman’s voice passed through the pain.

*If they cannot use you, they may let you live.*

Jin’s eyes opened.

Seolha’s face was close.

“Memory?”

He nodded once.

“Aera?”

He nodded again.

Something crossed Seolha’s expression. Not pity. Pity was soft and useless. This was anger made quiet so it could last longer.

“My master wrote that the seal was modified,” she said. “The original containment lattice was cruel, but precise. Later someone changed it. Sloppier. More desperate.”

Jin breathed through the cork.

“Mother?”

“I do not know.”

“Guess.”

Seolha looked down at the needle in her hand.

“Someone sealed you to hide you. Someone else damaged the seal to make you useless. Those may be the same person at different times, or enemies fighting over your body without asking you.”

Jin closed his eyes.

He wanted to hate one person.

One clean target.

Kang. Physician Lu. Director Yeom. A faceless Directorate. A mother who had hidden too much. A sect that had watched him crawl for years.

But his life had not been broken by one hand.

It had been passed between hands until no one needed to grip tightly anymore. The damage remained by habit.

The fifth needle entered.

His left arm went cold.

The Ghost Core flickered.

`[Signal interference detected.]`

`[Silent Meridian Needle: suppressive principle inferred at 12%.]`

Jin forced the thought away.

Do not record.

The text vanished.

Seolha did not look up, but she said, “Good.”

Doksu rubbed both hands over his face. “I am definitely going to die because of secrets I do not understand.”

“No,” Jin said around the cork.

Doksu looked at him.

Jin removed the cork carefully. “You will die because you keep following me.”

“That is exactly what I said, only with more friendship.”

“I told you we are not friends.”

“You also told Iron Moon Hall you were too ignorant to understand compensation clauses. Your words are not reliable.”

A breath escaped Jin that almost became a laugh.

It hurt enough to stop him.

Seolha inserted the sixth needle.

Then the room changed.

Not visibly. The lamps did not flicker. The rain did not pause. The brazier continued breathing red beneath ash.

But the Ghost Core went suddenly, absolutely still.

No warning line.

No pulse map.

No translucent diagrams.

Jin felt its absence the way a man in a dark cave feels the torch go out.

“Seolha.”

She had already noticed.

Her fingers hovered over the seventh needle.

Doksu stood.

This time, she did not tell him to sit.

A sound came from the roof.

Not a footstep.

A correction.

As if someone had placed weight on a tile, noticed the tile would speak, and removed the weight before sound finished being born.

Seolha blew out one lamp.

The room dimmed.

Jin reached for a weapon that was not there.

At the edge of the screen, a shadow moved.

A needle struck the wooden pillar beside Seolha’s head.

It made no sound at all.

The black tip sank halfway into seasoned wood and stopped, trembling without a whisper.

Doksu opened his mouth.

Seolha’s hand flashed across the room.

A silver needle pinned his sleeve to the bench.

“Down,” she breathed.

Doksu dropped.

Jin ripped the Silent Meridian Needles from his own arm.

Pain returned like a crowd kicking down a door.

The Ghost Core slammed back online.

`[Threat detected.]`

Too late.

The treatment room window folded inward.

Not shattered. Not broken dramatically. The paper screen simply split along four quiet lines, and a figure stepped through as if the room had invited him.

He wore plain gray cloth from throat to wrist. No sect badge. No clan mark. No metal decoration that might catch lamplight. A soft gray mask covered the lower half of his face, and his hair was tied under a dark wrap. In his left hand he held three black needles between his fingers. In his right, a short blade darkened with ash.

He had no killing intent.

That was the first thing that saved Jin.

There was no heat. No anger. No hunger. No pressure of hatred for the Ghost Core to catch early.

The man looked at them the way a clerk looked at an inventory shelf.

Then his gaze settled on Jin.

`[Threat classification: retrieval specialist.]`

Jin’s stomach tightened.

Not assassin.

Retriever.

The man moved.

Seolha threw a tray.

It was not meant to hit him. The bowls, knives, cloth, and medicine water exploded across the path between them, creating reflection, obstruction, and noise. The gray man adjusted without blinking. His blade passed through falling cloth and cut only what mattered: the cord holding the treatment room’s inner curtain.

The curtain dropped between Seolha and Jin.

A black needle came through it.

Jin fell sideways.

The needle touched his hair and buried itself in the wall.

`[Projectile path: non-lethal. Target: left cervical access point.]`

Behind the left ear.

Jin’s blood went cold.

The Ghost Core’s implant mark pulsed once as if offended.

Doksu grabbed a stool and charged.

“Don’t!” Jin shouted.

Too late.

The gray man pivoted. His sleeve brushed Doksu’s wrist. Not a strike. Barely a touch.

Doksu’s arm dropped dead at his side.

His momentum carried him forward into the floor.

He hit hard, teeth clenched around a curse.

Seolha’s voice cut through the curtain. “He is using silence points!”

Of course he was.

The same principle.

Not healing.

Not qi guidance.

Interruption.

A cruelty shaped like medicine.

Jin stumbled toward the shelf where Seolha kept spare knives.

The gray man’s blade arrived first.

It slid beneath Jin’s chin, close enough that the skin felt cold before it felt sharp.

Jin froze.

The man’s eyes were brown.

Ordinary.

That was worse.

“Host-03,” the man said softly.

Doksu made a sound from the floor.

Seolha went still behind the curtain.

Jin did not answer.

The gray man tilted his head. “Conscious. Stable after first stair. Concealment response present. External attachment patterns confirmed.”

External attachment.

Jin understood at once.

Seolha.

Doksu.

Baek.

The Skyless Directorate was not only testing his body now.

It was measuring who could be used against him.

The man lifted his left hand toward Jin’s ear.

`[Capture risk: critical.]`

Jin moved before the Ghost Core finished.

Not forward.

Down.

The blade opened a shallow line beneath his jaw instead of his throat. Blood warmed his skin. Not much. Enough.

The gray man’s eyes sharpened.

Jin slapped the shelf.

Jars fell.

Dried roots, powdered bark, bitter resin, and white ash burst across the floor.

The gray man stepped around the first spill.

Jin had already learned from carrying water.

People watched what might cut them.

They forgot what might slide.

His heel dragged through spilled medicine powder and kicked the brazier ash into the wet resin.

The floor became slick black paste.

The gray man’s foot shifted half an inch more than intended.

Half an inch was not victory.

For Jin Ryu, it was a doorway.

`[Opening: 0.32 seconds.]`

Jin slammed his shoulder into the treatment shelf.

Pain tore through his ribs, but the shelf tilted. Heavy jars crashed toward the gray man’s left side.

The man chose efficiency. He cut the shelf support with one short blade motion and let the falling weight collapse away from him.

But that motion took his right hand across his own center line.

Seolha’s warning flashed in Jin’s mind.

*Silent Meridian Needles interrupt. Used cruelly, they can silence a limb.*

He had promised not to record her pattern.

He had kept that promise.

But he had felt the treatment.

He had felt where his own nerves vanished.

The Ghost Core did not display a technique name.

Instead, it showed a single point on the gray man’s wrist.

`[Target: inner gate node.]`

`[Effect: temporary grip failure.]`

`[Fatal deviation risk if force exceeds threshold: 23%.]`

Jin’s breath stopped.

Twenty-three percent.

Not a number.

A grave with room for two people.

The gray man recovered his footing.

His blade rose.

Jin’s cracked saber was not in his hand.

His body was bruised. His meridians were sealed. Six black needles had just turned his nerves into confused ghosts. He had no power, no time, no clean path.

But he had one thing the Skyless Directorate had not accounted for.

He had lived so long as a weak person that throwing away pride felt like breathing.

Jin let himself fall.

Not dodge.

Fall.

His knees gave way. His shoulder scraped the floor. The gray man’s blade adjusted downward, perfectly efficient, following the line to pin him without killing him.

That was the man’s mistake.

He did not want Jin dead.

Jin used that mercy like a stolen knife.

Broken Star Step had begun as ugly footwork.

On the ground, it became uglier.

His left hand slapped the floor. His hips twisted beneath the blade. His right fingers rose, not as a fist, not as a strike, but as a needle made of flesh.

He touched the inside of the gray man’s wrist.

Not hard.

Precise.

The world narrowed to skin, pulse, and timing.

The Ghost Core whispered once.

`[Now.]`

Jin pressed.

The gray man’s hand opened.

The ash-dark blade fell.

His entire left side locked as if invisible chains had snapped tight from wrist to shoulder, shoulder to ribs, ribs to hip. He staggered one step. His ordinary brown eyes widened, not with pain, but with professional surprise.

Jin felt no triumph.

Only horror.

Because in that instant, he understood how easily a person could become a door with hinges in the wrong place.

The gray man dropped to one knee.

Jin tried to rise.

Pain exploded behind his left ear.

Not like a headache.

Like a bell made of bone being struck from the inside.

White light consumed the room.

`[Technique incomplete.]`

`[Host neural overload.]`

`[Motor control instability.]`

Blood poured from his nose.

His hand shook violently.

Then stopped.

Not because he controlled it.

Because he could not feel it.

Seolha tore the curtain down.

Doksu pushed himself up with one working arm.

The gray man reached for a needle with his free hand.

A gourd stopper popped somewhere near the window.

Everyone froze.

“Ah,” said Master Baek from outside. “I leave children alone for one cup of wine, and Medicine Pavilion becomes lively.”

The gray man moved.

Baek entered through the broken screen without using the door.

His clouded eyes were aimed at nothing. His wine gourd hung from his belt. In his right hand he held Jin’s cracked saber by the sheath, as though he had been carrying a bundle of firewood instead of the only weapon Jin trusted.

He tapped the floor once with the sheath.

The sound was soft.

The gray man stopped moving.

Not from obedience.

From calculation.

Baek smiled.

It was the same pleasant smile he wore when mocking disciples, drinking stolen wine, or asking Jin to catch cups that should not be catchable.

Only now, in the ruined treatment room, with blood running from Jin’s nose and Doksu half-paralyzed on the floor, the smile looked older than the sect.

“Move again,” Baek said, “and I will remove the part of you thinking about it.”

The gray man lowered his hand by a finger’s width.

Baek sighed. “You people always test whether blind means careless. It is adorable the first hundred times.”

Seolha knelt beside Jin.

“Do not sleep.”

“I wasn’t planning to,” Jin said.

The words came out wet.

Seolha pressed cloth under his nose and touched two points at the base of his skull. The room returned in pieces: lamp, ash, broken shelf, Doksu’s furious face, Baek’s bare feet on wet medicine paste, the gray man kneeling with one side locked.

The Ghost Core tried to produce text.

It failed twice.

Then:

`[Silent Meridian Needle prototype generated.]`

Jin wanted to curse.

Seolha saw his eyes move.

“What did it say?”

He hesitated.

Her expression hardened.

Jin looked away. “Prototype.”

Her hand stilled.

Doksu whispered a very bad word.

Baek’s smile disappeared.

For a moment, even the rain sounded distant.

Seolha removed the cloth from Jin’s face and replaced it with a clean one.

“You used it.”

“I did not record your pattern.”

“You used the principle.”

“He was taking him,” Doksu snapped from the floor.

Seolha did not look at him. “I know.”

The two words carried no forgiveness.

Only truth.

Jin’s throat worked.

“I touched his wrist.”

“You silenced a line.”

“I did not mean to—”

“You meant to survive.”

That stopped him.

Seolha’s eyes were not cold. Cold would have been easier. They were full of something worse: fear for him sharpened by anger at what survival was teaching him.

“You meant to survive,” she repeated softly. “That is the most dangerous excuse in the world.”

The gray man made a small movement.

Baek’s foot shifted.

The movement stopped.

Seolha stood and crossed to the attacker. She did not touch his mask. She did not search his robe first. Instead she looked at his locked arm and the angle of his shoulder.

“Temporary paralysis,” she said.

Baek tilted his head. “How temporary?”

“If Jin pressed cleanly, half an hour. If he pressed incorrectly, the arm may never fully recover. If the interruption climbed toward the heart, we would already be cleaning a corpse.”

Jin closed his eyes.

The Ghost Core remained merciless.

`[Fatal deviation avoided.]`

He wanted to ask if that was supposed to comfort him.

Seolha pulled back the gray man’s sleeve.

There was a mark on the inner wrist.

Three broken rings.

Not carved in jade this time.

Burned into skin.

Doksu saw it and went pale.

Baek hummed.

“Not a hired knife,” he said.

“No,” Seolha replied. “A retrieval hand.”

The gray man spoke through his mask.

“Interference with provincial assessment will be recorded.”

Baek laughed.

It was not loud.

That made it worse.

“Recorded by whom? Your right hand is on the floor, your left side is pretending to be a statue, and your mouth is the only part of you stupid enough to remain active.”

The gray man looked at Jin.

“Host-03 displays adaptive moral hesitation. Combat utility compromised by attachment response. Medical external influence significant.”

Seolha’s face went still.

Doksu’s fear became anger.

Jin forced himself upright despite the white sparks in his vision.

“Say that again,” he whispered.

The gray man looked at him as if he had asked a chair to repeat a poem.

“You are not being evaluated as a disciple.”

“I know.”

“You are not being evaluated as a person.”

Jin’s fingers dug into the floor.

“You are a vessel candidate.”

Baek’s hand tightened on the saber sheath.

The gray man continued, voice even. “Host-01 failed after third stair. Host-02 rejected integration before awakening. Host-03 survived containment rupture, first stair recognition, poison detection, social bait, blood verification avoidance, and retrieval contact. Report grade will improve.”

Jin heard his own heartbeat.

Host-02.

Another ghost added to the numbers.

“Who was Host-02?”

The gray man did not answer.

Baek moved one step closer.

“Answering the boy is the wise choice.”

“Host data sealed under Skyless Mandate authority.”

At the words *Skyless Mandate*, the Ghost Core flickered violently.

Jin saw a black sky without stars.

A chamber of kneeling children.

A woman screaming without sound behind glass.

Then the vision shattered.

Seolha grabbed his shoulders before he fell.

“Jin.”

He could not answer.

The Ghost Core displayed one line in red.

`[Mandate trigger recognized.]`

The gray man’s eyes sharpened.

He had seen something.

Not the vision, perhaps.

But the reaction.

He began to smile beneath the mask.

Then Baek struck him.

No flourish.

No visible draw.

The old man’s fingers tapped the gray man’s forehead, two knuckles between the brows.

The attacker collapsed.

Doksu stared. “Is he dead?”

Baek looked offended. “Of course not. Corpses are paperwork.”

Seolha knelt by the unconscious man and checked his pulse.

“Alive.”

“For now,” Baek said lightly. “We have very little time before someone wonders why their retrieval hand stopped moving.”

“We cannot keep him here,” Seolha said.

“We cannot release him.”

“We cannot kill him.”

“Such a demanding night.”

Jin tried to stand.

The room tilted.

Seolha pushed him back down with one hand.

“You cannot walk.”

“I need to know what he has.”

“You need to stop bleeding from the inside of your head.”

“Is that worse than outside?”

Doksu groaned. “Do not make jokes while your face looks like a murdered dumpling.”

Baek tossed the cracked saber to Jin.

Jin caught it badly. His fingers barely closed around the sheath.

“Keep that near you from now on,” Baek said. “Banquets are over. Polite traps have failed. The next ones will become less decorative.”

Seolha searched the gray man carefully. From his sleeve she removed three more black needles, a folded strip of waxed paper, a small vial of clear liquid, and a coin-thin metal disc carved with lines so fine they seemed to move.

The Ghost Core pulsed.

`[Relay token detected.]`

Jin’s left ear burned.

Seolha placed the disc inside a ceramic medicine cup and covered it with salt.

“Do not scan it.”

“I know.”

“Tell it.”

Jin exhaled. “Ghost Core. Do not scan the relay token.”

`[Host survival information priority—]`

“Override.”

A pause.

`[Restriction accepted.]`

Baek watched Jin with an unreadable expression.

“You are learning the shape of your leash,” he said.

Jin looked up.

Baek shrugged. “Do not glare. Better to know where it is before someone yanks.”

Seolha unfolded the waxed paper.

The writing inside was not ink.

It was a pattern of tiny punctures.

Needle code.

Her face drained of color.

Jin noticed immediately. “What?”

Seolha did not answer at first.

She held the paper near the lamp and tilted it.

The punctures became shadows.

Doksu leaned in, then gave up. “That is not writing.”

“It is,” Seolha said.

Baek’s voice lost its teasing edge. “Whose?”

“My master’s system.”

The rain hit the roof harder.

Jin felt something colder than pain settle in his stomach.

“Yun Saja?”

Seolha nodded once.

Her fingers tightened around the paper until it almost tore.

“This code was used in his private records. He created it because ink could be copied, but needle depth and spacing required the hand that knew it.”

“Can you read it?” Jin asked.

“Some.”

She held it closer.

Her eyes moved from puncture to puncture.

“One line repeats.”

“What does it say?”

Seolha’s mouth tightened.

Baek turned his head toward the broken window.

Outside, the night seemed to listen.

Seolha read softly:

“Do not let Host-03 learn the silent needle before he learns grief.”

No one spoke.

Jin looked at his hand.

The fingers that had touched the gray man’s wrist trembled now, very slightly.

Before he learns grief.

He almost laughed at the cruelty of it.

Had he not learned enough?

Years of being called Broken Pulse Dog. Years of carrying water for disciples who kicked him. Years of eating last, sleeping cold, hiding anger so it would not be beaten out of him. A mother turned into a memory. A body made into a prison.

But even as the thought rose, he knew Yun Saja had meant something else.

Not suffering.

Grief.

The difference was terrible.

Suffering made a person want to live.

Grief taught him what a life was worth before he took one.

Jin’s stomach turned.

“I did not kill him,” he said.

Seolha folded the paper with shaking precision.

“No.”

“But I could have.”

“Yes.”

The Ghost Core was silent.

That silence felt almost like guilt.

Doksu struggled to stand, one arm hanging uselessly. “Then teach him properly.”

Seolha looked at him.

Doksu’s face was pale with pain, but his voice did not shake. “I do not understand most of this. Directorate. Hosts. Ghost whatever. Fine. I am stupid. But I understand one thing. People are already trying to take him. If he learns ugly things by almost dying, he will become ugly faster. If you do not want that, teach him before they do.”

Seolha did not answer.

Baek laughed under his breath.

“Pig boy has wisdom tonight.”

“My name is Doksu.”

“Exactly.”

Jin looked at Seolha.

“I will not use it unless I must.”

Seolha’s eyes were tired.

“Everyone says that before life begins narrowing their definition of must.”

“Then make rules.”

“What?”

“Rules,” Jin said. His voice steadied as he spoke. “If the Ghost Core gives me a technique, it needs constraints. If I learn this, I need constraints. You said survival is dangerous as an excuse. Then do not let me hold the excuse alone.”

Seolha studied him.

Baek’s smile returned, but faintly.

Doksu lowered himself back to the bench. “Rule one: do not stab friends.”

“I used a finger.”

“That makes it worse.”

Despite everything, Seolha’s lips curved for half a breath.

Then she opened a fresh cloth and began writing.

“Rule one,” she said. “Silent Meridian Needle may be used only to disable, not punish.”

Jin nodded.

“Rule two. Never aim for throat, heart, spine, or major life gates unless death is already certain without it.”

He swallowed. “Yes.”

“Rule three. You will not train it on living people without consent.”

Doksu immediately lifted his working hand. “I do not consent.”

“No one asked you.”

“I am making sure the room knows.”

Seolha continued, “Rule four. If the Ghost Core recommends fatal deviation, you retreat if retreat exists.”

Jin hesitated.

Baek’s voice cut softly. “Boy.”

Jin looked at him.

The old man’s clouded eyes seemed to see too much.

“The path you are walking will offer you many reasons to become efficient. Efficiency is wonderful for cutting vegetables, solving locks, and killing men who have names. Be slower than the thing in your skull when the matter is a life.”

Jin nodded.

“Rule four,” he said. “I retreat if retreat exists.”

Seolha wrote the final line.

“Rule five. After every use, you report exactly what happened. No pride. No hiding.”

That one stung for reasons Jin did not want to name.

But he nodded again.

The Ghost Core displayed the rules in his vision without being asked.

`[Constraint set recorded: Silent Meridian Needle Protocol.]`

Then, beneath it:

`[Host ethical override registered.]`

Jin blinked.

Ethical.

The word looked strange inside a machine’s text.

Baek noticed his expression. “It said something dramatic?”

“Something annoying.”

“Then it is becoming family.”

Seolha packed the black needles away, except for one.

She laid it on the table between them.

“This stays with me,” she said. “You do not carry one. You do not practice with one. For now, your version remains touch-based and incomplete.”

“Good.”

“Do not say good as if temptation has never visited you.”

Jin looked at the unconscious gray man.

“It has.”

That answer satisfied her more than confidence would have.

Baek dragged the attacker upright by the back of his collar with no visible effort.

“What will you do with him?” Jin asked.

“Return him incorrectly.”

Doksu frowned. “What does that mean?”

“It means he will wake somewhere embarrassing with a headache, no relay token, and a report full of missing time. If his masters are smart, they will assume someone stronger than them interfered. If they are stupid, they will come faster.”

“Are they smart?” Doksu asked.

Baek considered. “Directorate people are clever. Clever is not smart. Clever builds cages. Smart remembers cages have doors.”

He slung the gray man over one shoulder.

At the window, he paused.

“Seolha.”

She looked up.

“Burn the floor cloth. Salt the threshold. Move the boy before dawn.”

“Where?”

“My shed.”

Jin stared. “Your shed is full of rotten training posts and wine jars.”

“Exactly. No one important would willingly enter.”

Doksu raised his hand weakly. “I have entered.”

Baek grinned. “My point stands.”

Then he vanished into the rain with the unconscious retrieval hand as if carrying a sack of laundry.

The broken window breathed cold air into the room.

For a few moments, no one moved.

Then Seolha turned back to Jin.

“Lie down.”

“I can help clean.”

“You can bleed into a bowl if you insist on productivity.”

Jin lay down.

Doksu tried to help, forgot one arm was numb, and nearly fell over. Seolha caught him by the collar and pushed him onto the bench.

“Both of you are impossible.”

Jin closed his eyes while she treated them.

This time, she used ordinary needles on Doksu and only two Silent Meridian Needles on Jin, placed far from any life gate. She worked slower than before, explaining each placement in simple words so the Ghost Core could hear the boundaries as much as Jin did.

“This quiets inflammation.”

“This does not stop a limb.”

“This point is forbidden for combat.”

“This point is for swelling only.”

The Ghost Core did not interrupt.

At some point, Jin realized Seolha was not only treating him.

She was teaching the machine what medicine sounded like when it was not trying to win.

That realization hurt more than the needles.

Near dawn, the rain thinned.

Doksu slept sitting upright, his numb arm wrapped and slowly regaining twitching life. Seolha sat at the table copying the puncture code onto clean paper. Jin lay with the cracked saber beside the treatment bed, one hand resting on the sheath.

He did not sleep.

Every time his eyes closed, he felt the gray man’s wrist beneath his fingers.

Not hard.

Precise.

A door closing inside another person.

The Ghost Core finally spoke.

`[Host distress detected.]`

Jin stared at the ceiling.

“No.”

`[Clarify.]`

“It is not distress.”

A pause.

`[Physiological indicators suggest fear, guilt, pain, and fatigue.]`

“Yes.”

`[Classification: distress.]`

Jin turned his head slightly toward the broken window, where pale morning gathered behind the repaired paper screen.

“It is memory.”

The Ghost Core did not answer immediately.

When it did, the text appeared slower than usual.

`[Memory stored.]`

Jin almost told it not to.

Then he thought of Yun Saja’s warning.

Do not let Host-03 learn the silent needle before he learns grief.

He let the memory remain.

Seolha stood and came to the bedside.

“You should sleep.”

“I will see his wrist.”

“Yes.”

“You are very comforting.”

“I am accurate.”

He looked at her.

Her face was exhausted, hair loosened from its pin, sleeves stained with medicine and ash. She had saved his life again. She had also looked at him like someone watching a knife decide whether it wanted to be a scalpel.

“I am sorry,” he said.

“For surviving?”

“For making you afraid of me.”

Seolha’s expression softened, and that softness was more difficult to face than anger.

“I am not afraid of you, Jin Ryu.”

He did not believe her.

Then she added, “I am afraid of who will teach you if I step away.”

That, he believed.

She placed the folded rule cloth beside his hand.

“Keep this.”

Jin touched it with numb fingers.

The five rules were written in Seolha’s clean hand.

Doksu’s contribution had been added at the bottom in smaller characters:

*Do not stab friends, even with fingers.*

Jin stared at it.

Then, despite the pain in his ribs, despite the pressure behind his ear, despite the fact that the Skyless Directorate had sent someone to retrieve him like stolen property, he laughed once.

It hurt.

But it was real.

The Ghost Core displayed one final line.

`[New technique registered under constraint protocol: Silent Meridian Needle.]`

Below it, another line formed.

`[Warning: Power acquired before wisdom stabilizes.]`

Jin closed his eyes.

Outside, the first dawn bell rang across the sect.

In Iron Moon Hall, Kang Taejun would soon learn that the banquet trap had failed.

In some hidden Directorate station, a relay token would fail to report.

Somewhere in the dark between old machines and living men, Director Yeom would receive a gap in his ledger and understand that Host-03 had not merely survived contact.

He had learned to touch back.

Jin tightened his fingers around the rule cloth.

Power had entered his hand.

So had fear.

For now, he decided, fear would hold the leash.

If he ever stopped fearing what his own hand could do, someone else would have already won.
