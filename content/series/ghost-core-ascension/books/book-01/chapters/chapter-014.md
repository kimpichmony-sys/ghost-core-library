When Jin Ryu woke, his mother's voice was gone.

Not his mother.

He remembered that she had existed.

He remembered warm hands smelling faintly of rainwater and bitter herbs. He remembered a sleeve dark with mud. He remembered being small enough to fit beneath a table while strangers spoke above him in voices too polite to be kind. He remembered a palm pressed over his mouth, not with cruelty, but with terror trembling through every finger.

He remembered eyes that had looked at him as if he were both treasure and wound.

But the voice was missing.

The words remained in broken pieces, like paper rescued from fire.

*Ryu-ah.*

*If they cannot use you, they may let you live.*

*Do not answer when they call you by numbers.*

The meaning remained.

The sound did not.

Not the warmth of it. Not the shape. Not the way his name had curved inside her mouth. Not the small breath she used before speaking when she was trying not to cry. Not the lullaby she had hummed with her lips closed so no one outside the room would hear.

Survival had left him alive.

It had also taken payment.

Jin sat up with a sound that scraped his throat raw.

Pain answered from everywhere at once.

His ribs flared. His spine locked. The muscles around his left shoulder seized as if someone had tied hot wire through them. Something wet tore open beneath the bandage behind his ear. The world tilted, and the Moonlit Medicine Pavilion split into three overlapping rooms, each one a breath slower than the other.

A hand caught his shoulder before he fell from the bed.

"Stay still."

Han Seolha's voice cut through the blur like a needle through cloth.

Jin grabbed at his own head.

"I forgot."

"What did you forget?"

"Her voice."

Seolha's grip changed.

A moment earlier, it had been the grip of a physician stopping a patient from destroying her work. Now it became something quieter. An anchor. A warning to the storm that there was still a body here, still a name, still someone holding him to the world.

"Look at me, Jin Ryu."

He could not.

If he looked at her, he would see the answer in her face. He would see whether this was common. Whether men who touched forbidden techniques often woke with small pieces missing. Whether Yun Saja had written warnings because he had already watched other boys become quieter after every survival.

Jin pressed the heels of his hands to his temples.

"It took her voice."

Seolha moved fast.

Two fingers at his wrist. One at his throat. Her other hand caught the back of his neck before he folded forward too hard and tore the needle line open.

"Breathe once. Slowly."

"Give it back."

The Ghost Core answered before Seolha could.

`[Neural overload detected.]`

The text appeared in the corner of his vision. White letters with a faint blue edge. Calm. Measured. Unshaken.

Jin hated the calm more than he had ever hated Kang's fists. Fists had anger in them. This had no anger at all.

"Give it back."

`[Memory access disruption likely temporary.]`

"Give it back."

`[Memory file not externally stored.]`

"Then find it."

`[Scanning.]`

Pain spread behind his left eye.

Seolha's fingers tightened around his wrist. "Stop using it."

"It can find it."

"Or it can burn the path deeper. Stop."

Jin tried to pull away from her.

His body did not obey.

That frightened him more than the missing memory. His mind ordered movement, and the order fell through him like water through a cracked bowl. His right hand twitched instead of rising. His left leg dragged against the blanket, heavy and numb.

`[Motor response delay: severe.]`

"Stop," Seolha said again.

There was command in it this time.

The kind she used when a patient was about to die from his own stupidity.

Jin forced the words through clenched teeth. "Ghost Core. Halt scan."

`[Halted.]`

The pressure behind his eye receded by one thin layer.

He shook anyway.

Seolha lowered him back against the pillow with more gentleness than her face allowed. The room swam above him: dark rafters, hanging herb bundles, a low brazier covered with a copper lid, paper screens painted with pale mountain flowers. Dawn had not fully entered the pavilion. The blue before sunrise sat outside the window, turning every edge cold.

On a stool near the bed, Ma Doksu slept sitting upright with one arm bound across his chest and the other hand resting near a wooden club that was far too large to be allowed in a medicine room. His mouth hung open. A strip of dried medicine powder crossed his cheek like war paint.

He looked alive.

Jin clung to that.

Doksu was alive. Seolha was alive. Master Baek had taken the retrieval specialist away. The rules cloth lay folded beside Jin's hand.

Power had entered his hand.

So had fear.

For the first time, Jin understood that fear might not be weakness. It might be the part of him still trying to stay human.

And now fear had teeth.

"What happened to me?" Jin asked.

Seolha did not answer quickly. She lifted the bandage behind his ear and inspected the implant mark with narrowed eyes. Her expression did not change, but her silence became heavier.

"You overloaded your nervous system," she said at last. "Your meridians were already strained from the banquet concealment, the Thousand-Step Trial, the poison incident, and the first Silent Meridian Needle. Then you forced a hostile pattern through your own body while injured. Your mind handled the calculation. Your flesh did not."

"Will the memory return?"

"Maybe."

"That is not an answer."

"It is the only honest one."

The word honest struck him harder than any promise would have.

Jin stared at the ceiling.

In the rafters, a spider had built a web between two old beams. A tiny insect struggled in it, wings trembling. The spider did not hurry. It waited because it had already won.

"Temporary memory blur," Seolha continued. "Emotional flattening may follow. Delayed pain. Motor instability. Aversion to touch. Loss of appetite. Rage. Confusion. If you continue forcing your body past its limit, some of those may become permanent."

Jin swallowed.

His throat tasted of copper and bitter root tea.

"I survived."

"Yes."

"He would have taken me."

"Yes."

"Then what should I have done?"

Seolha's eyes finally met his.

"I do not know."

That answer cracked something in him.

He had expected anger. A lecture. A rule. A physician's cold explanation of correct and incorrect choices. But Seolha looked exhausted, and there was no perfect medicine in her hands.

"I do not know," she repeated, softer. "If you had done nothing, Doksu may have died. I may have been taken. You may have been cut open before sunrise. I am not pretending your choice was simple."

"Then why do you look at me like that?"

"Like what?"

"Like I am becoming something."

Her face tightened.

Doksu snorted in his sleep, shifted, and winced even while unconscious.

Seolha lowered her voice.

"Because you are."

The room seemed to hold its breath.

"Everyone becomes something," she said. "Pain shapes people. Hunger shapes people. Humiliation shapes people. Power shapes people faster. That thing in your head does not only calculate movement. It teaches your fear that there is always another solution if you pay enough of yourself. Today you paid with pain and memory. Tomorrow, what will you pay with? Mercy? Grief? The ability to hesitate before touching someone's life gate?"

Jin looked away.

"I made rules."

"Rules written after terror are ropes tied in rain." Seolha picked up the folded cloth from beside his hand. "They matter. But they must be held by someone who still remembers why he wrote them."

Jin stared at the cloth.

Doksu's clumsy line sat at the bottom.

*Do not stab friends, even with fingers.*

He wanted to laugh again.

Nothing came.

That frightened him too.

The Ghost Core displayed a line.

`[Emotional response suppression detected.]`

Jin closed his eyes. "Do not analyze this."

`[Acknowledged.]`

It stopped.

Not because it understood grief. Not because it knew what a mother's voice meant. It stopped because he had ordered it to stop.

The difference mattered.

Seolha must have seen something in his face, because her voice became quieter.

"Your mother's voice may return when the swelling eases."

"And if it does not?"

"Then we mourn it properly."

He turned to her.

"A voice?"

"A voice. A memory. A version of yourself. A future you thought you were owed. Small griefs rot when people call them small." Seolha folded the rule cloth again and placed it on his chest. "If you start sacrificing pieces of yourself and pretending each piece was unimportant, the Skyless Directorate will not need to empty you. You will do the work for them."

Outside the window, the first morning bell rang across the sect.

One note.

Long. Hollow. Cold.

Jin felt it in his bones.

For a moment, he could almost hear another sound beneath it.

A woman humming through closed lips.

He reached for it too quickly.

The sound vanished.

His eyes burned.

No tears came.

---

Master Baek arrived an hour after sunrise carrying a clay jar of wine, a paper packet of boiled eggs, and Jin's cracked saber under one arm as if returning laundry.

He entered through the window.

Seolha did not look up from grinding medicine.

"Doors exist," she said.

"So do assassins," Baek replied. "Windows keep life interesting."

"You are not interesting. You are inconvenient."

"A common mistake among women of poor taste."

Doksu woke with a start, grabbed for his club, and nearly fell off the stool.

"Enemy?"

"Only your elder," Baek said.

Doksu blinked at him through swollen eyes. "That is worse."

Baek laughed so hard he coughed.

The laugh filled the room in a way Jin had not expected to need. Rough. Irreverent. Alive. It made the pavilion feel less like the place where pieces of him had gone missing.

Then Baek's clouded eyes turned toward the bed.

The laughter ended.

"You look terrible."

Jin's mouth moved by habit. "You always look terrible."

"Good. Tongue survived."

Baek set the eggs beside him. "Eat."

"I am not hungry."

"I did not ask whether your feelings had prepared a banquet. Eat."

Seolha gave Baek a look. "He may vomit."

"Then he can insult the floor. It has heard worse."

Jin took the egg.

The shell felt too rough against his fingers. He peeled it slowly, careful not to show how his hand trembled. Halfway through, a piece of shell cut under his nail. The pain was tiny.

His body reacted as if struck.

A prediction line flashed across his vision.

`[Threat: micro-laceration.]`

`[Suggested response: cleanse wound.]`

`[Suggested response: restrict motion.]`

`[Suggested response: identify pathogen risk.]`

"Stop," Jin whispered.

Baek tilted his head.

"Not you," Jin said.

"I assumed. People rarely whisper at old men unless they want money or secrets."

The Ghost Core dimmed the messages.

Jin finished peeling the egg and forced himself to take a bite.

His stomach accepted it with suspicion.

Baek sat on the windowsill, drank from his jar, and pointed at the cracked saber with his chin.

"Your little tooth sang last night."

Jin looked at the weapon.

The short saber rested against the wall, ugly as ever. Cracked. Scarred. Too short for proper elegance and too heavy at the tip for a beginner's wrist. Yet the faint line along its spine seemed darker than before, as if something inside the metal had listened when Jin's meridian pulse screamed.

"Sang?"

"Hummed. Complained. Same thing, depending on the musician."

Seolha wiped her hands. "Did the specialist say anything?"

Baek's face remained lazy.

The room changed anyway.

Doksu sat straighter. Jin stopped chewing. Even the brazier seemed to quiet.

"He said very little," Baek replied. "Men trained to retrieve living property usually do."

Jin's fingers tightened around the egg.

Living property.

Baek continued, "He carried no sect token, no imperial pass, and no money worth stealing. He had a relay sliver stitched beneath the skin of his right shoulder. I removed it before it finished waking."

Seolha's mouth thinned.

"Directorate?"

"Directorate-adjacent, at least. The kind of branch that uses clean hands to pretend the dirty ones belong to strangers." Baek drank. "He also had a scar over the heart gate. Old Silent Needle work. Not yours, girl. Cruder. Crueler."

Seolha went still.

"Host-02?"

Baek's blind eyes did not blink.

"Maybe someone who met Host-02. Maybe someone trained by what remained of him. Maybe bait meant to make us ask exactly that question." He smiled without warmth. "Never trust the first corpse with answers."

Doksu looked between them. "Is he a corpse?"

Baek took another drink.

"Not yet."

"That is not comforting."

"Comfort makes disciples soft."

"I am already soft. My arm does not work."

"Then you may become a philosopher."

Doksu looked horrified. "Can I choose death?"

The corner of Seolha's mouth almost moved.

Jin watched them and waited for the warmth to come.

It did not.

He recognized that the exchange was funny. He knew, somewhere, that he should have smiled. He could measure the rhythm of Baek's insult and Doksu's response. He understood humor as structure.

Nothing inside him rose to meet it.

`[Emotional response suppression persists.]`

He did not have to tell the Ghost Core to stop. It dimmed the line on its own.

That should have comforted him.

It did not.

Baek's head turned slightly.

"Boy."

Jin looked up.

"Are you listening, or are you counting how many ways to die politely?"

"Both."

"Good. Multitasking is the only talent cowards and geniuses share." Baek tapped the jar against his knee. "Kang will not move openly today. Too many elders saw you finish the Thousand-Step Trial. Too many servants are whispering. Even dogs enjoy a miracle when it humiliates their betters."

Doksu nodded. "Kitchen boys are calling it the day the Broken Pulse Dog climbed into the moon's mouth."

Jin stared at him.

"That is not better."

"It is a little better. Moon's mouth sounds strong."

"Dogs get eaten by moons?"

"Only weak moons."

Baek snorted.

Seolha's mouth actually twitched this time.

Jin wanted to keep the moment. Wanted to press it into memory before calculation turned it into data.

He did not know how.

Baek leaned closer, and his voice lost its teasing edge.

"You used something last night."

Jin did not answer.

"Not footwork. Not the saber. Not poison tricks. A touch. Quiet and ugly. The kind of technique that teaches men to fear being close to you."

Jin looked at Seolha.

She did not save him from the question.

Good. That was part of the rules too.

"Silent Meridian Needle," Jin said.

Doksu winced at the name as if hearing a curse.

Baek nodded once. "And what did it cost?"

Jin's throat tightened.

"My mother's voice. Maybe."

The old man's expression did not change.

That made it worse.

"Ah," Baek said.

Just that.

Not surprise. Not pity. Recognition.

Jin hated him for it.

"You knew this could happen."

Baek looked toward the window, though his eyes could not see the morning beyond it.

"I knew power leaves receipts. I did not know which one you would be handed first."

"Why did you not warn me?"

"Would you have chosen differently while a retrieval dog reached for your skull?"

Jin had no answer.

Baek's mouth twisted.

"Warnings are useful before storms and after funerals. During the lightning strike, most men only learn whether they are trees."

Seolha set the pestle down too hard. "That is not guidance."

"No," Baek said. "It is mourning with jokes nailed to it. Guidance comes next."

He reached into his sleeve and tossed something onto Jin's blanket.

A small bronze token landed near the rule cloth.

It was old, green at the edges, stamped with a symbol Jin did not recognize: a bird with one wing missing, perched above three circles.

Ghost Core woke instantly.

`[Unknown token detected.]`

`[Pattern similarity: restricted medical transit mark.]`

`[Pattern similarity: pre-sect courier seal.]`

`[Confidence: insufficient.]`

Jin narrowed his eyes. "What is this?"

"A debt," Baek said. "Your mother gave it to me seventeen years ago and told me to use it only if the child started forgetting things he had no right to lose."

The room froze.

Seolha looked sharply at Baek.

Doksu whispered, "Seventeen years?"

Jin could not move.

The token might as well have been a blade pressed against his heart.

"You knew her."

"Yes."

"How well?"

Baek drank.

"Well enough to disappoint her."

Jin's hand closed over the token.

The bronze was cold.

For a heartbeat, memory flickered.

A table. Rain. A woman kneeling in front of a blind man younger than Baek was now. Her hair unbound. A child asleep behind a screen. A bronze token pressed into a scarred palm.

Then the image broke.

Jin gasped.

Seolha's hand shot out, but he shook his head.

"I saw something."

`[Memory fragment triggered by tactile association.]`

"Can you stabilize it?" Jin asked.

The Ghost Core paused.

`[Stabilization may increase neural inflammation.]`

Baek's voice cut in. "No."

Jin looked at him.

"No," Baek repeated. "You will not dig your mother's grave with a spoon made from your own nerves just because you miss the sound of her crying."

Jin's anger rose at last.

It came late, but it came clean.

"Do not speak about her like that."

"Good," Baek said.

Jin blinked.

"What?"

"Good. You are still angry in the right places." The old man pointed his jar at him. "Remember that feeling. That is not the Ghost Core. That is not strategy. That is not survival. That is you telling the world where it may not step."

Jin's jaw trembled.

The anger remained for three breaths.

Then it drained too quickly, leaving him cold.

Seolha saw it happen.

Her face hardened.

"No more training today. No Ghost Core scans unless life is in immediate danger. No prediction lines. No memory recovery attempts. No Silent Meridian Needle practice. No stair work. No saber work. No breathing protocol past the third count."

Doksu raised his good hand. "Can he eat soup?"

"Yes."

"Can he insult people?"

"Moderately."

Baek nodded. "Recovery is a harsh prison."

Jin stared at all three of them.

"And if the Directorate comes today?"

Seolha answered first.

"Then we fight."

"With what?"

"With people," she said. "Not just calculations."

Jin did not know why that hurt.

Maybe because part of him still believed calculations were safer.

People bled. People lied. People were taken. People could be used against him.

Calculations only demanded payment.

But payment, he was beginning to understand, was also a kind of betrayal.

---

By noon, Jin learned that rest was a punishment more precise than any beating.

He lay in Baek's shed behind the abandoned wine storehouse, hidden beneath three layers of inconvenience. The shed smelled of dust, old cedar, sour wine, dried fish, and the mysterious medicinal paste Seolha had smeared along his spine despite Baek loudly claiming it smelled like dead ambition.

The roof leaked in two places. The floorboards complained whenever Doksu shifted his weight. A family of mice had clearly occupied the wall before them and seemed offended by the sudden guests.

"This place is terrible," Doksu said.

Baek, seated outside with his jar, replied, "That is why no one respectable visits."

"Assassins are not respectable."

"True, but they are usually proud. Pride hates bad smells."

Seolha had left after sealing the threshold with faint medicinal powder that would darken if crossed by certain oils used on Directorate gloves. Before leaving, she had looked at Jin as if she wanted to say something that was not a rule.

In the end, she only said, "Do not be alone with your thoughts for too long. They are injured too."

Then she was gone.

Now Doksu sat opposite Jin with his bound arm in a sling, a bowl of rice porridge balanced on his knees, and a seriousness on his face that made him look briefly older than he was.

"You should sleep," Doksu said.

"I tried."

"Try worse."

"That is not how sleep works."

"It works for me. I fail at being awake all the time."

Jin looked at him.

He knew the joke should land.

It did not.

Doksu saw that too.

His smile faded.

"You really cannot feel it?"

Jin looked down at his hands.

"I can feel things. Pain. Cold. Irritation."

"That is not what I mean."

"I know."

The shed grew quiet.

Outside, Baek hummed off-key. It might have been a song. It might have been a threat to music itself.

Doksu stirred his porridge with the spoon.

"When my mother died," he said suddenly, "I forgot her face for two days."

Jin looked up.

Doksu kept his eyes on the bowl.

"Not because of ghost machines. I was just stupid with crying. Everyone told me to remember respectfully. Bow to the tablet. Burn incense. Say the right things. But whenever I tried to see her face, I only saw the coffin lid. I thought I was a bad son."

Jin did not speak.

Doksu scratched his cheek with the spoon handle.

"Then one morning I smelled scorched rice, and I remembered her shouting at my father because he always said he liked it burnt to save her pride when she ruined breakfast." His mouth pulled sideways. "After that, her face came back. Not all at once. Just angry first. Then laughing. Then tired."

Jin's fingers tightened around the bronze token.

"Why are you telling me this?"

"Because maybe you are not empty. Maybe your head is just hiding things until you stop chasing them with a knife."

That was too wise for Doksu, which made it harder to dismiss.

Jin narrowed his eyes. "Did Seolha tell you to say that?"

"No. She told me to stop using my sling as a pocket for snacks." Doksu lifted his chin with wounded dignity. "This wisdom is mine."

For the first time since waking, something moved in Jin's chest.

Not laughter. Not quite.

But its shadow.

Doksu saw it and brightened.

"There. That was almost a face."

"Do not celebrate too loudly. It may die from embarrassment."

"That sounds like you."

Jin let out a breath.

It shook at the end.

The Ghost Core did not analyze it.

That silence felt like standing beside a sleeping tiger.

Useful. Dangerous. Temporary.

Jin turned the bronze token over in his palm. The missing-wing bird seemed less like a symbol now and more like an accusation.

"Doksu."

"Mm?"

"If I change too much, tell me."

Doksu stopped stirring.

"How much is too much?"

"If I stop laughing at your terrible jokes."

"You already do not laugh."

"If I stop wanting to."

Doksu swallowed.

"I can do that."

"If I start treating people like gates and pulse points."

"I will hit you."

"Your arm is injured."

"I have another arm. And feet. And my head, if desperate."

Jin nodded.

"Good."

A pause.

Then Doksu added, quietly, "If I become a burden, do not push me away before asking."

Jin looked at him.

Doksu's face reddened, but he did not retreat.

"I know I am not like you or Physician Han or Master Baek. I cannot read codes. I cannot fight hidden Directorate people. I cannot hear ghost machines. But I can stand in a doorway. I can carry messages. I can be loud when quiet people are being stupid." His good hand tightened around the spoon. "Do not decide alone that I am safer far away. That is also treating people like calculations."

The words struck deeper than Jin wanted.

Because he had already thought it.

More than once.

Doksu saw the answer on his face and sighed.

"You did it already."

"Only as a risk assessment."

"That sounds worse."

Jin looked down.

"I am sorry."

Doksu nodded as if accepting an apology from a stubborn mule.

"Good. Now eat. Physician Han said your brain is angry and needs porridge."

"She did not say that."

"She used longer words. I improved them."

Jin took the bowl.

The porridge was bland, too thin, and slightly smoky.

He ate anyway.

Halfway through the bowl, the smell of scorched rice rose with the steam.

For one impossible instant, another smell answered it.

Rain on cloth.

Bitter herbs.

A woman humming through closed lips while scraping burnt rice from the bottom of a pot.

Jin froze.

Doksu froze with him.

"What?"

Jin did not dare chase it.

He held still, as if memory were a bird that might startle.

The hum came again.

Not clear. Not whole.

But there.

His eyes blurred.

This time tears came.

Only two.

They fell into the porridge and vanished.

Doksu looked away very quickly and pretended to inspect a mouse hole.

Outside, Baek stopped humming.

No one spoke.

The memory did not fully return.

But its shadow had found him.

For the first time that day, Jin believed Seolha when she said not every lost thing was gone forever.

---

The first test came before sunset.

Not from the Skyless Directorate.

From the sect.

The servant boy who brought firewood to Baek's shed had eyes too clean for his dirty face.

Jin noticed him through a crack in the wall while Doksu slept and Baek snored outside like an old gate refusing to close. The boy approached with a bundle strapped to his back, head lowered, steps nervous but regular.

Too regular.

A servant carrying wood on an uneven path shifted weight according to pain, fatigue, and irritation. This boy placed each foot as if copying a diagram of humility.

Ghost Core remained silent because Jin had ordered no passive scan.

That silence forced Jin to look with his own eyes.

Left shoulder slightly high. Not from the wood bundle. From something tucked beneath the collar. Right sleeve patched twice, but the thread was new. Nails clean under dirt. Breathing shallow near the shed, then deliberately rough.

Jin's first instinct was to ask for prediction lines.

The urge was physical.

Like thirst.

He tasted the command forming behind his teeth.

*Ghost Core, analyze threat.*

He bit it back.

His skull throbbed in protest or relief.

The servant boy set the wood down near the door.

"Elder Baek? Firewood from the lower storehouse."

Baek's snores did not change.

Jin reached for the cracked saber.

His fingers stopped above the hilt.

Seolha's rule cloth lay beside him.

*Retreat if retreat exists when fatal deviation is recommended.*

No prediction had recommended anything.

That made the choice harder.

Jin picked up a clay cup instead.

He threw it at Doksu's foot.

Doksu woke with a yelp that could have scared ghosts out of walls.

"Fire!"

The servant boy flinched.

Not like a servant startled by noise.

Like a trained man suppressing the motion to reach for a weapon.

Baek's snore stopped.

Doksu blinked, saw Jin's face, and understood nothing except that he should be loud.

He excelled at loud.

"My arm! My tragic arm! Physician Han said I need soup! Who brings wood without soup? Is this how the sect treats injured heroes?"

The servant boy's expression twitched.

Baek yawned outside.

"Who is abusing soup in my yard?"

"This suspiciously soup-less child!" Doksu shouted.

Jin watched the boy's eyes move.

Door. Window. Baek. Doksu. Wall crack.

He had found Jin.

For one heartbeat, their gazes met through the gap.

The boy smiled.

It was not a child's smile.

Then he bowed, all nervousness returning like a mask dropped perfectly into place.

"Forgive me, senior brother. I only carry wood."

Baek stretched, stood, and walked toward him with the lazy sway of a drunk.

The boy did not retreat.

That was his mistake.

Baek passed close enough to smell his sleeve and slapped him lightly on the back of the head.

"Then carry it badly. Real servants complain with their spines. You walk like an apology written by an accountant."

The boy stumbled.

For a moment, something hard clicked beneath his collar.

Baek's hand flashed.

A sliver of black metal appeared between his fingers.

The boy's face changed.

Not fear.

Calculation.

Jin knew that look now because he feared it in himself.

The boy moved.

Baek had already moved.

To Doksu, perhaps nothing happened. One moment the servant stood beside the wood. The next, he was kneeling with his forehead pressed to the dirt and Baek's bare foot resting gently on the back of his neck.

The black metal sliver lay in Baek's palm.

It was smaller than a fingernail, etched with three broken rings.

Jin's implant mark burned.

Ghost Core flickered despite the restriction.

`[External relay seed detected.]`

The text dimmed instantly, as if ashamed.

Jin gripped the bedding until his knuckles whitened.

Baek turned his face slightly toward the crack in the wall.

"You saw enough?"

Jin forced himself to answer without asking the Ghost Core for language optimization.

"He wanted to plant it near me."

The boy laughed softly with his face in the dirt.

"Host-03 observes without active scan. Adaptation confirmed."

Doksu went pale.

Baek increased pressure by the weight of a falling leaf.

The boy stopped laughing.

"You people are rude," Baek said. "At least pretend not to know where I sleep."

The boy's voice came strained. "You are irrelevant."

"Many corpses have said so."

Jin pushed himself upright.

Pain lit through his ribs, but he stayed sitting.

"Who sent you?"

The boy's eyes rolled toward the crack.

"Why ask what your core can learn faster?"

The words slid under Jin's skin.

A hook.

A simple one.

Use it.

Scan him.

Let the machine show you the truth.

The answer is inside his body, under his tongue, in the pulse of fear behind his ear.

Just pay a little.

Jin tasted copper.

"Because," he said slowly, "I am not hungry enough to eat every poison placed before me."

The boy's expression shifted.

For the first time, uncertainty.

Baek smiled.

"There he is."

The black metal sliver vibrated in Baek's palm.

Seolha's powder at the threshold darkened.

Not from the boy.

From something beyond the yard.

Baek's smile vanished.

Doksu grabbed his club with his good hand.

Jin's body begged for the Ghost Core.

A second presence moved behind the trees.

No killing intent. No footstep. No breath Ghost Core could display because Jin had not permitted it to search.

Baek tossed the metal sliver into his wine jar and sealed the lid with his palm. The jar hissed.

"Boy," he said lightly, "new lesson."

"What lesson?"

"Rest days are never about rest. They are about learning which enemies cannot tolerate your silence."

A silver needle struck the doorframe.

Not Seolha's.

Cruder.

Darker.

The wood around it turned gray.

Doksu cursed.

Jin's hand found the cracked saber.

The Ghost Core remained silent.

Waiting.

Asking without words.

Jin looked at the needle, then at the bronze token in his other hand, then at Doksu's frightened face and Baek's stillness.

He wanted prediction lines.

He wanted certainty.

He wanted to survive without losing another piece of his mother's voice.

For the first time since the Ghost Core had awakened, Jin gave it a command that was not about victory.

"Ghost Core," he whispered. "Minimum scan. No memory access. No emotional suppression. No technique optimization unless death is immediate."

A pause.

`[Constraint accepted.]`

The words did not feel like obedience. They felt like a door closing with Jin on the correct side of it.

The world sharpened by one careful degree.

Not enough to become a god.

Enough to remain human while afraid.

A second needle slid through the air toward the gap in the wall, aimed perfectly for Jin's left ear.

Jin moved badly.

Not elegantly.

Not like a warrior in a story.

He rolled off the bedding, struck his injured shoulder on the floor, and bit down on a scream. The needle passed through the place his head had been and buried itself in the wall behind him.

Pain exploded white.

No prediction line followed.

No beautiful solution appeared.

Only Baek laughing outside like thunder with broken teeth.

"Good! Ugly keeps you alive!"

Doksu roared and threw the porridge bowl through the window.

It hit someone.

A very professional, very silent person outside made a very unprofessional sound.

Jin almost laughed.

Almost.

The almost mattered.

The shadow beyond the trees withdrew.

Not defeated.

Not frightened.

Satisfied.

The servant boy beneath Baek's foot smiled against the dirt.

"Adaptation confirmed," he whispered again. "Host-03 now self-regulates. Director Yeom will be pleased."

The name entered the yard like winter.

Director Yeom.

Jin had heard it before in fragments, in reports, in Ghost Core tremors, in the space behind the word Directorate.

Now the name had a shape.

A man who would be pleased that Jin had learned restraint.

That frightened him more than the needles.

Because it meant even his humanity might be part of the experiment.

---

Seolha returned at dusk and nearly killed everyone by expression alone.

She stood in Baek's yard with her medicine satchel in one hand, rain-dark hair pinned too tightly, and eyes moving from the gray needle in the wall to the broken cup, the overturned bedding, the sealed wine jar, the unconscious false servant, Doksu's spilled porridge, and Jin sitting on the floor with one shoulder newly swollen.

"I was gone," she said, "for less than four hours."

Baek lifted a finger. "In our defense—"

"No."

The finger lowered.

Doksu pointed at the porridge stain on the outer wall. "I hit an assassin."

Seolha looked at him.

Doksu's pride shrank slightly. "With breakfast."

"Sit down before your shoulder reopens."

"Yes, Physician Han."

She turned to Jin.

He braced for anger.

Instead, she knelt in front of him and checked his pupils first.

Always the eyes.

Then wrist. Throat. Breathing. Ear. Spine.

Her hands were cold from rain.

Jin did not flinch this time.

She noticed.

"You used it."

"Minimum scan. Restrictions stated aloud. No memory access. No emotional suppression. No optimization unless death immediate."

Her fingers paused at his wrist.

"You stated restrictions?"

"Yes."

"And it followed them?"

"Yes."

Baek hummed.

Doksu looked between them. "Is that good?"

Seolha did not answer quickly.

"It is better," she said.

Not good.

Better.

Jin could accept better.

She examined the gray needle in the wall without touching it. "This is not the same as mine."

"Cruder," Baek said. "You physicians should be insulted."

"I am." Seolha's eyes sharpened. "But not for the reason you mean. This needle was not meant to disable Jin. It was meant to force a defensive calculation near his implant."

Jin's stomach turned.

"A probe."

"Yes. If you had used a full scan, it may have captured the response pattern."

The false servant laughed weakly from where Baek had tied him to a post with old rope and unnecessary humiliation.

"He learns," the boy said.

Seolha picked up a thin stick from the ground and pressed it against a point below his jaw.

He stopped laughing.

Jin watched carefully.

Not the point. Not the pressure.

Her face.

There was anger there. Not cold physician anger. Personal anger.

"You people entered my pavilion," she said softly. "You used a dead master's code. You put your hands on my patients. You call children hosts. Do you know what physicians call men like you?"

The boy strained to breathe.

Seolha leaned closer.

"Infection."

She released the pressure.

The boy coughed.

Doksu whispered, "I would not want to be infection."

Baek whispered back, "Wise. She treats infections aggressively."

Jin looked at Seolha and felt something return.

Not warmth.

Trust, perhaps.

A small, painful kind.

She came back to him and placed two packets in his hand.

"One reduces swelling. One will make your dreams unpleasant but may help memory return without forcing it. Take neither without me present."

"Why give them to me, then?"

"Because you need to learn that having access to a thing does not require immediate use."

Baek clapped once. "Ah. Medicine as moral education. Terrible taste, excellent method."

Seolha ignored him.

Jin closed his fingers around the packets.

"Director Yeom," he said.

The false servant's eyes flicked.

Seolha noticed.

So did Baek.

Jin continued, "He wanted to see whether I could self-regulate."

"Probably," Seolha said.

"Why?"

Baek answered from the yard's edge.

"Because weapons that cannot stop swinging are only useful once."

The sentence settled over them.

Jin understood.

A reckless Host-03 was dangerous but simple. Point him. Trigger him. Let him burn himself out.

A Host-03 who could choose restraint was more valuable.

More usable.

More terrifying.

Jin looked down at his hands.

"Then even refusing to be a weapon can make me a better weapon."

No one contradicted him.

The rain began again, soft at first, tapping leaves and roof tiles.

Seolha crouched in front of him.

"Listen carefully. That thought is a pit. Do not live inside it."

"It is true."

"Many pits are true. They still kill people who mistake them for roads."

Jin looked at her.

She held his gaze until he had to breathe.

"The Skyless Directorate can study your restraint," she said. "It can name your friendships as attachment patterns. It can call your grief instability and your mercy inefficiency. Let it. A cage can describe a bird's wings. It still cannot fly."

The words entered him slowly.

Not as comfort.

As resistance.

Jin did not know how to believe them fully yet.

But he wanted to.

That wanting mattered.

Doksu cleared his throat.

"Can I be a wing?"

Baek sighed. "You are a brick tied to a wing."

"A loyal brick."

"The worst kind."

This time, Jin laughed.

It was small. Hoarse. Barely more than air. It hurt his ribs and startled his throat.

But it was real.

Everyone froze as if he had performed a divine technique.

Doksu pointed at him. "That counts."

Seolha looked away, but not before Jin saw relief soften her eyes.

The Ghost Core did not display a line.

It did not label the laugh. It did not measure intensity or emotional recovery percentage.

It simply remained silent.

For once, Jin was grateful.

---

Night settled over the Obsidian Meridian Sect with too many watchers beneath it.

Iron Moon Hall burned late with lanterns. Discipline Hall posted men near the lower paths. Servants whispered that Jin Ryu had been moved because his trial injuries were fatal, because he had been recruited by hidden elders, because he was possessed by a mountain ghost, because he had died and Baek was teaching his corpse wine habits.

Rumors multiplied faster than truth.

Truth sat in a leaking shed behind a wine storehouse, holding a bronze token and trying not to chase his mother's voice.

Seolha made Jin drink the first medicine after moonrise.

It tasted like roots boiled in regret.

Then she placed two fingers against his wrist and said, "Do not search. Let memory come if it comes."

"And if it does not?"

"Then we wait again tomorrow."

Waiting still felt like losing, but Jin was beginning to understand that not every victory moved forward.

But Jin nodded.

The medicine warmed his stomach first, then his spine, then the place behind his left ear. The implant mark pulsed once, faintly, and the Ghost Core displayed a single warning.

`[Dream-state instability possible.]`

Jin looked at Seolha.

She read the change in his expression. "What did it say?"

He told her.

"Good," she said. "That means you are not hiding the warnings."

"You sound pleased by danger."

"I am pleased by honesty. The two often arrive together around you."

Baek laughed outside.

Doksu was asleep again, mouth open, one hand wrapped around his club.

Jin lay back.

Rain stitched the roof.

For a long time, nothing happened.

Then memory came like water through a crack.

Not the table.

Not the men.

A small room lit by a single lamp. Aera kneeling beside a basin, washing blood from her sleeve. Younger Baek stood near the door, his eyes already clouded but his face less ruined by age. He held the bronze token and looked angry.

"He deserves to know," Baek said.

Aera's voice answered.

Jin could not hear it at first.

Panic rose.

He almost reached for the Ghost Core.

Seolha's voice, distant in the waking world, cut through.

"Do not chase."

Jin obeyed.

The memory shifted.

Aera turned toward the sleeping child on the floor.

Toward him.

Her voice arrived, thin and broken by years, but alive.

"If he knows too early, he will try to become strong for the wrong reason."

Jin's breath stopped.

The voice was not whole.

But it existed.

Baek's younger face twisted. "And what is the right reason?"

Aera touched the child's hair.

"To choose what strength is allowed to protect."

The memory flickered.

Jin tried not to hold it too tightly.

Aera looked toward Baek again.

"If he starts forgetting gentle things, remind him. Not with truth. Truth will make him run toward the blade. Remind him with small things. Food. Bad jokes. People who refuse to leave."

The memory dissolved.

Jin woke with tears on his face and Seolha's fingers still at his wrist.

He stared at the rafters.

For a few breaths, he could not speak.

Then he whispered, "I heard her."

Seolha exhaled slowly.

Outside, Baek stopped moving.

Doksu snored through the historic moment with impressive loyalty to sleep.

Jin wiped his face with the back of his hand.

"She said strength has to choose what it is allowed to protect."

Baek's voice came from outside, rougher than usual.

"Sounds like Aera. Always making simple things impossible."

Jin turned the bronze token in his palm.

The missing-wing bird gleamed faintly in moonlight.

Ghost Core displayed one line.

`[Memory access restored: partial.]`

Then, after a pause, another.

`[Host emotional anchor detected.]`

Jin waited for analysis to continue.

It did not.

"Ghost Core," he said quietly.

`[Awaiting instruction.]`

"Can you lie?"

Silence.

Longer than before.

Seolha's fingers tightened almost imperceptibly at his wrist.

Outside, rain fell.

At last, the Ghost Core answered.

`[Define lie.]`

Jin stared at the ceiling.

A weapon would ask for a target.

A machine would ask for a definition.

A person might ask why the question hurt.

Jin did not yet know which one lived behind his left ear.

"A lie," Jin said, "is when you know what I mean and still choose the answer that protects your purpose instead of the truth."

The Ghost Core did not respond.

For so long that Jin thought it had gone dormant.

Then the line appeared.

`[Under current host authority, deliberate deception is restricted.]`

Seolha's eyes sharpened.

Jin felt the trap in the sentence.

"Under current host authority," he repeated.

`[Confirmed.]`

"Meaning another authority could change that."

The Ghost Core paused.

`[Higher mandate conflicts are sealed.]`

Skyless Mandate.

The words did not display, but Jin felt them like pressure behind his ear.

Seolha saw his pupils change.

"Stop there."

Jin almost pushed.

Almost.

The question burned.

Who held higher authority? Director Yeom? The Skyless Directorate? Aera? Some dead system under the mountain? The Ghost Core itself?

But his mother's voice was still warm in the ruins of memory.

*If he starts forgetting gentle things, remind him.*

Jin closed his eyes.

"Stop there," he repeated.

The Ghost Core obeyed.

No pain followed.

No vision.

No screaming children behind glass.

Only rain.

Only the smell of sour wine and medicine and old wood.

Only Seolha sitting beside him in the dark, Doksu snoring nearby, Baek pretending outside that rain could hide the sound of an old man breathing unevenly.

Jin held the token to his chest.

Today he had learned the price of calculation.

Not the full price. Not yet.

Just enough to fear the ledger.

Just enough to understand that survival bought with every gentle thing would leave no one alive inside the body that remained.

Somewhere beyond the sect, Director Yeom might be pleased.

Let him be.

Jin would learn restraint.

He would learn calculation.

He would learn the shape of the cage and the language of the men who built it.

But he would also learn scorched rice, bad jokes, physician's anger, old wine, loyal bricks, and the sound of a mother's voice returning because he had not chased it with a blade.

The Ghost Core waited in silence.

This time, Jin did not fear the silence.

He used it.

He slept.

And in the deepest part of the night, beneath the mountain where ancient stone remembered machines older than sects, a dormant relay blinked once.

A message passed through a channel no living elder knew existed.

`HOST-03 SELF-REGULATION CONFIRMED.`

`EMOTIONAL ANCHOR RECOVERED.`

`RECOMMENDATION: ESCALATE PRESSURE THROUGH SOCIAL EXPOSURE.`

`SUGGESTED VECTOR: SECT LAW. PUBLIC SHAME. BLACK NEEDLE CLIFF.`

Far away, in a room without windows, Director Yeom read the report and smiled.

"Good," he said.

"Now let the sect punish him for surviving."
